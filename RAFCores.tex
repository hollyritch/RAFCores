\section{Introduction}

Wilhelm Ostwald's considerations in 1890 on whether chemical reactions
could be catalyzed by one of their own products \cite{Ostwald:90} marked
the provenance of autocatalysis. In its simplest form, \NV{a}utocatalysis
appears as an autocatalytic reaction where one participating species drives
its own formation, fueled by a sufficient supply of food molecules. In
subsequent decades, \emph{autocatalytic reactions} appeared, either
explicitly or implicitly, in the literature across a wide range of
reaction-network settings, including chemistry, ecology, population
dynamics, and epidemiology \cite{lotka_contribution_1910,
  lotka_analytical_1920, lotka1925elements, volterra1926variazioni,
  kermack_contributions_1991}. One of the most convincing examples is the
transmission of viral infections from one individual to another
\cite{kermack_contributions_1991}, resulting in an exponential explosion of
numbers of infected individuals, e.g., as observed during the COVID-19
pandemic \cite{saxena_propagation_2021}.

From the simple idea of an autocatalytic reaction, a growing field of
research has developed over the past decades, resulting in different
concepts of autocatalysis \cite{andersen_defining_2021}.  A first line of
studies focused on entities driving their own reproduction in complex
networks, so-called \emph{replicators}, such as Eigen's quasi-species, an
ensemble of genotypes that arrange themselves around a common species of
which they differ by a series of mutations
\cite{eigen_selforganization_1971}.  Subsequent research applied this
concept to metabolic networks to identify important metabolic
\emph{autocatalytic replicators} such as ATP
\cite{kun_computational_2008}. In a more general way, sequences of
reactions whose combined net reaction is autocatalytic have been termed
autocatalytic cycles, and shown to be prevalent in the central carbon
metabolism of \emph{Escherichia coli} \cite{barenholz_design_2017,
  smith_universality_2004}. Similar ideas have been studied subsequently
\cite{joshi_autocatalytic_2021}, as they relate closely to the hypercycle
concept originally proposed by Eigen and Schuster
\cite{eigen_principle_1977}.

\PFS{A concise definition of \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} was
  proposed in \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}, as a generalization of the
  idea of an autocatalytic cycle in accordance with the IUPAC definition of
  autocatalytic reactions \cite{gold_iupac_2025}.}  This formalization has
recently received increasing attention \PFS{since its conception in
  2020. It has lead to} the development of algorithms for enumerating
minimal and irreducible autocatalytic subsystems in fully reversible
\cite{kosc_thermodynamic_2025} CRNs and CRNs with reversible and
irreversible reactions \cite{gagrani_polyhedral_2024, golnik_birne_2025,
  golnik_enumeration_2026, golnik_using_2026}.  \emph{Stoichiometric
autocatalysis} was introduced in \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} for fully
reversible CRNs lacking explicit catalysis. Recently, the theory has been
extended to CRNs containing both reversible and irreversible as well as
explicitly catalyzed reactions \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}.

An alternative approach \PFS{to formalize system-level autocatalysis}
focused on systems of chemical reactions in which every reaction is, in
general, catalyzed \cite{lohn_evolving_1998, steel_emergence_2000}. The
original idea dates back to Kauffman's considerations
\cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986} about how life on Earth may have emerged
as a metabolism-first event rather than a genetics-first one. Kauffman
explored the idea of whether sets of polypeptide complexes that were
generated via reactions that are exclusively catalyzed by members of the
set could have led to collectively self-reproducing systems.  Under
reasonable assumptions and a sufficient supply of amino acid monomers,
surprisingly, their existence becomes almost guaranteed as long as
polypeptide chains exceed a certain length. These ideas built the
foundation for later work on \textsc{Reflexively Autocatalytic
  Food-generated sets} (RAF), which were studied extensively in many
variations \cite{steel_emergence_2000, mossel_random_2005, steel_minimal_2013,
  smith_autocatalytic_2014, hordijk_history_2019, steel20}.

Related to different approaches to thinking about the problem of how life
has emerged on a primordial Earth \cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986,
  unterberger_stoechiometric_2022}, \textsc{RAF sets} and
\emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} differ substantially in their
motivation, setting, and mathematical formalization. \PFS{Notably,
  \textsc{RAF} theory is phrased in terms of catalytic reaction systems
  (CRS) \textit{sensu} Lohn \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, while
  \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} is grounded in the tradition of
  chemical reaction network theory, tracing back to Rutherford Aris
  \cite{aris_prolegomena_1965}. A meaningful connection between these 
  two frameworks has been investigated} only very recently
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. Notably, the RAF framework, in its original
formulation, allows reactions to also occur uncatalyzed, at least
initially, which seems reasonable from a chemical perspective and
considering the time life required to develop. In a more restrictive
setting, i.e., in \textsc{Constructively Autocatalytic F-generated sets}
(CAFs), reactions can only be executed if all catalysts have been generated
beforehand. Interestingly, a RAF that is itself not entirely constrained in
such a manner is always \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}.


In this contribution, we extend this theory and set out to identify the
underlying structures carrying the autocatalytic capacity of a RAF that is
itself not a CAF. As an initial step, we overcome the menacing obstacle
that \textsc{RAFs} are embedded in catalytic reaction systems (CRS), while
\emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} was derived from chemical reaction
networks (CRNs). It turns out that the set of reactions of a CRS can be
obtained as the quotient set of the reactions in the underlying CRN. In
addition, we show that requiring minimality, with respect to the RAF
property, imposes remarkable constraints on the emerging graph
structures. Finally, we explore how these consequences impact on the
correspondence between the two notions of minimality: \textsc{irreducible
RAFs} and \emph{autocatalytic cores}. 

\section{Two different Representations of Chemical Systems}

Research on systems of chemical interactions has attracted
considerable attention, although their formalization lacks
consistency throughout the various frameworks \cite{lohn_evolving_1998,
  steel_emergence_2000, feinberg_foundations_2019}. In particular,
different authors have introduced different notions of reactions
\cite{muller_what_2022}.

\subsection{Chemical Reaction Networks (CRNs)}
A chemical reaction network (CRN) $\Gamma \coloneqq (X,\CR)$ is a tuple of two finite
sets, a set of species $X$ and a set of reactions $\CR$. A 
\emph{sub-CRN} is a tuple $(X',\CR')$ with $X'\subseteq X$ and $\CR'\subseteq
\CR$, where $r\in \CR'$ omits the reference to species $x\not\in X'$. 
For any $r \in \CR$, we can write a stoichiometric equation as an ordered association between a nonnegative combinations of reactant species to a nonnegative combinations of product species,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reaction}
  r: \sum_{x\in X}\ s^-_{xr} \cdot x \rightarrow \quad \sum_{x\in X} \ s^+_{xr}\cdot x
\end{equation}
where $s^{\pm}_{xr}$ are the reactant and product stoichiometry of the
molecule $x$ in the reaction $r$. %Reactants and products of a 
%reaction $r$ are collected in the sets:

 % \end{split}
%\end{equation}
The $|X|\times|\CR|$ \emph{stoichiometric matrix} $\SM$ encodes 
all \emph{net-stoichiometries}:
\begin{equation} 
  		\SM_{xr} \coloneqq s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr}.
\end{equation}

Clearly, stoichiometry and net-stoichiometry might not coincide, i.e.,  $s^{\pm}_{xr}\neq \mp S^{\pm}_{xr}$. One reasons is catalysis. From a chemical perspective, a \emph{catalyst} for $r$ is a species $x\in X$ that appears on both sides of the stoichiometric equation \eqref{eq:reaction}, i.e. with $s^-_{xr}s^+_{xr}\neq 0$, and that  additionally 
``increases the rate of a reaction without modifying the overall standard 
Gibbs energy change in the reaction'' \cite{gold_iupac_2025}. 

However, a \emph{solvent} $x$ in a reaction $r$ may also formally satisfy $s^-_{xr}s^+_{xr}\neq 0$ without being considered a catalyst 
\cite{marx_nature_1999}, e.g. water in Br{\o}nstedt/Lowry's acid-base theory 
\cite{eigen_proton_1964}. To be specific on the catalytic role a 
species plays, we partition stoichiometries as in \cite{golnik_bridging_2026} as
\begin{equation}
	 s^{\pm}_{xr}=\sigma^{\pm}_{xr} + k_{xr}.
\end{equation}
It is readily verified that $\SM_{xr} = s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr} = \sigma^+_{xr} - \sigma^-_{xr}$, 
thus the stoichiometric matrix is identically encoded in the coefficients $\sigma^{\pm}_{xr}$. 
The stoichiometric Eq.~\ref{eq:reaction} can then be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mod.SmEq}
  r: \sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{xr} \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr}
  \cdot x\right) \rightarrow \quad \sum_{x\in X} \ \sigma^+_{xr} \cdot x +
  \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr} \cdot x\right),
\end{equation}
We refer to species appearing in Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq} with $\sigma^{-}_{xr}>0$, 
$\sigma^{+}_{xr}>0$, and $k_{xr}>0$ as F-reactants,
F-products, and F-catalysts, respectively. The nomenclature `F-catalysts' is to 
distinguish this notion from the more loose definition of a catalyst, just as a 
species that is both reactant and product, which is often also found in literature. 
We collect F-reactants, F-products, and F-catalysts in the following sets:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \feducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^-_{xr} > 0\}; \\
    \fproducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^+_{xr} > 0\}; \\
    \cat(r)&\coloneqq \{(x, k_{xr})\ | \ x\in X,\; k_{xr}>0\}.
  \end{split}
\end{equation} 
Note that the set of F-catalysts is defined as a set of pairs $(x,k_{xr})$ 
to encode also the respective coefficient. In a CRN, distinct reactions $r_1\neq r_2$
may differ only in the F-catalysts $\kappa_F(r_1)\neq \kappa_F(r_2)$ 
and share identical F-reactants and F-products. For example, 
the hydrogenation of ethene to ethane can be mediated by platinum, 
palladium, or nickel separately \cite{crampton_ethylene_2016}. An 
example for co-catalysis, i.e., where the orchestrated cooperation of 
two catalysts is required, is the dual-catalyst system of palladium and 
copper in the Hoechst-Wacker process, where ethene is oxidized to 
acetaldehyde \cite{jira_acetaldehyde_2009}. The idea that one `same' chemical process, intended as a net-molecule transformation, can be catalyzed by different catalysts has led to the different, although much related, concept of Catalytic Reaction Systems, which we overview in the text section.

\subsection{Catalytic Reaction Systems (CRS)}\label{sec:CRS}

Catalytic Reaction Systems (CRS) \textit{sensu} 
Lohn \cite{lohn_evolving_1998} are defined as a triple 
\begin{equation}\mathbf{\Phi}\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat(\FR)),
\end{equation}
where $X$ is again a set of species, and each $\fr\in\FR$ is defined as a net-molecule transformation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:FReacEq}
	\sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{x\fr} \cdot x \quad \underset{\fr}\rightarrow \quad \sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{x\fr} \cdot x,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^\pm_{x\fr}\ge 0$ are again nonnegative stoichiometric coefficients. 
The \emph{catalyzation} set $\cat(\FR)=\{ \cat(\mathbf{r)} \}_{\fr\in\FR}$ constitutes the main difference with the CRN setting.
For a single F-reaction $\fr$, the set of catalyzations $\cat(\fr)$ is defined as
$\cat(\fr)=\{c_\fr\}$, where each catalyzation $c_\fr$ is in turn a set of pairs $(x,k_{x\mathbf{r}})$:
\begin{equation}
    c_\fr\coloneqq \{(x_1,k\TODO{^c}_{x_1\mathbf{r}}),\ldots, (x_n,k\TODO{^c}_{x_n\fr})\}.
\end{equation}
where $k_{x\fr}>0$ is a positive coefficient. We call an $\fr\in \FR$ \emph{F-reaction} to distinguish it from the CRN reactions \eqref{eq:Mod.SmEq}. Moreover, a comparison between a CRN reaction \eqref{eq:Mod.SmEq}  with a CRS F-reaction \eqref{eq:FReacEq} and the definition of catalyzations set $\cat$ suggests the following formalization of the relation between a CRN and a CRS.

{\textbf{From a CRN $\Gamma=(X,\mathcal{R})$ to a CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat(\FR))$.}  Consider the equivalence relation $\sim$ on CRN reactions defined as 
\begin{equation}
    r_1 \sim r_2 \quad \text{if $\sigma_{xr_1}^{\pm}=\sigma_{xr_2}^{\pm}$ for any $x\in X$.}
\end{equation}
To each CRN $\Gamma=(X,\mathcal{R})$ we can associate a CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}(\Gamma)\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat(\FR))$ where $\FR$ is just the quotient set of $\mathcal{R}$ over the equivalence relation $\sim$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \FR=\dfrac{\mathcal{R}}{\sim}.
\end{equation}
In particular, an F-reaction in a CRS is just an equivalence class of CRN reactions, $\fr=[r]_{\sim}$. Each catalyzation $c$ is then just the set of F-catalysts
$c(\mathbf{r})=\cup_ {r\in \fr}\{\cat(r)\}$. 


\textbf{From a CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat(\FR))$ to a CRN $\Gamma=(X,\mathcal{R})$.} Viceversa, by rolling back the previous argument, to each CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat(\FR))$ we may associate a CRN $\Gamma (\mathbf{\Phi})=(X,\mathcal{R})$, where each reaction $r\in \mathcal{R}$ corresponds to a pair $(\fr,c_\fr)$, naturally defined from \eqref{eq:FReacEq}, setting  $\cat(r)=c_\fr$, and defining $r$ via \eqref{eq:Mod.SmEq} with $\sigma^{\pm}_{xr}=\sigma^{\pm}_{x\fr}$. 


By construction, it is clear that 
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma(\mathbf{\Phi}(\Gamma))=\Gamma \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{\Phi}(\Gamma(\mathbf{\Phi))}=\mathbf{\Phi}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, since $\FR$ is a quotient set from $\mathcal{R}$, we have that:
\begin{equation}
   |\FR| \le |\mathcal{R}|,
\end{equation}
with equality achieved if, and only if, each F-reaction $\fr$, viewed as an equivalence on $\mathcal{R}$, consists only of one element, i.e.
$\fr=[r]_{\sim}=\{r\}$.

\section{Reflexively Autocatalytic Food-Generated Sets}

Catalytic Reaction Systems $(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ are the fundamental structure on which the
notion of \emph{Reflexively Autocatalytic F-generated Sets} (RAFs) is built
\cite{steel_emergence_2000, hordijk_detecting_2004}. Their definition relies
on the concept of \emph{closure}. The closure  $\closure{\RAF}{X'}$ of a species set $X' \subseteq X$ w.r.t.\ to a F-reaction set $\RAF \subseteq
\FR$ is the unique inclusion-minimal subset $Y\subseteq X$
containing $X'$ such that for any $\fr\in \RAF: \feducts(\mathbf{r})\subseteq Y
\Rightarrow \fproducts(\mathbf{r})\subseteq Y$. In symbols:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Closure}
  \closure{\RAF}{X'} \coloneqq \min_{\subseteq} \{Y  \ | \ X'\subseteq Y \subseteq X,\;
  \feducts(\mathbf{r}) \subseteq Y \Rightarrow \fproducts(\mathbf{r}) \subseteq Y \ \forall
  \mathbf{r}\in \RAF\}.
%%  %}_{\eqqcolon \mathfrak{C}_{\RAF}(X')}
\end{equation}
A Reflexively Autocatalytic F-generated set (RAF) is then defined as follows \holy{\cite{hordijk_required_2011}}:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}[RAF]\label{def:RAF}
  Let $(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ be a CRS with specified \emph{food set} $\food\subseteq X$. A 
  set $\RAF\subseteq \FR$ is called
  \vspace{6pt}
  \begin{itemize} 
  \item[i.]{\emph{Reflexively Autocatalytic} (RA) if, for each $\mathbf{r}\in \RAF$,  
  there exists at least one catalyzation $c\in \cat(\mathbf{r})$ such that $c \subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food}$;}\\[-1.0em]
    %\feducts(\RAF)\cup \fproducts(\RAF)$;}\\[-1.0em]
  \item[ii.]{\emph{\food-generated} (F) if $\feducts(\RAF)\subseteq
    \closure{\RAF}{\food}$;}\\[-1.0em]
  \item[iii.]{\emph{Reflexively Autocatalytic and $\food$-generated}
    (RAF) if both i. and ii. hold.}
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

We remark that Property~$ii.$ together with the definition of the closure, Eq.~\eqref{eq:Closure}, directly implies 
that all F-products of any F-reaction $\fr\in \RAF$ are also contained in $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$, thus $\fproducts(\fr)\subseteq
\closure{\RAF}{\food}$. It is also worthwhile noting that Property~$i.$ and ~$ii.$ are essentially independent of each other. In 
particular, Property~$ii.$ depends only on $\food$ and $\RAF$, and not on the catalyzations. This reveals an inherent freedom in 
the definition of catalyzations of a RAF, as the following Lemma underlines.



\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ArbCat}

Let $X$ be a set of species and $\FR$ be a set of $F$-reactions on $X$. Then, for any $\food$-generated set $\RAF$ there 
exist infinitely many sets of catalyzations $\{\cat(\FR)_i\}$  which make $\RAF$ a RAF in the CRS $(X,\FR,\cat (\FR)_i))$, for 
any $i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
To define such valid set of catalyzations $\cat(\FR)$, it is sufficient to arbitrarily assign to each F-reaction $\mathbf{r}$ any set 
$\{c_j(\fr)\}$ of sets of species $c_j(\fr)\subseteq X$. For $\mathbf{r}\in \RAF$, we shall further require that at least one set 
$c_{j^*}(\fr)$ consists only of species that are in the closure, i.e. ${c_{j^*}}(\fr)\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food}$. Obviously, 
any such choice of catalyzations renders $\RAF$ Reflexively Autocatalytic. It is also obvious that such arbitrary assignments 
are infinite.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A Partial Order on RAFs}

The reactions of a RAF can be partially ordered such that for each
  $x\in\feducts(\mathbf{r})$ we have $x\in\food$ or there is a reaction $\mathbf{r}'\prec \mathbf{r}$
  such that $x\in\fproducts(\mathbf{r}')$.  
   We construct one such order that is reminiscent of a breadth-first
  search.  To this end, we define sequences of nested species subsets $(X'_0, ..., X'_n)$ 
 where $X'_0=\food$ and \begin{equation}X'_i\coloneqq X'_{i-1}\cup
\bigcup_{\feducts(\mathbf r)\subseteq X'_{i-1}}
\fproducts(\mathbf r).
\end{equation} This iterative process builds 
 $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ in a finite number of steps as long as
$\vert \RAF\vert <\infty$. A formal account of this procedure is
  given in the Supplemental Material.

These ordered sequences define a \emph{generation reaction-index} 
$\gamma(\mathbf{r})$ where an F-reaction $\mathbf{r}$ happens for the first time, i.e. 
\begin{equation}\label{def:genreactionindex}
    \gamma(\mathbf{r})\coloneqq \operatorname{min} \{i \; | \; \feducts(\mathbf{r})\subseteq X'_i\} + 1,
\end{equation} 
where the index is shifted by one for normalization, so that the \emph{first} generation corresponds to 
index $\gamma(\mathbf{r})=1$. Similarly, the sequences define a \emph{generation species-index} $g(x)$
 where a non-food species $x\not\in \food$ is produced for the first time, i.e.
\begin{equation}
   g(x)= \operatorname{min} \{i \; | \; x\in X'_i\}.
\end{equation}
Clearly, the total order $\le$ on the natural numbers induces a preorder $\preccurlyeq$ on $X\setminus \food$ and a preorder $\preceq$ on $\mathbf{R}$ as:
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
  x_1\preccurlyeq x_2 \quad&\text{if}\quad \sgen(x_1) \leq \sgen(x_2)\\
  \fr_1\preceq \fr_2 \quad &\text{if}\quad \rgen(\fr_1) \leq \rgen(\fr_2)
\end{cases},
\end{equation}
which we refer to as \emph{generation orders} on species and F-reactions, respectively.

We conclude this section with a further definition.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}\label{def:CAF}
  A \emph{constructively autocatalytic and food-generated} set (CAF) is a
  RAF such that for each F-reaction $\fr$ all $x\in \feducts(\fr)$ satisfy
  $\sgen(x)<\rgen(\fr)$ and there is at least one catalyzation $c\in \cat(\mathbf{r})$ 
  such that all F-catalysts $x\in c$ satisfy $\sgen(x)< \rgen(\fr)$.
\end{definition}


\subsection{Irreducible RAFs}\label{sec:IrrRAF}

For a CRS $(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ with food-set $\food\subseteq X$, 
the minimality of the RAF structure $\RAF$ with respect to its F-reactions, 
has been investigated in \cite{steel_minimal_2013}. To this aim, the 
notion of \emph{irreducible RAFs} has been introduced.

\vspace{3mm}
\begin{definition}[Irreducible RAF]\label{def:irrerRAF}
  A RAF $\RAF$ is irreducible if no subset $\RAF'\subset \RAF$ of 
  F-reactions determines a RAF. 
\end{definition}

\emph{Example.} Consider a species set $X=\{x_0,...,x_{n+1}\}$ with food 
set $\food=\{x_0\}$ and a sequence of $n+1$ F-reactions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ExReacSeq}
 \fr_i:\qquad x_i\longrightarrow x_{i+1},
\end{equation}
such that $c=\{x_{i+1}\} \in \kappa_F(\fr_i)$, i.e. for each F-reaction $\fr_i$ 
there exists a catalyzation which consists of its F-product.
Clearly, any set $\RAF_k$ of the form $\RAF_k\coloneqq \{\fr_0,\ldots, \fr_k\}$, 
with $k\leq n$ satisfies Def.~\ref{def:RAF} and it is thus a RAF. In particular, 
$\RAF_0=\{\fr_0\}$ is an irreducible RAF.


The minimality condition imposed on the set of F-reactions via Def.~\ref{def:irrerRAF} 
directly yields the following structural lemma.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:OneFReacPerRAF}
  Let $(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ be a CRS with food-set $\food\subseteq X$. An irreducible RAF $\RAF$ never contains two F-reactions 
  $\fr_1$ and $\fr_2$ such that its net-stoichiometry coincides: 
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:netreaction}
      \sigma^+_{x\fr_1}-\sigma^-_{x\fr_1}=\sigma^+_{x\fr_2}-\sigma^-_{x\fr_2} \quad \quad \text{for all $x\in X$}.
  \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Indirectly assume there exist two F-reactions $\fr_1,\fr_2\in \RAF$ such that \eqref{eq:netreaction} 
holds and assume, without loss of generality, that $\fr_1\preceq \fr_2$. Consider the set 
$\RAF'\coloneqq \RAF \setminus \fr_2$. Since \eqref{eq:netreaction} holds, it follows that 
$\closure{\RAF}{\food} = \closure{\RAF'}{\food}$, since $\fr_1$ and $\fr_2$ have the same 
F-reactants and F-products. Therefore, $\RAF'$ is (i) Reflexively Autocatalytic, since $\RAF$ is, 
and (ii) is $\food$-generated; in particular, $\RAF'$ is a RAF which contradicts $\RAF$ being irreducible.
\end{proof}

Since a RAF is defined in Def.~\ref{def:RAF} as a subset of F-reactions, Def.~\ref{def:irrerRAF} imposes 
a minimality condition only on the set of F-reactions. However, motivated by the fact that in the CRN setting 
different catalyzations correspond to different reactions, we also consider irreducible RAFs $\RAF$ with a 
minimal number of catalyzations while conserving irreducibility. 

For any $\food$-generated set $\RAF$, Lemma~\ref{lem:ArbCat} shows indeed that infinitely many 
catalyzations exist that render $\RAF$ a RAF. Complementarily, here we address which catalyzation 
subsets $\cat'(\FR)\subset \cat(\FR)$ preserve the fact that an $\food$-generated set $\RAF$, which is 
an irreducible RAF in the CRS $(X,\FR, \cat(\FR))$, is still an irreducible RAF in the CRS $(X,\FR, \cat'(\FR))$. 
The following Lemma shows that $\cat'(\FR)$ can always be chosen such that $|\cat'(\fr)|=1$  for all 
F-reactions $\fr\in \RAF$.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:mono}
	Let $\mathbf{\Phi}\coloneqq (X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ be a CRS with specified \emph{food set} $\food\subseteq X$ 
	and let $\RAF$ be an irreducible RAF. Then $\RAF$ is also an irreducible RAF in any CRS $(X, \FR,\cat'(\FR))$ 
	where the catalyzations $\cat'(\FR)\subseteq\cat(\FR)$ satisfy:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:casesmono}\cat'(\fr)=\begin{cases}
	   \{c\}\text{ with $c\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food}$}\quad &\text{if $\fr\in \RAF$,}\\ 
\cat(\fr)&\text{otherwise.}
	\end{cases}, 
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
     The first case in \eqref{eq:casesmono} guarantees that $\RAF$ is Reflexively Autocatalytic in $(X, \FR,\cat'(\FR))$.
     Once a set $\RAF$ is Reflexively Autocatalytic, being an irreducible RAF only depends on the $\food$-generated 
     property, which - in turn - is independent from the catalyzations. Therefore, $\RAF$ is an irreducible RAF in $(X, \FR,\cat'(\FR))$
\end{proof}


\vspace{6pt}
Lemma~\ref{lem:mono} suggests introducing the following notion.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}
Let $(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ be a CRS with specified \emph{food set} $\food\subseteq X$. We call $\RAF$ a \emph{monocatalyzed 
RAF} if it is a RAF and it holds $|\cat(\fr)|=1$ for all F-reactions $\fr \in \RAF$.
\end{definition}

We stress that a monocatalyzed RAF may include, nevertheless, F-reactions $\fr$, whose unique catalyzation $c$ is a set 
of multiple species, i.e. $c=\{x_1,..., x_n\}$. 
Lemma \ref{lem:mono} implies that, for a CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}=(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ 
with irreducible RAF $\RAF\subseteq\FR$, there exists exponentially many sub-CRS $\Phi'_i$ for which $\RAF$ is a 
monocatalyzed irreducible RAF. This is formalized in the following lemma.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ExpoSubCRS}
	Let $\mathbf{\Phi}=(X,\FR,\cat(\FR))$ be a CRS with specified \emph{food set} $\food\subseteq X$ 
	and $\RAF$ be an irreducible RAF. Then there exist at least $N$ distinct sub-CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}'_1,\ldots,  \mathbf{\Phi}'_N$ with 
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:ExpoNum}
		N = \prod_{\fr\in \RAF}\ \vert \{ c \in\cat(\fr) \; \mid\; c \subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food} \vert, 
	\end{equation}
	such that for $i=1,\ldots,N$ it holds that $\RAF$ is a monocatalyzed irreducible RAF in $\mathbf{\Phi}_i$.
\end{lemma} 
\begin{proof}
	 Lemma \ref{lem:mono} implies that there are at least $N=\prod_{\fr\in \RAF}\ \vert \cat(\fr) \vert$  distinct combinations of possible 
	monocatalyzations for all F-reactions in $\RAF$. Any such monocatalyzation  $\cat'(\RAF)$ provides a sub-CRS 
	$\mathbf{\Phi}'=(X,\FR,\cat'(\FR))$, where $\cat'(\RAF)\subseteq\cat(\FR)$ is arbitrarily extended to $\FR$, for which $\RAF$ is a 
	monocatalyzed irreducible RAF.
\end{proof}

Finally, we underline that irreducibility is a property that is of interest only for RAFs that are not CAFs. We have the following proposition indeed:
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{proposition}\label{prop:Caftrivial}
Any irreducible RAF $\RAF$ that is itself a CAF is a single F-reaction $\RAF=\{\fr\}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Let $\RAF$ be an irreducible RAF that is a CAF and consider any F-reaction $\fr\in \RAF$, which satisfies $\gamma(\fr)=1$. By Def.~\ref{def:CAF} 
and of generation reaction-index $\gamma$ (Eq.~\eqref{def:genreactionindex}), it follows that all F-reactants $\rho(\fr)$ and at least one catalyzation 
$c\in \cat(\fr)$ are in the food set $\food$. Therefore $\fr$ is a CAF (and RAF) itself, and $\RAF=\{\fr\}$ by irreducibility of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}

Due to Prop.~\ref{prop:Caftrivial}, throughout the paper we exclude the trivial CAF case whenever making statements about irreducible RAFs. 
To keep the exposition concise, we assume this exclusion throughout and do not repeat it explicitly in each statement.


\subsection{The K\H{o}nig Graph of Monocatalyzed Irreducible RAFs}

\holy{
Minimal \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} CRN subnetworks, formally 
introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:AutoCatIrrRAF} and termed \emph{autocatalytic cores} 
\cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}, encompass a bipartite K\H{o}nig 
graph representation of the corresponding CRN that includes the 
property of being strong blocks - strongly connected subgraphs whose underlying 
undirected graph is 2-connected \cite{golnik_enumeration_2026, golnik_using_2026, 
golnik_autocatalytic_2026}. Taking into account that any RAF that is not itself a CAF 
is stoichiometrically autocatalytic \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, it will be of interest to 
investigate the structure of the bipartite K\"onig graph representation of irreducible 
RAFs.

To this end, we will be concerned with CRS $\mathbf{\Phi}'$, with food-set $\food$,
which themselves contain only the F-reactions of a monocatalyzed irreducible $\RAF$, i.e.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:RAF-CRS}
  \mathbf{\Phi}' \coloneqq (\closure{\RAF}{\food}, \RAF, \cat'(\RAF))
\end{equation}

where $|\cat'(\fr)|=1$ for each F-reaction $\fr\in \RAF$ and its corresponding CRN 
$\Gamma'\coloneqq \Gamma(\Phi')\coloneqq (X, \CR')$. In this "monocatalytic" case, 
each F-reaction is associated with a unique catalyzation, yielding a canonical identification 
of $\Gamma'$ with $\mathbf{\Phi}'$. Accordingly, each equivalence class reduces to a 
single element, so that CRN reaction and F-reactions are in bijective correspondence. 
We can therefore equivalently study the properties of $\mathbf{\Phi}'$ by studying the 
properties of $\Gamma'$, as $\mathbf{\Phi}'$ and $\Gamma'$ encode the same 
network structure.

We therefore consider only the K\H{o}nig graph of the CRN $\Gamma'$, which is the graph 
$\king(\Gamma') \coloneqq (X \cup \CR', E)$, whose edge set $E\coloneqq E_1\cup E_2$, 
induced by the two disjoint vertex sets, is composed of two distinct types given as
\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases} (x,r) \ | \ x\in X, r\in \CR: s^-_{xr}>0 \\
 			(r, x) \ | \ x\in X, r\in \FR: s^+_{xr}>0. \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Note that the K\H{o}nig graph emerged under various names in the context of CRN theory, as the 
species-reaction (SR) graph \cite{craciun_multiple_2006}, metabolite-reaction (MR) graph 
\cite{vassena_good_2020}, and also as Petri nets \cite{angeli_petri_2007}.}


\holy{
\paragraph{Strong connectivity}

We recall that a directed graph $(V,E)$ is \emph{strongly connected} if, for any pair of 
vertices $v_1,v_2\in V$, there exists a directed path from $v_1$ to $v_2$. In terms of 
$\king(\RAF)$, we cannot hope to have strong-connectedness unless we remove food 
and waste species, of which the latter are derived from the set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Waste}
  \mathbf{W}\coloneqq \{x\in \closure{\RAF}{\food} \ | \ \nexists r \in \CR' \text{ s.t. } s^-_{xr}>0\}.
\end{equation}
For any $r\in \CR'$, by definition, for a waste species $x_W$, there is no directed edge 
$(x_W,r)$, and for a food species $x_F$, there may be no directed edge $(r,x_F)$. We 
can, however, consider the subgraph of $\king(\RAF)$ that is void of food and waste 
molecules:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PrinceGraph}
    \lord(\RAF) \coloneqq
    \king[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus (\food\cup W) \cup \CR'],
  \end{equation}
where the general notation $\king[X'\cup \CR']$ refers to the subgraph of 
$\king$ \emph{induced} by the vertex sets $(X',\CR')$. We then have 
the following lemma.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:PSC}
  Let $\RAF$ be an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAF. Then $\lord(\RAF)$ 
  is strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Again, assume indirectly that this is not the case. Then there are two non-empty, 
  strongly connected components $\lord_1,\lord_2$.  W.l.o.g., we assume 
  that there is no path from any $u\in \lord_2$ to any $v\in \lord_1$. Then 
  all paths from vertices of $\lord_2$ to vertices of $\lord_1$ in $\king(\RAF)$ 
  pass through $\food$. This, however, implies that all F-catalysts and F-reactants 
  for all F-reactions in $\lord_1$ are contained in $\food \cup \lord_1$. Then 
  $R(\lord_1)/{\sim}$ is a non-empty RAF which contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}

\holy{
\paragraph{Cut-vertices}

Strongly connected graphs are not necessarily strong blocks, as they can still contain cut-vertices. 
A strong block $B$ of digraph $G=(V,E)$ is termed maximal if there is no vertex $v\in V$ such that 
$G[B\cup \{v\}]$ is a strong block. The Example depicted in Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex2} illustrates an 
irreducible RAF $\RAF$, with $\lord(\RAF)$ containing the cut-vertex $r_2$, whose removal would 
disconnect the graph. $\lord(\RAF)$ of an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAF $\RAF$ is therefore either 
a strong block itself or it contains a cut-vertex, but not both. In Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex2} both maximal 
strong blocks $\lord[\{x_1,x_2\} \cup \{r_1,r_2\}]$ and $\lord[\{x_3,x_4\}\cup \{r_2,r_3\}]$ share the 
common cut F-reaction vertex $r_2$. 

The minimality condition on the set of F-reactions suggests that only CRN reactions can be cut-vertices. 
If a species were a cut-vertex, separating $\lord(\RAF)$ into two strongly connected components, then 
the set of F-reactions of this component would be food-generated, as all F-reactants and F-catalysts for 
all F-reactions would be satisfied by species from inside. That this result holds in general is summarized 
in the following Lemma.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:CutVertexReac}
  Let $\RAF$ be an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAF. If $\lord(\RAF)$ is not
  a strong block, then every cut-vertex is a CRN reaction. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume there is a cut-vertex $w\in X(\lord)$. Then there are at least two
  	strongly connected components $\lord_1$ and $\lord_2$. Then for any two
  	vertices $u\in V(\lord_1)$ and $v\in V(\lord_2)$ we have $w\in P_{uv}$
  	and $w\in P_{vu}$ for arbitrary paths connecting $u$ and $v$ in $\lord(\RAF)$. 
	Therefore, except $w$, there is no $x_1\in X(\lord_1)$ such that $x_1$ 
	is an F-reactant, F-product, or F-catalyst for any reaction $r_2\in \CR(\lord_2)$, 
	and there is no $x_2\in X(\lord_2)$ such that $x_2$ is an F-reactant,
  	F-product, or F-catalyst for any reaction $r_1\in \CR(\lord_1)$.
  	Then either $\closure{\CR(\lord_1)/\sim}{\food}\neq \emptyset$ or (not mutually exclusive) 
	$\closure{\CR(\lord_2)/\sim}{\food}\neq \emptyset$, implying that
  	$\CR(\lord_1)/\sim$ or $\CR(\lord_2)/\sim$ is a RAF, respectively. Either case 
	contradicts the irreducibility of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}

\holy{
\paragraph{Strong Blocks}

The existence of cut-vertices, which can only be reaction vertices, allows for
the decomposition of $\lord(\RAF)$ into maximal strong blocks $\lord_1,\ldots, 
\lord_n$. Throughout, we will refer to maximal strong blocks simply as blocks. 

Food-connected blocks of $\lord(\RAF)$ contain CRN reaction vertices whose 
F-reactants are all elements of $\food$ and thereby no vertices of the block itself. 
We call such reactions \emph{liberal} and collect them for each block $\lord'\subseteq 
\lord$ in the sets
\begin{equation}
    \CR_\ell(\lord')\coloneqq \{r\in \CR(\lord') \ | \ \nexists x\in X(\lord'): \sigma^-_{xr}>0\},
\end{equation}
which is, indeed, a non-empty set for each block. To simplify notation, we might 
write $\fr$ instead of $[r]_{\sim}$ for the equivalence class (F-reaction) of a CRN 
reaction $r$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:cutVertexPureCat}
  Every strong block $\lord'$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ contains a liberal reaction.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Let $\lord'$ be a maximal strong block of $\lord(\RAF)$. If $\lord'$ contains 
  a CRN reaction $r: \rgen(\fr)=1$, all F-reactants of $r$ are elements of $\food$. 
  Otherwise, $\lord'$ contains a cut-vertex $r_1\in \CR(\lord')$. If $r_1$ is the only 
  cut-vertex in $\lord'$, then $\rgen(\fr_1)< \rgen(\fr)$, for all $r\in \CR(\lord') 
  \setminus \{r_1\}$. If some $x\in X(\lord')$ is an F-reactant for $r_1$, then
  $x$ is an F-product of another F-reaction $r_2\in \CR(\lord')\Rightarrow 
  \rgen(\fr_2)< \rgen(\fr_1)$, a contradiction. If $\lord'$ has cut-vertices 
  $\CR_\ell(\lord')\coloneqq \{r_{\ell_1}, \ldots, r_{\ell_k}\}$, we may choose 
  $r_{\ell_j}\in \CR_\ell(\lord')$ such that $\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})\leq \rgen(\fr_{\ell_i})$ 
  for all $r_{\ell_i}\in\CR_\ell(\lord')$. Assume now that $r_{\ell_j}$ has an F-reactant 
  $x_j\in X(\lord')$. Then $x_j$ is an F-product of some reaction in $r_1$, and
  $\rgen(\fr_1)<\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})$. Then $r_1$ cannot be a liberal reaction since
  otherwise $\rgen(\fr_1)<\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})\leq \rgen(\fr_1)$. Thus, $r_1$ 
  has an F-reactant $x_1 \in X(\lord')$, which is an F-product of some
  reaction $r_2\in \FR(\lord')$. If $r_2=r_{\ell_j}$ then
  $\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})< \rgen(\fr_1)<\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})$, a contradiction. 
  Otherwise, $\rgen(\fr_2)<\rgen(\fr_1)<\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})$ and by
  iterative application of the upper argument, we obtain with the finiteness of
  $\lord'$:
  \begin{equation}
    \rgen(\fr_k)<\rgen(\fr_{k-1}) <\ldots < \rgen(\fr_l)
  \end{equation}
  with $\fr_k=\fr_l$, which yields a contradiction and thereby
  $\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})\leq \rgen(\fr)$ for all $r\in \CR_\ell(\lord')\setminus
  \{r_{\ell_j}\}$ and $\rgen(\fr_{\ell_j})< \rgen(\fr)$ for all $r\in
  \CR(\lord')\setminus \CR_\ell(\lord')$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
}
\holy{
The proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} has two important
consequences. First, only cut-vertices can be liberal reactions, 
although not all cut-vertices necessarily are. Second, F-reactions of 
liberal reactions take an important position within the pre-order of blocks 
of an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAFs, as there is always one -- potentially 
multiple -- of which the corresponding F-reaction has the lowest generation 
of all reactions inside the block. In addition, strong connectedness of $\lord'$ 
ensures that each liberal reaction is connected to its single F-catalyst inside 
the block. Intuitively, but not trivially following, they also have at least one 
F-product inside the block, which can 
be the F-catalyst.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:LibReacFProd}
	For every liberal reaction $\fr_\ell \in \CR_\ell(\lord')$ there is at least on species 
	$x\in X(\lord')$ such that $\sigma^+_{xr_\ell}>0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume indirectly that this is not the case. Then $\lord'$ is composed of the single 
	reaction $r_\ell$ and its F-catalyst $x_\ell$. If $x_\ell$ is not an F-product of $r_\ell$, 
	then there is another reaction $r'$ of a block $\lord''\neq\lord'$ such that 
	$\sigma^+_{x_\ell r'}>0$. Then $x_\ell$ is a cut-vertex, which contradicts 
	Lemma~\ref{lem:CutVertexReac}.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
}

\subsection{Stoichiometric Autocatalysis of Irreducible RAFs}\label{sec:AutoCatIrrRAF}

\holy{
Liberal reactions play a crucial role in $\lord(\RAF)$. In particular, they can be 
considered as inflow reactions for the corresponding block $\lord'$, as they 
depend only on its single F-catalyst $x_\ell$ and F-reactants located outside 
the block, not inside. A liberal reaction $r_\ell$ thereby corresponds to a 
non-negative column in the associated stoichiometric matrix 
$\SM[X(\lord'),\CR(\lord')]$ with at least one positive entry that reflects an
F-product of $r_\ell$, i.e., $y \in X(\lord): \sigma^+_{yr_\ell}>0$. By definition 
of $r_\ell$ $y$ cannot be an F-reactant, thus $S_{yr_\ell}=\sigma^+_{yr_\ell}>0$.
The associated stoichiometric matrix then reads
\begin{equation}
  \SM[X(\lord'),\CR(\lord')] = 
  \begin{array}{c c}
    & 	\begin{array}{c c c} 			
	  & 	r^* 		& 
	\end{array} \\
    \begin{array}{c} 
      \ \\ 
      x_\ell \\
      \ \\ [0.5em]
      y \\
      \ \\
      \ \\		
    \end{array}
    & \begin{pmatrix}						
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 			& \ddots \\
	\ldots 	& 	0 				& \ldots \\
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 			& \ddots \\
	\ldots 	& 	\sigma^+_{yr_\ell} 	& \ldots \\
	\ldots 	& 	0 				& \ldots \\
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 			& \ddots

  \end{array}.
\end{equation}
\vspace{6pt}

The columns induced by liberal reactions are properties common 
not only to the stoichiometric matrices associated with each block, 
but also to the stoichiometric matrix representing all reactions and 
all non-food molecules of the closure, i.e., 
$\SM[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus \food, \CR']$. This allows us 
to construct a strictly positive vector $v$ of reaction rates which 
satisfies $\SM[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus \food, \CR]v>0$. 
A matrix $A$ satisfying $Aw>0$ for $w>0$ is called \emph{semi-positive}.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}[Proof: SI]\label{thm:NonFoodSemipositiv}
  For an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAF $\RAF$, the matrix 
  $\SM[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus \food, \CR']$ is semi-positive.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

Every species of a block $\lord'$ is produced and consumed only by

\end{equation}
Thus, each block has a semipositive stoichiometric submatrix. In addition, since strong 
blocks are strongly connected, each reaction vertex has at least one incoming edge, 
i.e., from an F-reactant or an F-catalyst, and at least one outgoing edge, i.e., to an 
F-catalyst or an F-product. Each reaction in the sub-network $(X(\lord'), \CR(\lord')$ 
therefore has at least one reactant and one product. We say that such a sub-network 
is \emph{well-formed}. 

In chemical reaction networks (CRN), a matrix-centered definition of 
\emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} was introduced by Blokhuis et al. 
\cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} in reversible setting and without 
catalysis. This framework was recently adapted to the irreversible setting with 
catalysts of the present paper \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}. The central definition 
is as follows.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}\label{def:autSubRN}
  A CRN $(X,R)$ is \emph{\NV{stoichiometric} autocatalytic} if it contains a sub-CRN $(X',R')$
  such that
\begin{enumerate}
 \item[(i)]  $(X',R')$ is  well-formed;
 \item[(ii)]  \NV{the associated stoichiometric matrix} $\SM'\coloneqq \SM[X',R']$ is semi-positive, i.e., there exists $v\in \mathbb{R}_{>0}^{|R'|}: \SM'v>0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

A sub-CRN $(X',R')$ that satisfies \textit{(i.)} and \textit{(ii.)}, while
no proper subnetwork $(X'',R'')$ with $X''\subseteq X', R''\subseteq R'$
does, is called an \emph{autocatalytic core}. 

From the obtained results above and the Def.~\ref{def:autSubRN} it follows that
for an irreducible, monocatalyzed RAF $\RAF$ the subnetworks corresponding 
to $(\closure{\RAF}{\food\setminus \food\cup W, \CR})$ and to each 
maximal strong block $\lord'$ of $\lord(\RAF)$, i.e., $(X(\lord'), \CR(\lord'))$, are 
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}. In particular, each block contains 
an \emph{autocatalytic core}. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}\label{Thm:StrBlockCore}
  Every strong block $\lord'$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ of an irreducible, monocatalyzed
  RAF $\RAF$ contains an autocatalytic core.
\end{corollary}

Since strong blocks are strongly connected, each vertex, and thus, also
each pure reaction, is located on an elementary circuit. Together with the
partial order, this observation can be engaged to construct an elementary
circuit $C$ for every strong block that gives rise to a stoichiometric
submatrix $\tilde{\SM}(C)\coloneqq \SM[X(C), R(C)]$ in which each column
and row has at most one negative entry, and one column is nonnegative with
at least one positive entry. The columns of $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ can then be
reordered to obtain a matrix $\SM(C)$ with nonpositive diagonal and
nonnegative off-diagonal entries.  A matrix with nonnegative off-diagonal
entries is called a \emph{Metzler} matrix. It is not hard to see that, in
case $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ is semi-positive, $\SM(C)$ is semi-positive. In
addition, the subnetwork of an elementary circuit $(X(C),R(C))$ is
well-formed.

\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:MetzCircuit}

  elementary circuit $C$ with one pure reaction vertex, and there is a
  reordering of the columns of $ \SM[X(C), R(C)]$ that yields a Metzler
  matrix.
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit} a Metzler circuit. Unfortunately, such a
Metzler circuit can, as shown in the proof, still contain a species-to-reaction

always \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}.

  If $C$ is a Metzler circuit containing a \emph{pure reaction}, then
  $(X(C), R(C))$ is \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}.
\end{lemma}

\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}, we say that $C$ is

which can give rise to strictly smaller circuits inside the constructed
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} Metzler circuit. The example in
Sec.~\ref{sec:NonUnStoich} captures such a scenario. As the K\H{o}nig graph
is a simple digraph, stoichiometries are naturally not encoded besides
being specified as edge labels. We can, therefore, not hope to find a
general construction method that identifies autocatalytic cores
immediately.

\section{Discussion}

\PFS{\textsc{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)} and
  \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} are grounded in different
  formalizations of chemical reaction networks that lead to inequivalent
  concepts of reactions, distinguished by the manner in which catalysis is
  handled. While in the CRNs underlying the theory of \emph{stoichiometric
  autocatalysis} catalysts are not formally distinguished from other
  reactants and products, the CRS framework focuses on net F-reactions
  considering catalysts as external to the F-reactions. As a consequence,
  F-reactions are equivalence classes of (CRN) reactions with respect to
  the equivalence relation $\sim$ describing reactions that share the same
  non-catalyst reactants (F-reactants), the same non-catalyst products
  (F-products) and the same net stoichiometry, see
  Sect.~\ref{ssect:CRNCRS}.  This observation also implies that graph
  representations of reaction systems in CRS are quotient graphs of
  corresponding constructions for CRN. Most importantly, the directed
  bipartite K\H{o}nig representation of a CRS $\queen(\Phi)$ is the quotient
  graph of the K\H{o}nig graph associated with its underlying CRN,
  $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$. The equation
  \begin{equation*}
    \queen(\Phi) = \king(\Gamma(\Phi))/\sim
  \end{equation*}
  establishing the formal mathematical correspondence between CRSs and CRNs
  that we then leveraged to analyze autocatalytic substructures.}
  
%% \textsc{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)}
%% \cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986, steel_emergence_2000} and
%% \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}
%% represent two different interpretations of the concept of an explicit
%% autocatalytic reaction, which was first discussed in 1890 by Wilhelm
%% Ostwald \cite{Ostwald:90}. While RAFs are defined in catalytic reaction
%% systems (CRS), in which each reaction is explicitly catalyzed,
%% \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} developed, in accordance with the IUPAC
%% definition of an autocatalytic reaction \cite{gold_iupac_2025}, from
%% chemical reaction networks (CRN), where reactions can but don't have to be
%% catalyzed.


Although inherently distinct, it was recently demonstrated that
every \textsc{RAF} that is not a \textsc{CAF} is nevertheless
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. Here,
we continued examining this relationship in greater detail, focusing in
particular on the interplay between the minimal structures arising in both
frameworks.

%% The principal obstacle to a direct comparison between the two frameworks
%% lies in their divergent interpretations of chemical reactions. In CRNs,
%% reactions are defined via their associated stoichiometric equations
%% \cite{muller_what_2022}. Therefore, each equation reflects a different
%% chemical reaction, which we call a \emph{reaction}. In particular,
%% reactions that share the same net stoichiometries but engage a different
%% set of catalysts or involve species that appear on both sides of the
%% reaction with identical stoichiometry, albeit being considered catalysts,
%% are unique mathematical objects. In contrast, reactions in CRS
%% \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, referred to here as \emph{F-reactions}, treat
%% reactions that differ solely in their catalysts as identical. Accordingly,
%% an F-reaction can be viewed as an equivalence class of reactions that share
%% the same non-catalyst reactants (F-reactants) and the same non-catalyst
%% products (F-products).

%% Building on this observation, we defined an equivalence relation, denoted
%% by $\sim$, on the set of reactions based exclusively on their F-reactants
%% and F-products. Under this relation, reactions are mapped to the set of
%% F-reactions and can be identified as representatives of their corresponding
%% $\sim$-equivalence classes, that is, their associated F-reaction images.
%% As a convenient consequence, we obtained the directed bipartite K\H{o}nig
%% representation of a CRS $\queen(\Phi)$, as the quotient graph of the
%% K\H{o}nig graph associated with its underlying CRN, $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$,
%% thereby establishing a formal mathematical correspondence between CRSs and
%% CRNs. This result was not only essential for the results presented in this
%% contribution, but also provides a foundation for future research aimed at
%% further exploring the relationship between these two interpretations of
%% chemical reaction systems. Moreover, it facilitates the transfer of results
%% obtained in one framework to the other, and vice versa.

In the present contribution we examine this relationship in greater
detail, focusing in particular on the interplay between the minimal
structures arising in both frameworks.  \PFS{To this end we started from
  Thm.~3.1 in \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, which asserts that every
  \textsc{RAF} that is not a \textsc{CAF} is \emph{stoichiometrically
  autocatalytic}. As an immediate consequence, \emph{irreducible RAFs}
  \cite{steel_minimal_2013}, i.e., \textsc{RAF}s that do not contain proper
  sub-RAFs, are \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} as long as they are
  not CAFs.} Lemma~\ref{lem:trivRAFCAF} show that irreducible RAFs are CAFs
if and only if they are trivial, i.e., consisting of a single reaction.

The minimality of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs
%% is a particularly strong property, as it gives rise to several 
%%noteworthy structural consequences that are also characteristic of \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} CRNs. 
%%In particular, it imposes
implies strong connectivity on both the R-graph of the CRS and the
underlying CRN. 

Since strong connectivity is a also prerequisite for autocatalytic cores.
Their bipartite digraph representation in CRNs with explicit catalysis are
\emph{fluffles}, and thus in particular strong blocks
\cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}. The bipartite K\H{o}nig graph of
non-trivial, irreducible RAFs, reflecting the underlying CRN without food
and waste molecules, $\lord(\RAF)$, \PFS{is not only strongly connected but
  composed of strong blocks that are separated by cut vertices that are
  always reaction vertices and never species vertices
  (Lemma~\ref{lem:CutVertexReac}).}
%See also Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex} for an example. 

Strong blocks are, by definition, well-formed. In addition, some of their
cut vertices, so-called \emph{pure reactions}, which have only F-catalysts
as reactants inside the corresponding block $B$, can act as import
reactions as they induce a non-negative column in the stoichiometric
submatrix $\SM(B)$. In theory, this would allow for an infinitely high
in-flow such that all species inside the block are net-produced. Indeed,
$\SM(B)$ is semi-positive for any strong block $B$, such that each block is
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} (Thm.~\ref{Thm:StrBlockCore}).

The structures responsible for each strong block being
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} are \emph{Metzler circuits};
elementary circuits that allow for a column-reordering of their associated
stoichiometric submatrix such that the resulting Matrix is a Metzler matrix
(Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}). Such an ordering can be obtained by
arranging the columns according to the direction of the circuit. In the
proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}, we provided a construction of a
Metzler circuit that always contains a pure reaction.

The existence of a Metzler circuit with a pure reaction has already been
shown to exist for any RAF that is itself not a CAF
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. However, in the proof of Thm.~3.1 of
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, the construction follows the F-catalysts as
reactants for each reaction, and thereby not necessarily the reactant with
the lowest generation number. Non-trivial minimal RAFs exist, where this
construction would not yield a \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
Metzler circuit for each strong block $B$. Such an example is depicted in
Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex2}. Indeed, the block $(\{x_1,x_2\}, \{r_1,r_2\})$
does not contain an elementary circuit, where each species vertex is the
F-catalyst of its appurtenant reaction. Nevertheless, $C\coloneqq
(x_1,r_1,x_2,r_2,x_1)$ is still a Metzler circuit with a pure reaction
($r_1$), and thereby \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
(Lemma.~\ref{lem:MetzCircAut}). In addition, $(x_3,r_2,x_4,r_3, x_3)$
satisfies the construction of Thm.~3.1 of \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}.

The example depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:AutCircNotCore} clearly illustrates,
however, that these \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} Metzler
circuits are not necessarily autocatalytic cores. On the one hand,
non-trivial stoichiometries can give rise to strictly smaller cores that
can be contained inside the constructed circuits. An example of this case
is shown in Sec.~\ref{subsec:OneRAFManyCores}. In turn, \PFS{the same} core
can be contained in \PFS{more than one} irreducible RAFs (see
Sec.~\ref{subsec:ManyRAFsOneCore}) or the construction in
Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit} can yield two equivalent autocatalytic cores
(see Sec.~\ref{subsec:IrrEquivCores}). We might also find autocatalytic
reactions in trivial and non-trivial irreducible RAFs (see Eq.~4.18,
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026} and Fig.~\ref{Fig:ExplAutocatReac}).

%\section{Concluding Remarks}

\PFS{The results obtained here show that there are meaningful connections
  between the the CRS and CRN formalisms that also imply strong
  similarities between notions of autocatalysis, in particular between
  non-trivial irreducible RAFs and autocatalytic cores. While our results
  yield useful implications, they are still far from establishing 1-1
  correspondences between concepts in the two alternative formal
  frameworks. While the present results in essence characterize non-trivial
  minimal RAFs in terms of CRNs, the consverse, i.e., a characterization of
  autocatalytic cores in the language of CRS remains an open reseach
  project. Given the differences between the two formalisms, it is not
  clear 1-1 correspondences exist, in particular since \emph{stoichiometric
    autocatalysis} depends on the numerical values of the stoichiometric
  coefficient.}
