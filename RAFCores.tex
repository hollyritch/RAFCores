\section{Introduction}

\section{Representation of Systems with Chemical Reactions}

The literature on systems of chemical interactions is inconsistent in the way how 
they are formalized. In particular, different authors have introduced different notions of 
reactions \cite{muller_what_2022}. We will therefore take advantage of different style formats, i.e., $\CR$ 
and $\FR$, to highlight sets of reactions that are derived from chemical reaction networks 
\cite{muller_what_2022} and from catalytic reaction systems \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, 
respectively. We will see that both sets do not contain the same mathematical objects. 

\subsection{Chemical Reaction Networks (CRNs)}

The most general setting is given by the framework of a chemical reaction network (CRN). 
A CRN $\Gamma \coloneqq (X,\CR)$ is a tuple of two finite sets, a set of metabolites $X$ 
and a set of reactions $\CR$. For any $r\in \CR$, we can write a stoichiometric equation as: 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reaction}
  \sum_{x\in X}\ s^-_{xr} \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ s^+_{xr}\cdot x
\end{equation}
where $s^{\pm}_{xr}$ are the educt and product stoichiometry of the
molecule $x$ in the reaction $r$. Reactants and products are collected in the sets:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\ceducts&:  \CR \rightarrow X, \ceducts(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^-_{xr}>0\} \\
		\cproducts&: \CR \rightarrow X, \cproducts(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^+_{xr}>0\} 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
A \emph{sub-CRN} is a tuple $(X',R')$ with $X'\subseteq X$ and $R'\subseteq R$. A \emph{sub-CRN}
is \emph{well-formed} if for each reaction $r\in R'$ there are specis $x,y\in X': s^-_{xr}>0, s^+_{xr}>0$. 
The \emph{stoichiometric matrix} $\SM$ encodes all net-stoichiometries:
\begin{equation}
  \SM_{xr}\coloneqq s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr}
\end{equation}
For a reaction $r$ we can define its \emph{net reaction} $\net{r}$ as
  \begin{equation}
      \sum_{x\in X}\ \net{s}^-_{xr} \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X}
      \ \net{s}^+_{xr}\cdot x 
    \end{equation}
    with 
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      \net{s}^-_{xr} = -\SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr} < 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise}\\
      \net{s}^+_{xr} = \SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr}> 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise}   
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}
  and 
  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      \net{\ceducts}: \CR \rightarrow X, \net{\ceducts}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \net{s}^-_{xr}>0\} \\
      \net{\cproducts}: \CR \rightarrow X, \net{\cproducts}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \net{s}^+_{xr}>0\} \\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}
  as the net reactants (educts) and net products of reaction $r$, respectively. Note, we thereby have 
  $\net{\ceducts}(r) = \ceducts(\net{r})$ and $\net{\cproducts}(r) = \cproducts(\net{r})$.

Multiple reasons exist that account for the case $s^{\pm}_{xr}\neq \net{s}^{\pm}_{xr}$;
one are catalysts. In the original CRN setting, a catalyst for a reaction $r$ is an entity $x\in X$ 
such that $s^{-}_{xr}\cdot s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, thus it appears on both sides of the stoichiometric 
equation of $r$. In case $s^-_{xr}>s^+_{xr}>0$, we say $x$ is both reactant and catalyst of reaction 
$r$, while in case $s^+_{xr}>s^-_{xr}>0$, $x$ is both product and catalyst of reaction $r$. In the latter 
$x$ is also called an \emph{autocatalyst}, while in the former case, $x$ is called an \emph{autoinhibitor}. 

From a chemical perspective and the IUPAC definition, a catalyst is an entity $x\in X$ that not only satisfies 
$s^{-}_{xr}\cdot s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, but also ``increases the rate of a reaction without modifying the overall 
standard Gibbs energy change in the reaction'' \cite{gold_iupac_2025}. However, a solvent, such 
as water, can also act in such a way without being considered a catalyst \cite{marx_nature_1999}. For example, 
in Brønstedt/Lowry's acid-base theory, water is rather considered a reactant, i.e., a proton acceptor, removing 
environmental constraints to facilitate proton transfer between reactants \cite{eigen_proton_1964}.

To account for the different roles an entity can play within a chemical 
reaction, we partition stoichiometries as done in \cite{bridging_golnik_2026} such that: 
\begin{equation}
	\sigma^{\pm}_{xr} + k_{xr} = s^{\pm}_{xr}.
\end{equation}
By this definition, it is readily verified that $\SM_{xr} = s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr} = \sigma^+_{xr} - \sigma^-_{xr}$, thus the net stoichiometries
remain unchanged. The stoichiometric Eq.~\ref{eq:reaction} can then be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mod.SmEq}
	r: \sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{xr} \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr} \cdot x\right)\rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ \sigma^+_{xr}  \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr} \cdot x\right).
\end{equation}
We refer to species appearing in Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq} with $\sigma^{-}_{xr}>0$, $\sigma^{+}_{xr}>0$, and $k_{xr}>0$ as 
F-reactants, F-products, and F-catalysts, respectively. We collect F-reactants and F-products in the following sets:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\feducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^-_{xr} > 0\} \\
		\fproducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^+_{xr} > 0\} \\
	\end{split}
\end{equation} 
Different reactions with the same net reaction may differ in their usage of catalysts, by requiring 
different or multiple synergistically acting catalysts. For example, the hydrogenation of ethene to ethane 
can be mediated by platinum, palladium, or nickel separately \cite{crampton_ethylene_2016}. An example 
for co-catalysis, i.e., where the orchestrated cooperation of two catalysts is required, is the dual-catalyst 
system of palladium and copper in the Hoechst-Wacker process, where ethene is oxidized to acetaldehyde 
\cite{jira_acetaldehyde_2009}. We therefore define the set of F-catalysts as a multiset:
\begin{equation}
	\cat(r)\coloneqq \{\kappa_{xr} \cdot x\ | \ x\in X\}
\end{equation}
The set of F-catalysts may be empty, i.e., for $\cat({\net{r}})=\emptyset$ by definition, or if the reaction is uncatalyzed. 

In a CRN $(X,\CR)$, each reaction $r$, as specified by Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq}, is a distinct object. Thus,
it is possible that two reactions sharing the same net reaction are elements of $\CR$. In this case, they 
appear as unique vertices in the bipartite K\"onigs representation of a CRN 
$\king\coloneqq (X\cup \C{R}, E_1\cup E_2)$, where
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		E_1\coloneqq \{(x,r) \ | \ s^-_{xr}>0\} \quad \text{ and } \quad E_2\coloneqq \{(x,r) \ | \ s^+_{xr}>0 \}. 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
We will refer to these kinds of reactions as C-reactions and denote them in normal font. The reaction or R-graph
of a CRN is the directed graph $\mathcal{R}(\Gamma)$ with vertex set $R$ and edge set 
$E\coloneqq \{(r_1,r_2) \ \vert \ \exists x\in X: s^+_{xr_1}>0,s^-_{xr_2}>0\}$.

\subsection{Catalytic Reaction Systems (CRS)}

Catalytic reaction systems (CRS) sensu Lohn \cite{lohn_evolving_1998} take a different approach. 
In particular, two C-reactions $r_1, r_2$ are considered indistinguishable if they differ only in their 
F-catalysts but share the same F-reactants and F-products:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fEquiv}
	r_1\sim r_2 \Leftrightarrow \feducts(r_1)=\feducts(r_2), \fproducts(r_1)=\fproducts(r_2), 
\end{equation}
Indeed, $\sim$ is an equivalence relation, and we call a $\sim$-equivalence class an F-reaction. 
For consistency, we denote them in bold, i.e., $\fr\coloneqq [r]_\sim$ for:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fEquivClass}
	\begin{split}
		\fr= \left\{r': \sum_{x\in X}\ (\sigma^-_{xr'} + \kappa_{xr'}) \cdot x)\rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ (\sigma^+_{xr'} + \kappa_{xr'} \cdot x) \ \middle\vert \ \sigma_{xr'}^{\pm} = \sigma_{xr}^\pm, 	
			\forall x\in X \right\}
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
With a slight abuse of notation, we write $r\in \fr$ for $r\in [r]_\sim$, and $\FR$ for sets of 
F-reactions. Moreover, we may also write $\feducts(\fr)$ and $\fproducts(\fr)$ since 
$\feducts(r') =\feducts(r), \fproducts(r') =\fproducts(r), \forall r' \in[r]_\sim$. The natural 
representative is the uncatalyzed reaction $\tilde{r}\in [r]_{\sim}: \cat(\tilde{r}) = \emptyset$, 
which coincides with the net reaction $\hat{r}$ if and only if $\feducts(r)= \net{\ceducts}(r)$ and 
$\fproducts(\tilde{r}) = \net{\cproducts}(r)$. 

We may conveniently obtain for a given set of C-reactions $R$ the corresponding F-reactions 
via:
\begin{equation}
	\varphi: R\rightarrow \FR: \varphi(r) \coloneqq \fr, 
\end{equation}
where $\FR\coloneqq \{[r]_{\sim} \ | \ r\in R\}$. This allows access to the pre-image 
of a set of F-reactions $\FR$ by $\varphi^{-1}(\FR)$. 

By definition, $[r]_\sim$ is of infinite size, which is an unrealistic scenario for 
a chemical system. Given a set of F-reactions $\FR$ together with a set of species $X$, each 
$\sim$-equivalence class can be restricted to representatives that members catalyze 
of the species set or are uncatalyzed. This is achieved by \emph{catalyzations}, i.e., by
defining \emph{a priori} for each $\fr\in \FR$ pairs $(\cat(r), r)$ and storing the collection of 
catalysts $\cat(\fr)\coloneqq \{\cat(r) \ \vert \ r\in \fr\}$ in the set of catalyzations $\cat(\FR)$. 
Note that if an F-reaction has no catalyzations, $\cat(\fr)=\emptyset$. In this case, $\fr$ contains 
only the natural representative, while in all other cases, the natural representative is excluded 
from the equivalence class:
\begin{equation}
	\fr\coloneqq \begin{cases} 
				\tilde{r} & \text{if } \cat(\fr)=\emptyset \\
				\{r' \ \vert \ \cat(r')\neq \emptyset\} & \text{else}
			   \end{cases} 
\end{equation}
In this way, each F-reaction is of finite size, and throughout we will consider 
them finite unless otherwise specified. A CRS is then the triple $\Phi \coloneqq (X,\FR, \cat)$, where
$\Phi \coloneqq (X,\FR, \cat)$, thereby, has a unique corresponding CRN $\Gamma(\Phi) = (X, \CR)$, 
where $\CR=\bigcup_{\fr \in \FR}\fr$. For clarity, we might write the CRN as a triple as $(X, \CR, \cat)$.

Analogously to the bipartite graph representation of a CRN, the bipartite graph of a CRS 
is given as $\queen(\Phi=(X,\FR, \cat))\coloneqq (X \cup \FR, E_1\cup E_2)$ with:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		E_1\coloneqq \{(x,\fr) \ | \ x\in X, \fr\in \FR: s^-_{xr}>0 \text{ for some } r \in \fr \} \\
		E_2\coloneqq \{(x,\fr) \ | \ x\in X, \fr\in \FR: s^+_{xr}>0 \text{ for some } r \in \fr \}
	\end{split}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The Relationship between CRN and CRS}

In fact, the $\sim$-equivalence relation induces a convenient connection between the bipartite graph of 
$\queen(\Phi)$ and the corresponding bipartite graph of the $K(\Gamma(\Phi))$. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:QuotGraph}
	Let $\Phi\coloneqq (X,\FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with corresponding CRN $\Gamma(\Phi)$. The bipartite
	directed K\"onig graph graph $\queen\coloneqq \queen(\Phi)$ is the quotient graph of $\king \coloneqq \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ w.r.t. the
	equivalence relation $\sim$ as defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:fEquiv}. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $(x,\fr) \in E_1(\queen)$. By definition, there is an $r'\in \fr: s^-_{xr'}>0$, thus $(x,r')\in E_1(\king)$. 
	Let now, $(x,r)\in E_1(\king)$. By definition of $\queen$, there is an edge $(x,\fr)\in E_1(\queen)$. 
	Similar arguments hold for edges from $E_2(\queen)$ and $E_2(\king)$. 
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
In the monocatalyst case, for example, where each F-reaction has exactly one catalyst,  
$\vert \fr \vert = 1, \forall \fr \in \FR$. Thus, each reaction vertex of $\queen(\Phi)$ is substituted 
in $\queen(\Gamma(\Phi))$ by its single representative, while all edges to adjacent vertices are 
conserved. This yields that $\queen(\Phi)$ and $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ are isomorphic. In turn, 
if one equivalence class contains more than one representative, then 
$|V(\king(\Gamma(\Phi))|>|V(\queen(\Phi)|$, which directly implies that 
$\king(\Gamma(\Phi))\not \simeq \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:IsoQK}
	Let $\Phi\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with corresponding CRN $\Gamma(\Phi)$. Then 
	$\queen(\Phi)\simeq \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ if and only if $\vert \fr \vert = 1, \forall \fr \in \FR$.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}

As a remark, we might note that Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK} not only holds in the monocatalytic case,
but for every CRS that exhibits only trivial equivalence classes, in particular also for the uncatalyzed 
case.

An obvious result for quotient graphs is that $\queen(\Phi)$ is strongly-connected whenever 
$\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ is strongly connected. This follows immediately from the fact that 
strong connectivity is preserved under surjective digraph homomorphisms \cite{hell_graphs_2026}. 
By Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK}, the converse also holds if each equivalence class is of size $1$. 
In general, however, this is not true, in particular, if one F-reaction has two alternative 
catalysts, and one of them is not connected by a path originating in any of the products. 
Such an example is depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:QScKNot}. We will see that we regain this 
property by requiring minimality on the set of reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs).

\section{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)}

Catalytic reaction systems (CRS) are the fundamental structure on which the notion of reflexively autocatalytic sets 
is built \cite{steel_emergence_2000, hordijk_detecting_2004}. In addition, they rely on the closure of a set $X' \subseteq X$ 
w.r.t. to a set $\RAF \subseteq \FR$, denoted as $\closure{\RAF}{X'}$, which is the unique minimal set $Y$ containing $X'$ 
such that for any $\fr\in \RAF: \feducts(r)\subseteq Y \Rightarrow \fproducts(r)\subseteq Y$. Thus:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Closure}
	\closure{R'}{X'} \coloneqq \min \{Z \subseteq X \ | \ X'\subseteq Z, \feducts(r) \subseteq Z \Rightarrow \fproducts(r) \subseteq Z \ \forall r\in \RAF'\}%}_{\eqqcolon \mathfrak{C}_{\RAF}(X')}
\end{equation}

A reflexively autocatalytic set (RAF) is then defined as follows:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}[RAF]\label{def:RAF}
	Let $(X,\FR,\cat)$ be a CRS with $\food\subseteq X$. A set $\RAF\subseteq \FR$ is called  
		\vspace{6pt}
		\begin{itemize} 
			\item[i.]{\emph{reflexive autocatalytic} (RA) if $\forall r\in \RAF: \emptyset \neq \cat(r), \exists c\in \cat(r): c \subseteq \feducts(\RAF)\cup \fproducts(\RAF)$}\\[-1.0em]
			\item[ii.]{\emph{\food-generated} if $\feducts(\RAF)\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food}$}\\[-1.0em]
			\item[iii.]{\emph{reflexive autocatalytic} and \emph{\food-generated} (RAF) if both i. and ii. hold}
		\end{itemize}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

We remark that Property $i.$ requires each F-reaction to be catalyzed, 
and for each F-reaction, all catalysts for at least one catalyzation are in the 
support of $\RAF$, i.e. in $\feducts(\RAF)\cup \fproducts(\RAF)$. Property $ii.$ 
of Def.~\ref{def:RAF}, together with Eq.~\ref{eq:Closure}, directly implies that all 
F-products for any reaction $\fr\in \RAF$ are also contained in the $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$, 
thus $\fproducts(r)\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{\food}$. 

\subsection{A Partial Order on RAFs}

The closure naturally induces an order, a sequence in which the F-reactions of a RAF can be executed. This 
is not a total order since some reactions, e.g., the ones requiring only food molecules as F-reactants 
can be executed at the same time, while others, depending on molecules produced by these food-dependent reactions, 
can only take place consecutively. Here, we briefly explain the main idea and its consequences, while we will devote 
the supplement to formally deriving the partial order. 

A convenient way to construct an order on the F-reactions of a RAF $\RAF$ is by generating the set $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ 
iteratively, in particular, by applying in each step all F-reactions for which all F-reactants are already available. Therefore, 
it is only consequent to assume that this iterative process converges against $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ in a finite number of 
steps, all species of $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ as long as $\vert \RAF\vert <\infty$.

The sequences specifying when an F-reaction can happen for the first time and when a non-food species is produced for the
first time provide an order $\preceq$ on the set of F-reactions $\FR$ and on the set of species $X$. The generation in 
which a species $x\in X$ appears as a product for the first time will be denoted by $\sgen(x)$ and set by definition 
$\sgen(f)\coloneqq 0$ for all $f\in \food$. In addition, we denote the generation in which all F-reactants are available for 
an F-reaction $\fr$ by $\rgen(\fr)$. A species $x_1$ precedes a species $x_2$, i.e.: 
	\begin{equation}
		x_1\preccurlyeq x_2 \Leftrightarrow \sgen(x_1) \leq \sgen(x_2)
	\end{equation} 
and a reaction $\fr_1$ precedes a reaction $\fr_2$, i.e.: 
\begin{equation}
	\fr_1\preceq \fr_2 \Leftrightarrow \rgen(\fr_1) \leq \rgen(\fr_2)
\end{equation}
We refer to the values of $\sgen$ and $\rgen$ as the generation of a reaction or metabolite. Since all representatives
of an F-reaction share the same F-reactants, we may define the generation of a C-reaction as $\delta(r)\coloneqq \rgen(\fr)$.
A \emph{constructive reflexively autocatalytic} set (CAF) is a RAF such that for each F-reaction 
$\fr$ all $x\in \feducts(x)$ satisfy $\sgen(x)<\rgen(\fr)$ and there is at least one $r\in \fr$ such that 
all catalyst $x\in \cat(r)$ satisfy $\sgen(x)< \rgen(\fr)$.

One important consequence of the partial order is that if a RAF contains two $\sim$~equivalence classes 
that share the same net reaction, the "later" can be removed.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:OneFReacPerRAF}
	Let $(X,\FR,\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food\subseteq X$, $\RAF\subseteq \FR$ a $\RAF$, and 
	$\fr_1,\fr_2 \in \RAF: \fr_1\neq \fr_2, \hat{\fr}_1 = \hat{\fr}_2'$. Then $\RAF\setminus \{\fr_2\}$ is a RAF
	if $\rgen(\fr_1)\leq \rgen(\fr_2)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $\RAF' \coloneqq \RAF \setminus \{r''_2\}$. Since $\RAF$ was reflexively autocatalytic, 
	$\RAF'$ is reflexively autocatalytic. In addition, since $\nfeducts(\fr_1)=\nfeducts(\fr_2)$ 
	and $\nfproducts(\fr_1)=\nfproducts(\fr_2)$ and $\rgen(\fr_1)\leq \rgen(\fr_2)$, 
	$\closure{\RAF}{F} = \closure{\RAF'}{F}$. 
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt} 
\noindent We may remark that the converse is not true. To see this, consider the following CRN example:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		r_1&: f + (x_1) \hspace{22pt} \rightarrow x_1 + x_2 + (x_1)\\
		r_2&: f + x_1 + (x_1) \rightarrow 2x_1 + x_2 + (x_1)
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
Then clearly, $\fr_1\neq \fr_2$ and $\rgen(\fr_1) = 1 < 2 = \rgen(\fr_2)$ since $\fr_2$ requires an $x_1$ as 
an F-reactant. 

The partial order $\preceq$ allows us to observe that each species vertex of the $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ 
is reachable from at least one food molecule in $\food$ in the bipartite K\"onig graph, 
which is restricted to $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$, the reactions in $\RAF$, and to those catalyzations 
$\cat'$ of reactions in $\RAF\subseteq \FR$. Throughout, we denote this graph as 
$\queen'\coloneqq \queen(\closure{\RAF}{\food}, \RAF, \cat')$.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:RAFPath}
	Let $(X, \FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with $\food \subseteq X$ and a RAF $\RAF\subseteq \FR$. 
	For any $x\in \closure{}{\food}\setminus \food$ there is an $f\in \food$ such that $P=(f, \ldots, x)$ 
	is a path in $\queen'$.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{irreducible RAFs}

Minimal stoichiometrically autocatalytic subnetworks are termed autocatalytic cores. Their characterization and 
computational enumeration has gained reasonable attention in chemical reaction networks with and without explicit 
catalysis \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020, golnik_birne_2025, golnik_using_2026, golnik_autocatalytic_2026}. 
In \cite{bridging_golnik_2026}, we have shown that each RAF that is not a CAF contains a stoichiometrical 
autocatalytic subnetwork and thereby an autocatalytic core. It is therefore reasonable to ask for the 
substructures of a RAF that carry autocatalytic cores. 

The most natural starting point for this endeavour are RAFs that contain no proper subset that is also
a RAF. Minimal sets of F-reactions w.r.t. the RAF properties have been termed \emph{irreducible} RAFs 
in the literature \cite{steel_minimal_2013}. A direct consequence of Lemma~\ref{lem:OneFReacPerRAF} 
is that irreducible RAFs contain, for each net reaction, only one F-reaction. 
\vspace{6pt} 
\begin{corollary}
	Let $\fr_r,\fr_2 \in \RAF: \fr_1\neq \fr_2, \hat{\fr}_1=\hat{\fr}_2$. Then $\fr_1\in \RAF\Rightarrow \fr_2\notin \RAF$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt} 
Still, they might contain F-reactions that have more than one representative, as the following example shows.
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		r'_1&: f + (x_1) \rightarrow x_3 + (x_1)\\
		r''_1&: f + (x_2) \rightarrow x_3 + (x_3)\\
		r_2&:  x_3 + (x_3) \rightarrow x_1 + x_2 + (x_3)
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
The only irreducible RAF in this example is $\{\fr_1=\{r_1',r_1''\}, \fr_2=\{r_2\}\}$, which implies that 
Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK} does not hold for irreducible RAFs.

According to Theorem 3.1 of \cite{bridging_golnik_2026}, an autocatalytic elementary circuit can be found 
in a RAF as long as it is not a CAF. irreducible RAFs exhibit the convenient property that they are not CAFs 
as long as they are non-trivial, i.e., if they are composed of more than a single reaction.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:trivRAFCAF}
	Let $(X,\RAF,\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food$ and $\RAF$ a irreducible RAF. Then $\RAF$ is a CAF if and only
	if it is trivial. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $\RAF$ be a CAF and assume $|\RAF|>1$. Let $r\in \RAF: \delta(r)=1$. Then all F-reactants and all F-catalysts for one 
	\emph{catalyzation} are in $\food$ since $\RAF$ is a CAF. Thus $\{r\}$ is a RAF itself, contradicting the minimality of $\RAF$. 
	Conversely, assume that $|\RAF|=1$. Then all F-reactants and all F-catalysts for at least one \emph{catalyzation} are in $\food$ 
	since otherwise $\RAF$ would not be a RAF. Thus, $\RAF$ is a CAF.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
This Lemma justifies focusing only on non-trivial, irreducible RAFs. In fact, for each non-trivial, 
irreducible RAF, each F-reaction has at least one reactant that is not in the food set. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:OneReac}
	Let $(X,\RAF,\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food$ and $\RAF$ a irreducible RAF.
	Then $\fr\in \RAF$ has at least one reactant (F-reactant or F-catalyst) that is not 
	in $\food$. For $\fr\in \RAF: \rgen(\fr)=1$, this is an F-catalyst.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	 Assume this is not the case for an $\fr\in \RAF$. Then $\{\fr\}$ is a RAF which 
	 contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

The consequences of the result of Lemma~\ref{lem:OneReac} were already investigated 
in \cite{steel_minimal_2013}. It was shown that the R-graph for each such 
RAF contains an elementary circuit. In the next subsection, we will therefore turn to investigate
more thoroughly the structural properties of two graph representations of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs, 
the bipartite and the reaction graph, in both the CRS and the CRN setting. In particular,
we will strengthen the result obtained by Steel et al. in \cite{steel_minimal_2013}.

\subsection{The Bipartite and Reaction Graphs of irreducible RAFs}

Throughout, we will consider a CRS $\Phi\coloneqq (X,\FR,\cat)$ with food-set $\food\subseteq X$ and 
K\"onig graph $\queen\coloneqq \queen(\Phi)$. In addition, we assume a non-trivial, 
irreducible RAF $\RAF\subseteq \FR$ with sub-CRS 
\begin{equation}
	\Phi'= (\closure{\RAF}{\food}, \RAF, \cat')\\
\end{equation}
and K\"onig graph $\queen'\coloneqq \queen(\Phi')$ as well as its CRN representation 
$\king'\coloneqq \king(\Gamma(\Phi'))$. We denote both R-graphs of $\queen'$ and $\king'$ 
as the graphs 
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\RGraph(\queen')&\coloneqq (\RAF, E), \hspace{24pt} E\coloneqq \{(\fr_1,\fr_2) \ \vert \ \exists x\in X: s^+_{x\fr_1}>0, s^-_{x\fr_2}>0\} \\
		\RGraph(\king') &\coloneqq (\varphi^{-1}(\RAF), E), E\coloneqq \{(r_1,r_2) \ \vert \ (\fr_1,\fr_2)\in E(\RGraph(\queen')\},
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
respectively. In line with calling elements of $\food$ food-molecules, we kindly refer to molecules of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Waste}
	W\coloneqq \{x\in \closure{\RAF}{\food} \ | \ \nexists \fr \in \RAF \text{ s.t. } s^-_{xr}>0\}		
\end{equation}
as waste molecules. We might note that $V(\king')=\closure{\RAF}{\food})$.

\subsubsection{Strong connectivity}

We start this section by showing that the R-graph of a non-trivial, irreducible RAF in the CRS setting, 
$\RGraph(\queen)$, is strongly connected.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:RGQSC}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then $\RGraph(\queen')$ is strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case. Then there are at least two strongly connected components
	$\RAF_1$ and $\RAF_2$. Assume w.l.o.g. that there is no path from any $r_2\in \RAF_2$ 
	to any $r_1\in \RAF_1$. Then all F-reactants and F-catalysts of all F-reactions in $\RAF_1$
	are in the closure $\closure{\RAF_2}{\food}$, in symbols $\feducts\cup \cat(\RAF_1)\subseteq 
	\closure{\RAF_2}{\food}$. With $\RAF_1\subseteq \RAF$ and $\RAF$ being a $\RAF$, this 
	implies that $\RAF_1$ is itself a $\RAF$ which contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
Lemmata~\ref{lem:trivRAFCAF} and \ref{lem:RGQSC} can be summarized to the following statement.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:RAFStrCon}
	Let $\RAF$ be a irreducible RAF. Then $\RAF$ is non-trivial if and only if the graph $\RGraph(\queen')$ 
	is not a single F-reaction and strongly connected.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

It is not hard to see that $\RGraph(\queen')$ is also the quotient graph for $\RGraph(\king')$. 
Fig.~\ref{fig:QScKNot} illustrates that the strong connectivity of a quotient graph does not guarantee 
strong connectivity of the original graph. However, for non-trivial, irreducible RAFs, $\RGraph(\king')$ is 
indeed strongly connected. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:KSC}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then $\RGraph(\king')$ is strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}
It is readily verified that the subgraphs of $\queen'$ and $\king'$ containing only those vertices that have at least 
one incoming and one outgoing edge are strongly connected as well. However, these subgraphs might still contain 
food molecules. The next two statements show that their removal does not destroy strong connectivity.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:PSC}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then 
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:PrinceGraph}
			\princess(\RAF) \coloneqq \queen'[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus (\food\cup W) \cup \RAF]
		\end{equation}
	is strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case. Then there are two strongly connected components $\princess_1,\princess_2$.
	W.l.o.g., we assume that there is no path from any $u\in \princess_2$ to any $v\in \princess_1$. Then
	all paths from $\princess_2$ to $\princess_1$ pass through $\food$ in $\queen'$. This, however, implies
	that all F-catalysts and F-reactants for all F-reactions in $\princess_1$ are contained in $\food\cup 
	\princess_1$. Then $\FR(\princess_1)$ is a non-empty RAF which contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.	
\end{proof}	
\vspace{6pt}

Following the same argument as in Lemma~\ref{lem:PSC}, we obtain an analogous result for the subgraph of $\king'$ 
that contains no food and waste molecules.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then 
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:PrinceGraph}
			\lord(\RAF) \coloneqq \king'[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus (\food\cup W) \cup \varphi^{-1}(\RAF)]
		\end{equation}
	is strongly connected.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

\subsubsection{Strong Blocks}

The existence of cut-vertices, which can only be reaction vertices, enables the decomposition of $\princess$ and $\lord$
 into strong blocks. For the graph $\lord(\RAF)$, each of these blocks contains an autocatalytic core, which 
 is warranted by a unique structural property common to any strong block that guarantees semipositivity. In particular, each
 block in $\princess(\RAF)$ and $\lord(\RAF)$ contains reaction vertices for which all reactants are F-catalysts but not F-reactants. 
 We thereby collect them in the following sets:
 \begin{equation}
 	\begin{split}
	 	\FR^*(\block)\coloneqq \{\fr\in \FR(\block) \ | \ s^-_{x\fr}>0 \Rightarrow s^+_{x\fr}=s^-_{x\fr}, \forall x\in X(\block)\} \\
		R^*(\block)\coloneqq \{r\in \varphi^{-1}{\FR}(\block) \ | \ s^-_{xr}>0 \Rightarrow s^+_{xr}=s^-_{xr}, \forall x\in X(\block)\}.
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
and refer to these vertices as \emph{pure F-reactions} and \emph{pure reactions}, respectively.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:cutVertexPureCat}
	For a non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$, any strong block $\block$ of $\princess(\RAF)$ contains a \emph{pure reaction}.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}
Following the same line of argument as in the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat}, the same statement also holds for 
$\lord(\RAF)$, with the small difference that for some of the cut-vertices $\fr\in \RAF$ all representatives that are not cut-vertices 
in $\lord(\RAF)$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:cutVertexPureCat}
	For a non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$, any strong block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ contains a \emph{pure reaction}.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
The proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} has three important consequences. First, it identifies 
cut-vertices as \emph{pure F-reactions} (equivalently \emph{pure reactions}), although not all cut-vertices necessarily 
are. Second, \emph{pure F-reactions} take an important role in the partial order of strong blocks of irreducible RAFs, as 
there is always one with the lowest generation number in a strong block, according to the partial order. Third, 
Cor.~\ref{cor:cutVertexPureCat} implies that the stoichiometric matrix of a strong block $B$ always contains a 
non-negative column. This column represents a \emph{pure reaction}, i.e., it has only reactants that are F-catalysts 
but not F-reactants within $B$ and at least one positive entry for a product $y^*\in X(\block): s^+_{yr^*}>0$:
\begin{equation}
	\SM[X(B), R(B)] = 
			\begin{array}{c c}
				& 	\begin{array}{c c c} 			
						 	& 	r^* 		& 
					\end{array} \\
					\begin{array}{c} 
						\ \\ 
						x^* \\
						\ \\ [0.5em]
						y^* \\
						\ \\
						\ \\		
					\end{array}
					& \begin{pmatrix}						
						 \ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
					 	 \ldots 	& 	0 		& \ldots \\
						 \ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
					 	 \ldots 	& 	s^+_{y^*r^*} & \ldots \\
						  \ldots 	& 	0 & \ldots \\
						\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots 
					\end{pmatrix}
			\end{array}
\end{equation}
\vspace{6pt}

In fact, the nonnegative column reflecting $r^*$ in $\SM(B)\coloneqq \SM[X(B), R(B)]$ renders $r^*$ as 
an import reaction for the strong block $B$. In fact, this allows us to construct a strictly positive vector $v$ 
of reaction rates which satisfies $\SM(B)v>0$. 

\subsection{Autocatalytic Capacity of irreducible RAFs}

CRNs with explicitly catalyzed reactions suffer from the fact that the stoichiometric matrix does 
not uniquely identify the underlying network. The matrix-centered definition of autocatalysis as 
introduced by Blokhuis \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} and Vassena \cite{vassena_unstable_2024} 
therefore required an extension that is based on subnetworks \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}\label{def:autSubRN}
  A CRN $(X,R)$ is \emph{autocatalytic} if it contains a sub-CRN $(X',R')$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
 \item[(i)]  $(X',R')$ is  well-formed;
 \item[(ii)]  $\SM'\coloneqq \SM[X',R']$ is semi-positive, i.e., there is $v\in \mathbb{R}_{>0}^{|R'|}: \SM'v>0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

A sub-CRN $(X',R')$ that satisfies $(i.)$ and $(ii.)$, while no proper subnetwork $(X'',R'')$ with $X''\subseteq X', 
R''\subseteq R'$ does, is called an \emph{autocatalytic core}. With this definition, we are in the position to show 
that $\lord(\RAF)$ for each non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$ is autocatalytic.

The results on the structure of irreducible RAFs imply that each strong block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ for a non-trivial, 
irreducible RAF $\RAF$ satisfies $(i.)$ of Def.~\ref{def:autSubRN}. To show semi-positivity, the key observation 
is that each strong block $B$ contains at least one \emph{pure reaction}, i.e., a reaction for which all reactants 
in $B$ are F-catalysts (Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} and Cor.~\ref{cor:cutVertexPureCat}). In particular, 
all $r \in \varphi^{-1}(\RAF)$ that satisfy $\delta(r)=1$ are \emph{pure reactions}. \emph{Pure reactions} generate a 
nonnegative column with at least one positive entry in the corresponding stoichiometric matrices, either of the strong
block $\SM[X(B), R(B)]$ or of the substoichiometric matrix $\SM[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus \food, \varphi^{-1}(\RAF)]$ 
of the whole RAF without food molecules. In fact, they can be interpreted as import reactions, which allow a sufficiently 
high import into the network such that all succeeding reactions exhibit strictly positive flow values as well. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}[Proof: SI]\label{thm:NonFoodSemipositiv}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. The matrix $A\coloneqq \SM[V(\king')\setminus \food, \varphi^{-1}
	(\RAF)]$ is semi-positive.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

As a remark, the proof can be applied to the sub-stoichiometric matrix representing the species and reaction vertices
of any strong block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ to show their semi-positivity. From another point of view, every species of 
a strong block is only produced and consumed by a reaction of that block. Thereby, the vector $v_{R(B)}$ that 
denotes the sub-vector of $v$ restricted to the reactions of the strong block $B$ directly yields for the submatrix 
restricted to the species and reactions of the strong block $B$: 
\begin{equation}
  \SM[X(B), R(B)]v_{R(B)}>0.
\end{equation}
Thus, each block is semipositiv and also well-formed.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{Thm:StrBlockCore}
	Every strong block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ for a non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$ contains an autocatalytic core. 
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

Thm.~\ref{Thm:StrBlockCore} provides only information on the existence, but not on the structure of the 
subnetworks responsible for the semipositivity of the corresponding submatrix. Leveraging on the result of 
Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat}, we recall that for any strong block $B$, there is a \emph{pure reaction} 
vertex $r^*$, i.e., all reactants of $r^*$ in $B$ are F-catalysts but not F-reactants. As shown above, 
\emph{pure reactions} give rise to a non-negative column with at least one positive entry in the stoichiometric 
submatrix $\SM[X(B), R(B)]$, which allows for the construction of a strictly positive vector of reaction rates $v$ 
that yields $\SM[X(B), R(B)]v>0$, as done in the proof of Thm.~\ref{thm:NonFoodSemipositiv}. 

Since each strong block is strongly connected, each vertex is located on an elementary circuit. In particular, 
\emph{pure reactions} are located on an elementary circuit. The next statement shows that is actually possible 
to provide a construction of an elementary circuit $C$ for every strong block that gives rise to a stoichiometric 
submatrix $\tilde{\SM}(C)\coloneqq \SM[X(C), R(C)]$ in which each column and row has at most one negative 
entry, and one column is nonnegative with at least one positive entry. The columns of $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ can 
then be reordered to obtain a matrix $\SM(C)$ with nonpositive diagonal and nonnegative off-diagonal entries. 
A matrix with nonnegative off-diagonal entries is called a \emph{Metzler} matrix. It is not hard to see that, in 
case $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ is semipositive, $\SM(C)$ is semipositive. In addition, the subnetwork of an elementary 
circuit $(X(C),R(C))$ is well-formed. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:MetzCircuit}
	Let $B$ be a strong block of $\lord(\RAF)$. Then $B$ contains an elementary circuit $C$ with one 
	\emph{pure reaction} vertex, and there is a reordering of the columns of $	\SM[X(C), R(C)]$ that 
	yields Metzler matrix.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}

now Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}, \ref{lem:MetzCircAut}, and Cor.~\ref{cor:AutoCatReac}, we 
obtain the following main result of this contribution. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then every strong block $B$ of $L(\RAF)$ contains an autocatalytic 
	Metzler circuit. In case $B$ is composed of a single reaction $r$ and a single species $x$, then $r$ is an 
	explicit autocatalytic reaction and $x$ is an F-catalyst and an F-product of $r$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Discussion}

\textsc{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)} \cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986, steel_emergence_2000} 
and stoichiometric autocatalysis \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} represent two different 
interpretations of the concept of an explicit autocatalytic reaction, which was first discussed
in 1890 by Wilhelm Ostwald \cite{Ostwald:90}. While RAFs were developed from catalytic
reaction systems (CRS) in which each reaction is explicitly catalyzed, stoichiometric autocatalysis
developed in accordance with the IUPAC definition of an autocatalytic reaction \cite{gold_iupac_2025} 
from chemical reaction networks (CRN), where reactions can but don't have to be catalyzed. Although 
inherently distinct, it was recently shown that each \textsc{RAF} that is not a \textsc{CAF} is stoichiometrically 
autocatalytic \cite{bridging_golnik_2026}. Here, we take a closer look and focus on the investigation 
of the interrelation between the minimal structures of both frameworks. 

The main obstacle for a direct comparison between the two frameworks so far is their different 
interpretation of a chemical reaction. Reactions in CRNs are derived from their corresponding 
stoichiometric equation \cite{muller_what_2022}. Therefore, each distinct equation reflects a 
different chemical reaction, and we call them C-reaction. In particular, reactions that share the 
same net stoichiometries but engage a different set of catalysts or involve species that appear 
on both sides of the reaction with identical stoichiometry without being considered catalysts 
are unique mathematical objects. In contrast, reactions in CRS \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, also 
referred to as F-reactions here, consider C-reactions that differ only in the use of their catalysts as the 
same reaction. In this sense, F-reactions can be described as equivalence classes of C-reactions 
that share the same non-catalyst reactants (F-reactants), and the same non-catalyst products 
(F-products). 

Leveraging on this observation, we derived an equivalence relation, denoted as $\sim$, on the set 
of C-reactions that is solely defined on the set of F-reactants and F-products. Accordingly, C-reactions 
are mapped into the set of F-reactions and identifiable as representatives of their $\sim$-equivalence 
classes, i.e., their F-reaction image. As a convenient consequence, we obtained the directed bipartite 
K\"onig representation of a CRS $\Phi$, denoted as $\queen(\Phi)$, as the quotient graph K\"onig graph 
of the underlying CRN $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ and established a formal mathematical correspondence 
between CRS and CRNs. This result was not only crucial for the subsequent results in this contribution,
but also enables future research to investigate further the relationship between these two interpretations 
of chemical reaction systems and allows for the translation of results obtained for one framework into the 
other one and vice versa.

Starting from the result of Thm.~3.1 in \cite{bridging_golnik_2026}, in this contribution, we were mainly 
concerned with identifying the minimal structures in RAFs that are stoichiometrically autocatalytic.
Since any RAF that is itself not a CAF is autocatalytic, then RAFs that contain no proper sub-RAFs, denoted 
as \emph{irreducible RAFs} in the literature \cite{steel_minimal_2013}, are autocatalytic as well, as 
long as they are not CAFs. In particular, irreducible RAFs are CAFs if and only if they are trivial 
(Lemma~\ref{lem:trivRAFCAF}), which justified our focus on non-trivial, irreducible RAFs. 

The minimality of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs is a particularly strong property, as it gives rise to several 
noteworthy structural consequences that are also characteristic of autocatalytic CRNs. In particular, the 
minimality requirement implies strong connectivity in both the R-graph of the CRS and the underlying 
CRN associated with non-trivial, irreducible RAFs. Consequently, every F- and C-reaction lies on an 
elementary circuit, not only within the corresponding R-graphs, but also within the associated bipartite 
graphs excluding food molecules. It is therefore not surprising that autocatalytic cores reside inside 
those structures, as they exhibit a \emph{fluffle} representation in the bipartite K\"onig 
graph of CRNs with explicit catalysis \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}, and are themselves strongly
connected blocks. 

Similar structures can be found in irreducible RAFs. The bipartite K\"onigs graph of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs, 
reflecting the underlying CRN without food and waste molecules, is not only strongly connected but 
composed of strongly connected blocks that are separated by reaction vertices. Strong blocks are necessarily
well-formed, and some of their cut vertices can act as import reactions as they induce a non-negative column 
substoichiometric matrix $\SM(B)$ of the corresponding block $B$. 

In theory, this would allow for an infinitely high inflow such that all species inside the block are net-produced. 
In fact, $\SM(B)$ is semipositive for any strong block $B$, such that each block is indeed stoichiometrically 
autocatalytic. We can further provide a general construction of an autocatalytic Metzler circuit, carrying such 
an import reaction. However, as Fig.~\ref{fig:AutCircNotCore} shows, they do not necessarily need to be 
autocatalytic cores, as non-trivial stoichiometries can give rise to strictly smaller elementary circuits that can 
be contained inside. Furthermore, an irreducible RAF can contain multiple autocatalytic cores as demonstrated 
in Example~\ref{subsec:OneRAFManyCores}. In turn, one core can be contained in different irreducible RAFs 
(see Fig.~\ref{}) or the construction in Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit} can yield two equivalent autocatalytic cores. 
We might also find autocatalytic reactions in trivial, irreducible RAFs (see Eq.~4.18, \cite{bridging_golnik_2026}).
