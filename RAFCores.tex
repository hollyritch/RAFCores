\section{Preliminaries}



A reaction network $(X,R)$ is tuple of two finite sets, set of metabolites $X$ 
and a set of reactions $R$. For any $r\in R$ we can write a stoichiometric equation as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reaction}
  \sum_{x\in X}\ s^-_{xr} \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ s^+_{xr}\cdot x
\end{equation}
where $s^{\pm}_{xr}$ are the educt and product stoichiometry of the
molecule $x$ in the reaction $r$. Reactants and products are collected in the sets:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\educts&:  R\rightarrow X, \educts(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^-_{xr}>0\} \\
		\products&: R \rightarrow X, \products(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^+_{xr}>0\} 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}

The \emph{stoichiometric matrix} $\SM$ encodes all net-stoichiometries:
\begin{equation}
  \SM_{xr}\coloneqq s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr}
\end{equation}
For a reaction $r$ we can define its \emph{net reaction} $\hat{r}$ as
  \begin{equation}
      \sum_{x\in X}\ \hat{s}^-_{xr} \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X}
      \ \hat{s}^+_{xr}\cdot x 
    \end{equation}
    with 
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      \hat{s}^-_{xr} = -\SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr} < 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise}\\
      \hat{s}^+_{xr} = \SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr}> 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise}   
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}
  and 
  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      \hat{\educts}: R \rightarrow X, \hat{\educts}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \hat{s}^-_{xr}>0\} \\
      \hat{\products}: R \rightarrow X, \hat{\products}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \hat{s}^+_{xr}>0\} \\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}
  as the net reactants (educts) and net products of reaction $r$, respectively. Note, we thereby have 
  $\hat{\educts}(r) = \educts(\hat{r})$ and $\hat{\products}(r) = \products(\hat{r})$.

Multiple reasons exist that account for the case $s^{\pm}_{xr}\neq \hat{s}^{\pm}_{xr}$;
one are catalysts. In the original CRN setting, a catalyst for a reaction $r$ is an entity $x\in X$ 
such that $s^{-}_{xr}\cdot s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, thus it appears on both sides of the stoichiometric 
equation of $r$. In case $s^-_{xr}>s^+_{xr}>0$, we say $x$ is both reactant and catalyst of reaction 
$r$, while in case $s^+_{xr}>s^-_{xr}>0$, $x$ is both product and catalyst of reaction $r$. In the latter 
$x$ is also called an autocatalyst, while in the former case, $x$ is called an autoinhibitor. 

From a chemical perspective, a catalyst is an entity $x\in X$ that not only satisfies $s^{-}_{xr}\cdot 
s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, but also increases the speed of a reaction. However, not only can catalysts act in this way, 
but also the solvent, such as water. Consider the reaction of water-free sulfuric acid with sodium hydroxide 
pellets, which is in general initiated at those sites where water is extracted from the surrounding gas phase 
due to the high hygroscopy of sulfuric acid and sodium hydroxide on some localized surface moisture. Thus in:
\begin{equation}
	\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \leftrightharpoons 2\text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{SO}_4^{2-} 
\end{equation}
H$_2$O serves as the proton acceptor, dissolves the solid sodium hydroxide, and hydrates the hydroxide ions:
\begin{equation}
	\text{NaOH}  \leftrightharpoons \text{Na}^+ + \text{OH}^- \text{ (hydrated) }  
\end{equation}
The hydronium ions consequently react with the hydroxide ions to form water molecules again:
\begin{equation}
	2\text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + 2\text{OH}^-  \leftrightharpoons 4 \text{H}_2\text{O},
\end{equation}
which yields the overall reaction:
\begin{equation}
	\text{H}_2{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} + 2\text{NaOH} \leftrightharpoons 4\text{H}_2\text{O} + 2\text{Na}^+ + \text{SO}_4^{2-}. 
\end{equation}
In this case, $2=s_{xr}^-\neq \hat{s}_{xr}^-=0$, and $4=s_{xr}^+\neq \hat{s}^+_{xr}=2$ for H$_2$O.
Although complete water-freeness can not be guaranteed under realistic labratory conditions, water is not considered
to act as a catalyst since it does not fundamentally change the reaction mechanism, but rather removes environmental
constraints by facilitating proton transfer via hydronium ion formation, stabilizing hydroxide ions through hydrate shells,
and forming chains of hydrogen bonds.

To account for the different roles an entity can play within a chemical reaction, we partition  
stoichiometries as done in \cite{bridging_Golnik_2026} in the following way: 
\begin{equation}
	s^{\pm}_{xr} \coloneqq \sigma^{\pm}_{xr} + \kappa_{xr}.
\end{equation}
By this definition, it is readily verified that $\SM_{xr} = s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr} = \sigma^+_{xr} - \sigma^-_{xr}$, thus the net stoichiometries
remain unchanged. The stoichiometric Eq.~\ref{eq:reaction} can then be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mod.SmEq}
	r: \sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{xr} \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ \kappa_{xr} \cdot x\right)\rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ \sigma^+_{xr}  \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ \kappa_{xr} \cdot x\right).
\end{equation}
We refer to species appearing in Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq} with $\sigma^{-}_{xr}>0$, $\sigma^{+}_{xr}>0$, and $\kappa_{xr}>0$ as 
F-reactants, F-products, and F-catalysts, respectively, and collect them in the following sets:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\feducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^-_{xr} > 0\} \\
		\froducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^+_{xr} > 0\} \\
		\cat(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \kappa_{xr} > 0\}
	\end{split}
\end{equation} 
As shown in \cite{bridging_Golnik_2026} we have:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\hat{\educts}(r) \subseteq \feducts(r) \subseteq \educts(r) \\
		\hat{\products}(r) \subseteq \froducts(r) \subseteq \products(r) \\
		\cat(r) \subseteq \feducts\cap \froducts(r) \subseteq \educts(r)\cap \products(r)\\
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
We may, additionally, observe that $\hat{\feducts}(r) = \feducts(\hat{r}) = \educts(\hat{r}) = \hat{\educts}(r)$ and 
$\hat{\froducts}(r) = \froducts(\hat{r}) = \products(\hat{r}) = \hat{\products}(r)$, which motivates to 
introduce the following relation:
\begin{equation}
	r_1\sim r_2 \Leftrightarrow \SM_{xr_1}=\SM_{xr_2}, \forall x\in X
\end{equation}
It is readily verified that $\sim$ is indeed an equivalence relationship. The equivalence class $[r]_{\sim}$ thereby collects 
all stoichiometric equations that share the same net reaction:
\begin{equation}
	[r]_{\sim} = \left\{r':  \sum_{x\in X}\ (\sigma^-_{xr} + \kappa_{xr}) \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ (\sigma^+_{xr} + 
	\kappa_{xr}) \cdot x \ \middle\vert \  \hat{s}^{\pm}_{xr'} = \hat{s}^{\pm}_{xr}\right\}
\end{equation}
Each equivalence class $[r]_{\sim}$ therefore has a natural representative, i.e., $\hat{r}$. Different representatives of 
the same equivalence class may have different or multiple synergistically acting catalysts \cite{}. For example, the 
hydrogenation of ethene to ethane can be mediated by platinum, palladium, or nickel separately. A famous example 
for co-catalysis, i.e., where the orchestrated cooperation of two catalysts is required, is the dual-catalyst system of 
palladium and copper in the Hoechst-Wacker process, where ethene is oxidized to acetaldehyde \cite{}. The set of 
catalysts may also be empty, i.e., for $\cat({\hat{r}})=\emptyset$ by definition, or if the reaction is uncatalyzed. In
accordance, the catalysts of an equivalence class $[r]_{\sim}$ is a collection $\cat([r]_{\sim})=\{\cat(r') \ | \ r'\in [r]_{\sim}\}$. 

A simple observation is that $\cat(r) \neq \cat(r') \Rightarrow r\neq r'$. However, in general, the converse is true, 
since $r,r'\in [r]_{\sim}$ may be distinct due to $\educts(r)\neq \educts(r')$ or $\products(r)\neq \products(r')$. We thus
can state a first result:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $r,r'\in [r]_{\sim}$. Then $r=r'$ if and only if $\educts(r)=\educts(r'), \products(r)= \products(r')$, and $
	\cat(r) = \cat(r')$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

We denote by $\cat(R)\coloneqq\{(\cat(r),r) \ | \ r\in R, \cat(r) \neq\emptyset\}$ the set of \emph{catalyzations}. In case all reactions 
are $r\in R$ are uncatalyzed, $\cat(R)=\emptyset$. The triple $(X,R,\cat)$ with $\cat=\cat(R)\neq\emptyset$ is called a partially catalyzed 
RN. If $|\cat| = |R|$, i.e., each reaction is catalyzed, we call $(X,R,\cat)$ a catalyzed RN.

\section{The partial order of RAFs}

Property $ii.$ of Def.~\ref{def:RAF}, together with Eq.\ref{eq:Closure}, directly 
implies that all products for any reaction $r\in \RAF$ are also contained 
in the closure of $F'$, thus $\products_r\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{F}$. 
A closed RAF \emph{(CAF)} is a RAF $\RAF$ where the reactions 
can be ordered $r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_k$ such that, for each $i \geq 1$, each 
reactant of $r_i$ and at least one catalyst of $r_i$ is contained in the food set 
and/or is a product of an earlier reaction in the sequence. 

Before connecting the notion of a RAF with the definition of an autocatalytic subnetwork, we start by translating specific properties of a RAF 
into the bipartite K\"onig graph. We begin by examining the consequences of the closure. The closure of a set $X'$ w.r.t. to a set $R'$ denoted 
as $\closure{R'}{X'}$ is the unique minimal set $Y$ containing $X'$ such that for any $r\in R': \educts(r)\subseteq Y \Rightarrow \products(r)\subseteq Y$. Thus:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:Closure}
	\closure{R'}{X'} \coloneqq \min \underbrace{\{ Z \subseteq X \ | \ X'\subseteq Z, \educts(r) \subseteq Z \Rightarrow \products(r) \subseteq Z \ \forall r\in R'\}}_{\eqqcolon \mathfrak{C}_{R'}(X')}
\end{equation}

However, it will be convenient to obtain the set $\closure{R'}{X'}$ iteratively. We therefore define:

\begin{equation}
	\cl_{R'}: \mathfrak{P}(X) \rightarrow \mathfrak{P}(X), \cl_{R'}(Z) \coloneqq Z \cup \{\products(r) \subseteq Z \ \vert \ \educts(r)\subseteq Z\}. 
\end{equation}
\vspace{6pt}

We will write $\cl(X')$ instead of $\cl_{R'}(X')$ as well as $\closure{}{X'}$ instead of $\closure{R'}{X'}$ where appropriate. 
It is indeed not obvious that the repetitive iteration of $\cl_{R'}(X')$ yields $\closure{R'}{X'}$. However, 
recall that an RN is composed of two finite-sized sets, i.e., $|X|<\infty$ and $|R|<\infty$. By definition, the composition 
$\cl\circ \cl$ is well defined. Moreover, we let $\cl^n(X')\coloneqq \left(\bigcirc^n_{i=0} \ \cl \right)(X')$, i.e., $\cl^0(X')\coloneqq X'$. 
The intuitive assumption that a repetitive application of $\cl$ on a set $X'$ actually converges is indeed true.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:finSize}
	Let $(X,R)$ be a reaction network. For a set $X'\subseteq X$ and $R'\subseteq R$ there 
	is an $n\in \mathbb{N}$ such that: 
	\begin{equation}
		\cl^n(X') = \cl^m (X'), m\geq n, m\in \mathbb{N}
	\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case. Then in each $k$-th step 
	\begin{equation}
		|\cl^k(X')| < |\cl^{k+1}(X')|,
	\end{equation}
	which implies that
	\begin{equation}
		|\cl^k(X')| \overset{k\rightarrow\infty}{\longrightarrow}{\infty}	
	\end{equation}
	a contradiction given that $|X|<\infty$.
\end{proof}

The sequence $(\cl^k(X'))_{k\in \mathbb{N}}$ thereby converges, and it remains to show that for the limit it holds
$\lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \cl^k(X') = \closure{}{X'}$. By definition, for any $k\in\mathbb{N}: \cl^k(X')\subseteq \closure{}{X'}$. 
In addition, the convergence of $\cl^k(X')$ gurantees that $\lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \cl^k(X')\in \mathfrak{C}(X')$. Thus by 
minimality $\closure{}{X'}\subseteq \lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \cl^k(X')$, which yields by mutual inclusion that 
$\lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \cl^k(X') = \closure{}{X'}$. With the uniqueness of the limit, the subsequent corollary follows.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:seqLimit}
	Let $(X,R,\cat)$ be a catalyzed network of finite size with $X'\subseteq X, R'\subseteq R$. Then $\closure{R'}{X'}$ 
	is the unique limit of the sequence
	\begin{equation}
		\left(\left(\bigcirc_{i=1}^k \ \cl_{R'}\right)(X')\right)_{k\in\mathbb{N}}.
	\end{equation}
	In addition, there is an $n\in\mathbb{N}: \cl^n_{R'}(X')=\cl^m_{R'}(X'), m\geq n$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
By definition of $\cl_{R'}$, the sequence $(\cl_{R'}^k(X')_{k\in\mathbb{N}}$ directly implies a partial order on the reactions 
that can be carried out in the $k$-th step, i.e., for which all reactants are contained in the set $\cl^k_{R'}(X')$. We thereby 
let:
\begin{equation}
	\PO_{R'}^k(X') \coloneqq \{r\in R' \ | \ \educts(r)\subseteq \cl^k_{R'}(X')\}
\end{equation}

For both $\cl^k_{R'}(X)$ and $\PO_{R'}^k(X')$, it might be convenient to consider their partial order as:
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\POX_{R'}^0(X')& \coloneqq X' \qquad \POX_{R'}^k(X')\coloneqq \cl^k_{R'}(X)\setminus \left(\bigcup_{i=0}^{k-1} \cl^k_{R'}(X) \right)\\
		\POR_{R'}^0(X') & \coloneqq \emptyset \qquad \POR_{R'}^k(X')\coloneqq \PO^k_{R'}(X) \left(\bigcup_{i=0}^{k-1} \PO^k_{R'}(X) \right)
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
In addition, we define two maps that determine the generation of the first appearance of a metabolite or a reaction. 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gen}
	\begin{split}
		g&: X' \rightarrow \mathbb{N}, g(x)\coloneqq k \quad \text{ s. t. } x\in \POX_{R'}^k(X') \\
		h&: R' \rightarrow \mathbb{N}, h(r)\coloneqq k \quad \text{ s. t. } r\in \POR_{R'}^k(X') 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
We refer to the values of $g$ and $h$ as the generation of a reaction or metabolite. We are now in the position to 
deduce the consequences of Def.~\ref{def:RAF}ii. to the bipartite K\"onig representation of a RAF. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:RAFPath}
	Let $(X,R,\cat)$ be a catalyzed network with $F\subseteq X$ and $\RAF$ an RAF. Then, 
	for any $x\in \closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F: \exists f\in F: P=(f, \ldots, x)$ is a path in 
	$\king'\coloneqq \king(\closure{\RAF}{F}\cup F, R', \cat')$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $x\in \closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F$. By Corollary~\ref{cor:seqLimit} we have for some $n\in\mathbb{N}$ that
	$\closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F = \cl^n_{\RAF}(F) \setminus F$. Thus, there is the sequence:
	\begin{equation}
		(\mathscr{Z}^k_{\RAF}(F))_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\coloneqq (\cl^{k-1}_{\RAF}(F), \PO^k_{\RAF}(F))_{n\in\mathbb{N}}
	\end{equation}
	such that for any $r\in \PO^k_{\RAF}(F)$ there is $y\in \cl^{k-1}_{\RAF}(F), z\in\cl^{k}_{\RAF}(F): (y,r)\in E_1(\king'), (r,z)\in E_2(\king')$. 
	Thus, starting with $x\in \cl^{n}_{\RAF}(F)$ and recursively applying the upper argument, there is a sequence of vertices $
	(x_0, r_1, x_1, \ldots, r_n, x_n): (x_{i-1},r_i)\in E_1(\king'), (r_i,x_i)\in E_2(\king'), 1\leq i\leq n, x=x_n$ that is a path 
	in $\king'$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

\section{Minimal RAFs}

A question, which naturally arises for a given RAF $\RAF$, is whether there is an $\RAF'\subset \RAF$ that is itself a RAF. This 
notion has been termed \emph{irreducible} RAFs in the literature \cite{hordijk_detecting_2004, hordijk_autocatalytic_2013, 
hordijk_autocatalytic_2010}. However, to avoid confusion with the notion of irreducibility of a matrix, we refer to them as 
\emph{minimal RAFs}. We shall see that the minimality condition yields a number of interesting structural consequences. The
first is an obvious but important result.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $(X,R,\cat)$ be a catalyzed RN with food-set $F$ and $\RAF$ be a RAF. For $r',r'' \in \RAF$ with $r',r'' \in[r']_{\simeq}, r'\neq r''$ 
	it holds that $\RAF\setminus \{r'\}$ is a RAF and $\RAF\setminus \{r''\}$ is a RAF.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $\RAF'\coloneqq \RAF \setminus \{r'\}$ and $\RAF''\coloneqq \RAF \setminus \{r''\}$. Since $\educts(r')=\educts(r'')$ and 
	$\products(r')=\products(r'')$ we observe that $\closure{\RAF}{F}= \closure{\RAF'}{F}=\closure{\RAF''}{F}$. With $\RAF$ 
	being a RAF, both $\RAF'$ and $\RAF''$ are RAFs.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt} 
A direct consequence of this Lemma is that only one representative of each equivalence class can be part of a RAF:
\vspace{6pt} 
\begin{corollary}
	Let $(X,R,\cat)$ be a catalyzed RN with food-set $F$ and $\RAF$ be a minimal RAF. For $r',r'' \in[r']_{\sim}, r'\neq r''$ 
	$r'\in \RAF\Rightarrow r''\notin \RAF$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt} 
This allows us to CRNS that contain only one representative for each equivalence class of a minimal RAF.
Before venturing forth, we give an example of a non-minimal RAF:\\

Indeed, $\RAF=\{r_1\}$ is a RAF, and thus, any addition of $r_1$ or $r_2$ would violate minimality. We call any 
RAF of size $1$ a \emph{trivial} RAF. At this point, we note that for catalyzed reaction networks, where 
each reaction is catalyzed by at most one catalyst, we get the following statement. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $(X,R,\cat)$ be a catalyzed reaction network with food-set $F$ such that $|\cat_{r}|\leq 1, \forall r\in R$. Then every minimal
	CAF is trivial. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $\RAF$ be a CAF and assume $|\RAF|>1$. Let $r\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$. Then all reactants and catalysts are for $r$ are in 
	$F$ since $\RAF$ is a CAF. Thus $\{r\}$ is a RAF itself, contradicting the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

An example for a trivial CAF is $\{r_0\}$ in the last example. 

Interestingly, the minimality condition has a series of interesting consequences on the structure of the K\"onig representation for non-trivial 
RAF when all food molecules and all species exhibiting no outgoing edges are removed. We start with the following trivial observation, 
which is a direct consequence of Lemma~\ref{lem:RAFPath}.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:weaklyConnected}
	A $(X, R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ be a minimal RAF. 
	Then $\king[\closure{\RAF}{F}, \RAF]$ is weakly-connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case. Then we have at least two connected components $\king', \king''$. By assumption both $X(\king')$ 
	and $X(\king'')$ are $F$-generated. Since all reactions in $\RAF$ are catalyzed, every reaction in $R(\king')$ and every reaction 
	in $R(\king'')$ is catalyzed. Since there is no path from any $x'\in X(\king')$ to any $r''\in R(\king'')$ and no path from 
	any $x''\in X(\king'')$ to any $r'\in R(\king')$, every reaction $r'\in R(\king')$ is catalyzed by an entity of $x'\in X(\king')$ and every reaction
	of $r''\in R(\king')$ is catalyzed by an entity of $x''\in X(\king'')$. Thus $R(\king')$ and $R(\king'')$ are RAFs themselves and since
	$R(\king'), R(\king'')\subseteq \RAF$, $\RAF$ was not a minimal RAF, thus a contradiction.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
A minimal RAF, thereby, has a corresponding weakly-connected bipartite K\"onig graph. 
We note that the upper result not only holds for the graph 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:graphG}
	G\coloneqq \king[\closure{\RAF}{F}, \RAF],
\end{equation}
but also for the graph 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:graphH}
	H\coloneqq \king[\closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F, \RAF]
\end{equation} 
where all food-vertices have been removed.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:weaklyConnected2}
	A $(X,R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ be a minimal RAF. Then 
	$H\coloneqq \king[\closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F, \RAF]$ is weakly-connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	By Lemma~\ref{lem:weaklyConnected} $G\coloneqq \king[\closure{\RAF}{F}, \RAF]$ is weakly-connected. 
	Assume now there is an $f\in F$ and $u,v\in \closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F$ and such $G[V(G)\setminus \{f\}]$
	is composed of at least two connected components $C_1, C_2$. Then $\closure{R(C_1)}{F}\cap \closure{R(C_2)}{F} 
	\subseteq F$, i.e., none of the species $x_1\in X(C_1)$ is a reactant, product, or catalyst for a reaction $r_2\in R(C_2)$ 
	and vice-versa. Thus, $R(C_1), R(C_2)$ are both RAFs and $\RAF$ is not minimal,  a contradiction.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
If a minimal RAF is non-trivial, i.e., if it is composed of more than one reaction, the graph $H$ 
as defined in Lemma~\ref{lem:weaklyConnected2}, is more than a sequence of reactions. The following 
lemmata allow us to gain more insight into the organization of minimal, non-trivial RAFs.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $(X,R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ be a minimal, non-trivial RAF. Then 
	for every $r\in \RAF$ there is an $x\in \closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F: s^-_{xr}>0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case for an $r\in \RAF$. Then $\{r\}$ is a RAF and thus $\RAF$ was not minimal.
\end{proof}

We next investigate the cyclicity of $G$ and $H$. Since all food-molecules are absent in $G$ and $H$, we first 
obtain that all vertices in $H$ have an in-degree greater than $0$. A simple result from 
\cite{bang-jensen_digraphs_2002} then yields:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	$H$ contains an elementary circuit. 
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

This was already shown in \cite{steel_minimal_2013}, however, not for the bipartite but for the R-graph. Since addition 
of the food molecules does not destroy this property, we also obtain:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	$G$ contains an elementary circuit. 
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

For minimal RAFs this result can be strengthened. To this end, we define the descendants and ascendants of a vertex $v$ in a graph 
in the following way

\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		D(v) & \coloneqq \{u \in V \ | \text{ exists a path } P=(v,\ldots, u)  \} \\
		A(v) & \coloneqq \{u \in V \ | \text{ exists a path } P=(u,\ldots, v)  \} \\
	\end{split}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}
	Let $\RAF$ be a minimal, non-trivial RAF. Then every reaction in $r\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$ 
	that is located on an elementary circuit that is not a digon in $G$ and $H$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume this is not the case. Then there is a reaction $r_1\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$, such that 
	all reactants and catalysts are in $F$. The $\{r_1\}$ is a RAF which directly contradicts 
	the minimality of $\RAF$. Since $r_1\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$ are in $F$, there is 
	$c_1\in \cat_{r_1}: c_1\not in F$. Then $c_1\in D(r_2), r_2\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$, thus 
	$r_1\in D(r_2)$. Now, we apply the same argument as above for $r_2$. If $\cat_{r_2}\subseteq F$,
	the $\{r_2\}$ is a $\RAF$ contradicting minimality. Thus there is $c_2\in \cat_{r_2}: c_2\notin F$. 
	Then there is an $r_3\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$ such that $c_2\in D(r_3)$. We repeat the arguments
	until we reach an $r_l$ such that $r_l=r_k$ for some $k<l$, thus we reach a reaction again, which 
	is guaranteed since $\RAF$ is finite. Then all reactions $r_k, \ldots, r_{l-1}$ are located on an 
	elementary circuit. If $l>1$ then $\RAF'\coloneqq \RAF\setminus (D(r_1)\cup\{r_1\})$ is a $\RAF$.
	Again, we observe that $\RAF'\neq \emptyset$ since it contains the reactions $r_k,\ldots, r_{l-1}$.
	Assume this is not the case. Since $\RAF'\subseteq \RAF$, it is reflexively autocatalytic. Then 
	there is again a reaction $r'\in \RAF'$ for which a reactant or a catalyst $x$ is not in $\closure{\RAF'}(F)$. 
	Since $x\in \closure{\RAF}(F)$ then $x\in D(r_1)$. Then $r'\in D(r_1)$ a contradiction. Thus, $\RAF'$ is
	a RAF, and $\RAF$ was not minimal. Adding the food molecules to $H$ does not change the arguments,
	thus it holds also for $G$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

We are now in a position to prove an important result of this section.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:elemCirc}
	Let $(X,R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ be a minimal, non-trivial RAF. Then 
	every $r\in \RAF$ is part of an elementary circuit that is not a digon in $G$ and $H$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Again, we start with the graph $H$. We know that each $r_1\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$ is 
	is located on an elementary circuit. Assume now there is a reaction $r\in \RAF$ that is 
	not located on an elementary circuit. We now show that $\RAF' \coloneqq \RAF\setminus 
	(R(D(r))\cup \{r\}$ is a RAF. First, we observe that $\RAF'\neq \emptyset$, since $\POR^1_{\RAF}(F) 
	\subseteq \RAF'$. Otherwise, $r$ would be located on an elementary circuit. Therefore, 
	assume $\RAF'$ is not a $\RAF$. Since $\RAF$ is reflexively autocatalytic and 
	$\RAF'\subseteq \RAF$, $\RAF'$ is reflexively autocatalytic. Then, there is a reaction 
	$r'\in \RAF'$ for which a reactant or a catalyst $x\in X(H)$ with $s^-_{xr'}>0$ it holds that 
	$x\notin \closure{\RAF'}(F)$. Since $x\in \closure{\RAF}(F)$ is a product of some reaction 
	$r''\in R(D(R'))\cup \{r'\}$. However, then $r''\in R(D(R'))\cup \{r'\}$, which is a contradiction. 
	Thus, $\RAF'$ is a RAF and therefore $\RAF$ was not minimal. Adding the food molecules 
	to $H$ does not change the arguments, thus the result also holds for $G$. 		
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

We say that two elementary circuits $C_1, C_2$ are 1-connected from $C_1$ to $C_2$ if there are vertices $u\in V(C_1)$ 
and $v\in V(C_2)$ such that there is a path $P_{uv}$ connecting them. Since elementary circuits are strongly connected, 
if $C_1$ and $C_2$ are 1-connected from $C_1$ to $C_2$ there is a path $P_{uv}$ connecting any two vertices $u\in V(C_1)$ 
and $v\in V(C_2)$. We say two elementary circuits are strongly connected if they are 1-connected from $C_1$ to $C_2$ and 
from $C_2$ to $C_1$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:strElCirc}
	Let $(X,R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ a minimal, non-trivial RAF. Any two elementary circuits $C_1,C_2$ 
	in $G \ (H)$ are strongly connected. 
\end{lemma}
	\begin{proof}
		We start again with $G$ and reaction vertices in $\POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$. Assume the trivial case $|\POR^1_{\RAF}(F)|=1$. According to 
		Lemma~\ref{lem:elemCirc}, $r^*\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$ is located on an elementary circuit $C^*$. Assume there is an elementary
		circuit $C\neq C^*$ such that $C^*$ is not strongly-connected with $C$. Since $G$ is weakly-connected and $|\POR^1_{\RAF}(F)|=1$, 
		$C^*$ is $1$-connected to $C$. If $C$ is not $1$-connected to $C^*$ then $\RAF\setminus R(C)$ is a RAF thus $\RAF$ not minimal. 
		Now assume that $|\POR^1_{\RAF}(F)|>1$ and let $r_1,r_2\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$. Then $r_1,r_2$ are located on two elementary circuits
		$C_1$ and $C_2$. If $C_1=C_2$, there is nothing to show. Thus, assume $C_1\neq C_2$. If $C_1$ is not 1-connected to $C_2$ and 
		vice versa, then $\RAF\setminus R(C_2)$ is a RAF and $\RAF\setminus R(C_2)$ is a RAF which contradicts minimality of $\RAF$. So 
		assume that $C_1$ is 1-connected to $C_2$, but $C_2$ is not $1$-connected to $C_1$. Then $\RAF\setminus R(C_2)$ is a $\RAF$ 
		contradicting minimality. Substituting the roles of $C_1$ and $C_2$ yields that $C_1$ and $C_2$ are strongly connected. Together
		with the trivial case, this implies that any two elementary circuits are strongly connected. The same arguments hold for $H$.
	\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:CutVertexReac}
	Let $(X,R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed network with food-set $F$, and $\RAF$ 
	be a minimal, non-trivial RAF. If $L$ is not a strong block, then every 
	cut-vertex is a reaction vertex.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Assume there is a cut-vertex $w\in X(G)$. Then $L[V(L)\setminus \{w\}]$ is composed of at least two connected
	components $C_1,C_2$. Then for any two vertices $v\in C_1$ and $u\in C_2$ we have $w\in P_{uv}$ and 
	$w\in P_{vu}$ for any path connecting $u$ and $v$. Therefore, there is no $x_1\in X(C_1)$ such that $x_1$ is 
	a reactant, product, or catalyst for any reaction $r_2\in R(C_2)$, and there is no $x_2\in X(C_2)$ such that $x_2$ 
	is a reactant, product, or catalyst for any reaction $r_1\in R(C_1)$. Then $\cl_{R(C_1)}(F)\neq \emptyset$, implying
	that $R(C_1)$ is a RAF or (not mutually exclusive) $\cl_{R(C_2)}(F)\neq \emptyset$, implying that $R(C_2)$ is a RAF, 
	in any case, contradicting the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}	
\vspace{6pt}

This result allows us to decompose $L$ into strong blocks. We will see in the next section that each of these strong blocks 
indeed contains an autocatalytic core. Here, however, we continue by stating a necessary condition for a reaction to be a 
cut-verex.
\vspace{6pt}

\begin{lemma}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, minimal RAF. If $r\in R(L)$ is a cut-vertex, then $r$ has at least two incoming and two outgoing edges.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	We denote the two parts of $L$ separated by $r$ by $L_1$ and $L_2$. Assume now that $r$ has 
	only one incoming edge. Then there can only be one catalyst $c_r$, which is also the only net reactant. 
	Assume w.l.o.g. that $c_r \in V(L_1)$. Then, however, there is no path from any $u\in V(L_2)$ to any 
	$v\in V(L_1)$. Thus, $L$ is not strongly connected, and thereby $\RAF$ is not minimal. Assume now 
	that $r$ has only one outgoing edge. Then it has only one catalyst $c_r$ again, which is the net 
	product of $r$. Assume again w.l.o.g. that $c_r\in V(L_2)$. Then, there is again no path from any 
	$u\in V(L_2)$ to any $v\in V(L_1)$ which implies that $L$ was not strongly connected and thus $\RAF$ 
	not minimal.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
Monomolecular reaction systems, i.e., those that have only one net reactant and one net 
product, thereby contain no cut-vertices:
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial minimal RAF. If each $r\in \RAF$ has only one net reactant
	and one net product, then $L$ is a strong block.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
We continue by identifying a structural property common to any strong block that warrants semipositivity. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:cutVertexPureCat}
	Any strong block of $L$ contains a reaction vertex $r\in \RAF$ that has only \emph{pure} catalysts as reactants.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	We start with strong blocks $B$ which contain a reaction vertex $r\in \POR^1_{\RAF}(F)$. The net reactants of any of these 
	reactions are subsets of $F$. Since $L[B]$ is strongly-connected, it has at least one \emph{pure} catalyst as reactant. Any 
	other strong block $B'$ contains a cut-vertex $r^*\in \RAF$. We start with the simple case in which $B'$ contains only one cut-vertex. 
	With $h(r^*)$ as defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:Gen} we observe that $h(r^*)< h(r), \ \forall \ r\in R(B')\setminus \{r^*\}$. Since $L[B']$ is 
	strongly connected, it has at least one reactant and one product. Assume a reactant $x\in X(B')$ 
	is a net reactant. This, however, implies that $x$ is a product of a reaction $r\in R(S): h(r)< h(r^*)$, a contradiction. Assume 
	now that $B'$ has cut-vertices: $r^*_1, \ldots, r^*_k$. Again, we observe, that there is an $r^*_j: h(r^*_j)< h(r), \forall \ r\in R(B')
	\setminus \{r^*_j\}$. Assume now that every cut-vertex has a net reactant $x_i\in X(S')$. Then each $x_i$ is a product of some reaction 
	in $r_i$, and it follows that $h(r_i)<g(r^*_i)$. This contradicts the previous observation, and the claim follows.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

A direct consequence of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} is that the stoichiometric submatrix reflecting the strong block $B$, 
always contains a non-negative column. This column is induced by a reaction $r^*\in R(B)$ that has only catalysts as reactants 
in $B$ and at least one positive entry for a product $y^*\in X(B): s^+_{yr^*}>0$:

\begin{equation}
	\SM[X(B), R(B)] = \begin{pmatrix}
						& 	\ldots 	& 	r^* 		& \ldots \\
						& 	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
					x^* 	& 	\ldots 	& 	0 		& \ldots \\
						& 	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
					y^* 	& 	\ldots 	& 	s^+_{y^*r^*} & \ldots \\
						& 	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
	\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

Cut-vertices with only catalysts as reactants play an important role in the partial order of a strong block. To this 
end, we define the set of \emph{pure reactions} by 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pureReactions}
	R^*(B)\coloneqq \{r\in R(B) \ | \ s^-_{xr}>0 \Rightarrow s^+_{xr}=s^-_{xr}, \forall x\in X(B)\}
\end{equation}
In particular, there is always at least one \emph{pure reaction} that has the lowest generation number of all 
reactions within the block. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:genPureReacs}
	Let $B$ be a strong block of $L$. Then there is $r^*\in R(B*): g(r^*)\leq g(r), \forall~r\in R(B)$ and 
	$g(r^*)<g(r), \forall r\in R(B)\setminus R^*(B)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Let $B$ be a strong block of $L$. We choose an $r^*\in R^*(B)$ and assume $|R^*(B)|=1$. 
	Assume now there is an $r\in R(B)\setminus \{r^*\} : h(r) < h(r^*)$. Then $r$ has a net-reactant 
	$x \in X(B)$ since $B$. Thus, there is a reaction $r'$ that has $x$ as a net-product, which implies 
	$h(r')< h(r)$. If $r'$ has a net-reactant $x'\in X(B)$, the same argument holds for $r'$ and, i.e., for 
	all reactions that have a net-reactant in $B$, a contradiction since $B$ is finite. Thus $h(r*)<h(r), 
	\forall r\in R(B)\setminus \{r*\}$. Assume now $|R^*(B)|>1$. We may then chose $r^*\in R^*(B): 
	g(r^*)\leq g(r') \forall r'\in R^*(B)$. Assume now there is $r\in R(B): h(r)<h(r^*)$. Then $r \notin R^*(B)$ 
	due to the choice of $r^*$. For $r\in R(B)\setminus R^*(B)$, we apply the upper argument and obtain 
	the same contradiction as above.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
Any pure reaction vertex is located on an elementary circuit. These circuits may have metabolite-to-reaction,
so-called MR-, chords. However, in each strong block, there is always one elementary circuit containing 
a pure reaction that is MR-chordfree. For brevity of presentation, the \emph{canonical} CS of an elementary 
circuit is defined as follows $\child(C)\coloneqq \{X(C), R(C), \kappa: \kappa(x)\coloneqq v^C_{out}\}$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $B$ be a strong block of $L$. Then $B$ has an elementary circuit $C$ with $r\in R(C): r\in R^*(B)$ and
	$\SM[\child]$ is a Metzler matrix.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	We start with the simple case, i.e., $|R^*(B)|=1$, and show by construction. For $r_1\in R^*(B)$ we have
	by Lemma~\ref{lem:genPureReacs} that $h(r_1)<h(r'), \forall r'\in R(B)\setminus r_1$. We may then choose 
	a catalyst of $r_1$ as $x\in X(\cat_{r_1}): g(x)\leq g(x'), x\in X(\cat_{r_1})$ since they are the only reactants
	of $r_1$. In every $k$-th step we may then choose, for $x_k$ the reaction $r_{k+1}\in P(x_k)\coloneqq \{r \in R(B) \ 
	| \ s^+_{x_k,r}-s^-_{x_k,r}>0\}$ such that $h(r_{k+1})\leq h(r_l), r_l\in P(x_k)$ and $x_{k+1}\in \educts(r_{k+r}) \cup 
	X(\cat_{r_{k+1}}): g(x_{k+1})\leq g(x_l), x_l \in \educts(r_{k+1}) \cup X(\cat_{r_{k+1}})$. In this way, we obtain 
	\begin{equation}
		h(x_k) \leq g(r_{k+1}) \leq h(x_k), \forall k\geq 0
	\end{equation}
	and therefore two strict monotone decreasing sequences of $g(x_i), i\geq 1$ and $h(r_j), j\geq 2$. Thus, no 
	vertex is visited twice, and after a maximum of $2\cdot \min(|X(B)|, |R(B)|)$ vertices, we reach $r_1$ again. Assume there 
	is an MR-edge $(x_i,r_j)$ with $i>j$. Then $g(x_i)>h(r_j)$, which implies that $x_i$ is a pure catalysts for $r_j$.

If the strong block consists of a single reaction $r$ and a single species $x \in \cat_r$, then $r$ is an explicit autocatalytic reaction.

\begin{lemma}

	$B$ to $B'$ since otherwise $x$ would be a cut-vertex, which is not possible by Lemma~\ref{lem:CutVertexReac}. Then 
	$r$ is an explicit autocatalytic reaction. 
\end{proof}

\section{RAFs and autocatalytic subnetworks}

We continue by connecting the notion of RAFs to autocatalysis sensu Blokhuis \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}, which  
provided the widely accepted definition of (structural) autocatalysis in 2020.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}\label{def:autSubRN}
  An RN $(X,R)$ is \emph{autocatalytic} if it contains a sub-RN $(X',R')$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
 \item[(i)]  $(X',R')$ is  well-formed;
 \item[(ii)]  $\mathbf{S}[X',R']$ is semi-positive.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

Our first goal is to find suitable restrictions of RAFs that satisfy both conditions. Indeed, restricting the 
set of considered molecules to all but the food molecules yields semipositivity of the particular submatrix.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:NonFoodSemipositiv}
	Let $(X,R)$ be a catalyzed RN with food-set $F$ and $\RAF$ a RAF. Then it holds that 
	the matrix $A\coloneqq \SM[\closure{\RAF}{F}\setminus F,\RAF]$ is semipositive, i.e., $Av>0$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	We prove by induction. We know that for some $n\in \mathbb{N}: \cl^n_{\RAF}(F)=\closure{\RAF}{F}\eqqcolon X'$. 
	We observe that for an $x\in \POX^k\coloneqq \POX^k_{\RAF}(F)$, $\SM_{xr}=0$ for any 
	$r\in \POR^l \coloneqq \POR^l_{\RAF}(F), l<k \ (*)$. We initialize a map: $f: X' \rightarrow \mathbb{R}, f(x)\coloneqq 0$, 
	that will be updated in each step. Thus, we start with the set $\POR^n$. For each an $r\in \POR^n$, we let 
	$v_r\coloneqq 1$, and update for each $x\in X'$:
	\begin{equation}
		f(x) \leftarrow f(x) + \sum_{r\in \POR^n} v_r \cdot \SM_{xr}
	\end{equation}
	then for all $x\in \POX^n: f(x)>0$. 
	
	Assume now that for some $1\leq k\leq n$ it holds that $f(x)>0, \forall x\in \POX^k$. Then, for $k-1$
	we choose an arbitrary ordering of $r\in \POR^{k-1}$, since each $x\in \POX^{k-1}$ can only be 
	a product of reactions in $\POR^{k-1}$ but never be a reactant. We let:
	\begin{equation}
		v_r\coloneqq \left\vert \min_{x\in \POX^{k-1}} \left(\frac{f(x)}{s}\right)\right \vert + 1, s\coloneqq \begin{cases}\SM_{xr} & \text{if } \SM_{xr}\neq 0 \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
	\end{equation}
	Then we update again for each $x\in X'$:
	\begin{equation}
		f(x) \leftarrow f(x) + \sum_{r\in \POR^n} v_r \cdot \SM_{xr}
	\end{equation}	
	 and obtain again that forall $x\in \POX^{k-1}: f(x)>0$ and with observation $*$ that also $f(x)>0, \in \POX^{\ell}: f(x)>0, k-1\leq \ell \leq n$.
	 Then by construction, we obtain $v_r$ is strictly positive and $Av>0$. 
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

Every species of a strong block is only produced and consumed by a reaction of that block. Thereby, the 
vector $v|_{R(C)}$ that denotes the sub-vector of $v$ restricted to the reactions of the strong block $C$ 
directly yields for the submatrix restricted to the species and reactions of the strong block: 
\begin{equation}
  \SM[X(C), R(C)]v|_{R(C)}>0
\end{equation}
and thereby semipositivity. In addition, any strong block is by definition well-formed, and we obtain
the following statement.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{Thm:StrBlockCore}
	Any strong block of a minimal RAF contains an autocatalytic core. 
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

Thm~\ref{Thm:StrBlockCore} provides only information on the existence, but not on the structure of the 
subnetworks responsible for the semipositivity of the corresponding submatrix. Leveraging on the result 
of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat}. Recall, therefore, that for any strong block $B$, there is a reaction
vertex $r^*\in R(B)$ such that for all $x\in X(B)$ with $s_{xr^*}^->0$ it holds that $s_{xr^*}^+=s_{xr^*}^->0$, i.e.

submatrix $\SM[X(B), R(B)]$. However, this alone is not sufficient to warrant semipositivity, neither for any CS 
matrix nor for a CS induced by an elementary circuit inside the strong block containing a \emph{pure reaction}.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $B$ be a strong block of the graph $L$ and $C$ be an elementary circuit $C$ in $B$ with 
	associated CS $\child$ that is defined by the edges $E_1(C)$. In addition, let $C$ contain a  
	\emph{pure reaction} (Eq.~\ref{eq:pureReactions}). Then $\SM[\child]$ is autocatalytic if it is 
	a Metzler matrix. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	If $C$ is an elementary circuit its associated CS matrix $\SM[\child]$ of the $k$-CS 
	$\child = (X(C), R(C), \kappa: \kappa(x) \coloneqq v^C_{out}(x)$ is irreducible, by pre-requisite 
	Metzler, and reads by construction:
	\begin{equation}
			\SM[\child] = 
				\begin{pmatrix}
						0 	& 	 		&	 			&  			& 	p_1	\\
						p_2	& 	-r_2  		& 				&     P''  		&		\\
					 		& 	p_3  		& 	\ddots 		&      			&		\\
							& 	 		& 	\ddots 		& \ddots 		&    	 	\\
						P'	& 	 		& 				& p_k 		& -r_k     	\\
				\end{pmatrix},
	\end{equation}

i.e., $\SM[\child]$ with $\{r_i\}\in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, i=2,\ldots, k$ and $\{p_i\}\in \mathbb{R}_{>0},i=1,\ldots, k$ and non-negative
entries in $P'$ and $P''$. We may then construct $v\in \mathbb{R}^{k}_{>0}$ as $v(r_k)\coloneqq 1$ and 
\begin{equation}
	v_{i}=\begin{cases}
			\frac{v_{i+1}\cdot r_{i+1}}{p_{i+1}} +1 & \text{if } r_{i+1}>0 \\
			\frac{v_{i+1}}{p_{i+1}} +1 & \text{if } r_{i+1}=0
		\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Then $v>0$ and by construction we directly obtain $\SM[\child]v>0$ since $P'$ and $P''$ contain only non-negative entries. 
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

Note that the statement of the Lemma allows MR-chords that emerge as digons induced by pure catalysts and thereby yield only
$0$ as off-diagonal entries in $\SM[\child]$. A direct consequence is then the following:
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Any MR-chordless elementary circuit of a strong block in $L$ containing a \emph{pure reaction} vertex has an autocatalytic
	CS matrix.  
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

Such elementary circuits, MR-chordfree or not, are neither necessarily autocatalytic cores, nor is their existence guaranteed. 
However, they can be constructed directly, which proves the existence of at least on of them in each strong block.

\section{RAF-Subnetworks}

In this subsection, we aim to generalize the framework of RAFs to a subnetwork setting.

\subsection{Reflexively autocatalytic subnetworks}

We start with the reflexive-autocatalytic part. We call a subnetwork $(X', R')$ partially catalyzed if $\cat(R')\neq \emptyset$. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}
	Let $(X,R)$ be a catalyzed RN. A subnetwork $(X',R')$ is called reflexive-autocatalytic if for each 
	$r\in R': \cat_r\neq \emptyset \ (X(\cat(R')\subseteq X')$. It is called autonomously reflexive-autocatalytic 
	if for each $r\in R': \cat_r\neq \emptyset \ and X(\cat(R')\subseteq X'$.
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}
We denote the subgraph of a subnetwork by $\king'\coloneqq \king(X',R')$ and write $\cat'$ instead of $\cat(R')$. A direct consequence 
is the following lemma:
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:RASubnetwork}
	Let $(X, R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed RN. A subnetwork $(X',R')$ is (autonomously) reflexive-autocatalytic if and only if
	for all $r\in R' \ \exists x\in X: (x,r),(r,x)\in E(\king(X\cup X(\cat'), R') \ (E(\king'))$. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	If $(X',R')$ is reflexive autocatalytic $|R'|=|\cat'|$, thus each reaction $r$ is explicitely catalyzed by an $x_r\in X(\cat_r)\subseteq X$.
	Then $s^-_{x_rr}>0$ and $s^+_{x_rr}>0$ yields by definition $(x_r,r)$ and $(x_r,r) \in E(\king(X\cup X(\cat'), R')$, respectively.
	If $(X',R')$ is autonomously reflexive-autocatalytic then $X(\cat'\subseteq X'\Rightarrow \king(X\cup X(\cat'), R') = \king'$ which
	yields the second part of the claim. Conversely, if for each $r\in R': \exists x\in X$ such that $(x,r),(r,x)\in E(\king(X\cup X(\cat')), R')$, 
	then by definition $s^-_{xr}>0,s^+_{xr}>0$, thus $x\in X(\cat_r)$. If, additionally, $(x,r),(r,x) \in E(\king')$ then $x\in X'$ and thus 
	$X(\cat'\subseteq X'$ which yields that $(X',R')$ is autonomously reflexive-autocatalytic. 
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

\subsection{F-generated subnetworks}

In theory, for an RN $(X,R,\cat)$, $F$ could be chosen arbitrarily. Depending on the network structure and the RAF $R'$, 
$F$ might be identical to its own closure. To see this, consider the following example network: 
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		r_1: x_1 \overset{x_1}{\longrightarrow} x_2 \\
		r_2: x_2 \overset{x_2}{\longrightarrow} x_1 \\
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
with $F=\cat=X=\{x_1,x_2\}$ and RAF $\RAF = \{r_1,r_2\}$. Then $F=\closure{\RAF}{F}$. However, $F$ could also contain reactions 
that are not connected to the rest, depending on the RAF. For example
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		r_1: x_1 &\overset{x_1}{\longrightarrow} x_2 \\
		r_2: x_2 &\overset{x_2}{\longrightarrow} x_1 \\
		r_3: x_3 &\overset{x_3}{\longrightarrow} x_1 
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
with $F=\cat=X=\{x_1,x_2,x_3\}$ and RAF $\RAF=\{r_1,r_2\}$, we have $x_3\in F\Rightarrow F\subseteq \closure{\RAF}{F}$ but $x_3$ is disconnected
in the graph $\king(X(\RAF) \cup F, \RAF, \cat(\RAF))$. In contrast, a food-set $F'$ for a subnetwork $(X', R')$ as defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:FoodWaste} is a direct 
consequence from the set of entities $X'$ and the set of reactions $R'$. 

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}[F-generated subnetwork]
	Let $(X, R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed RN. A subnetwork $(X',R')$ is called $F$-generated, if $X'\subseteq \closure{R'}{F}$.
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

An immediate question arising is then how to construct a minimal set $F$ to obtain an $F$-generated subnetwork. Clearly, we have  
that $F'\subseteq F$ if $(X',R')$ is $F$-generated. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $(X, R, \cat)$ be a catalyzed RN and $(X',R')$ a subnetwork. If $\king[X'\cup R']$ is a fluffle, then $(X',R')$  
	is an $F$-generated subnetwork for any of the sets $F_x\coloneqq F'\cup\{x\}, x\in X'$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	If $\king[X'\cup R']$ is a fluffle, then any reaction has a single reactant in $(X',R')$. In addition, there is a unique CS $\child$ 
	defined by the reactant-to-product edges. For an arbitrary $x'\in X'$ and the set $F'$ we have $E(\kappa(x))\subseteq 
	F_x\coloneqq F'\cup\{x\}$, which yields that $P(\kappa(x))\subseteq \cl_{R'}(F_x)$. For all $y\in P(\kappa(x))$, the same 
	argument applies as well as for all any $z\in \POX^k_{R'}(F), k\leq n$. Since $\king[X'\cup R']$ is a fluffle, it is strongly 
	connected. Thus, for each $r\in R'$ there is path $(x, \kappa(x), \ldots, r)$, and thereby an $k\leq n: r\in \POR_{R'}(F)$. 
	Hence, $X'\subseteq \closure{R'}{F}$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
Since irreducible Metzler CS matrices have a bipartite graph representation as a fluffle, 
the following statement is a direct consequence from the former lemma.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $(X',R')$ be a subnetwork that has an associated autocatalytic, irreducible 
	Metzler CS matrix $\SM[\child]$. Then $(X',R')$ is an $F$-generated
	subnetwork for any $F_x\coloneqq F'\cup \{x\}, x\in X'$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
Any autocatalytic core $(X',R')$ can then be generated by the union of $F'$ and a single species from $X'$. 
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $(X',R')$ be an autocatalytic core. Then $(X',R')$ is an $F$-generated subnetwork for any $F_x\coloneqq F'\cup \{x\}, x\in X'$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
For irreducible non-Metzler matrices, such a result cannot be obtained. In the worst case, 
all but one species might have to be added to $F'$ to obtain an $F$-generated subnetwork. 
To see this, consider the following subnetwork $(X',R')$: 
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		r_1&: x_1 + \epsilon x_3 \rightarrow x_2 \\
		r_2&: x_2 + \epsilon x_1 \rightarrow x_3 \\
		r_3&: x_3 + \epsilon x_2 \rightarrow x_1
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
with corresponding CS matrix:
\begin{equation}
	\SM[\child]=\begin{pmatrix}
				-1 & -\epsilon & 2 \\
				1 & -1 & -\epsilon \\
				-\epsilon & 1 & -1 
			\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
which is autocatalytic for epsilon small enough with $v=\begin{pmatrix}5 & 4 & 3\end{pmatrix}^T$. 
In \cite{golnik_enumeration_2025}, it has been shown that any CS matrix with irreducible Metzler has a 
spanning fluffle $\king(\child)$. Although still strongly connected the induced subgraph $\king[\child]$ 
might contain additional reactant-to-reaction edges that are not encoded by the CS-bijection $\kappa$.
However, it is sufficient to add all species with an off-diagonal entry.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}
	Let $(X', R')$ be a subnetwork with a non-Metzler CS matrix with irreducible Metzler part. Then $(X',R')$
	is $F$-generated for $F= F' \cup N, N=\{x\in X' | \exists r \neq \kappa(x), \SM^-_{xr}>0\}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
	Since $\SM[\child]$ has irreducible Metzler part, $\king(\child)$ is strongly-connected, which implies that
	also $\king[\child]$ is strongly connected. Then for any $x\in N: E(\kappa(x))\in F$, thus $\kappa(x)\in \POR_{R'}(F)$ 
	and $P(\kappa(x))\in \cl_{R'}(F)$. We can now recursively apply this argument to all $y\in P(\kappa(x))$
	and obtain with the strong-connectedness of $\king[\child]$ that $X'\subseteq \closure{R'}(F)$.
\end{proof}
