\section{Introduction}

Wilhelm Ostwald's considerations in 1890 on whether chemical reactions
could be catalyzed by one of their own products \cite{Ostwald:90} marked
the provenance of autocatalysis. In its simplest form, \NV{a}utocatalysis
appears as an autocatalytic reaction where one participating species drives
its own formation, fueled by a sufficient supply of food molecules. In
subsequent decades, \emph{autocatalytic reactions} appeared, either
explicitly or implicitly, in the literature across a wide range of
reaction-network settings, including chemistry, ecology, population
dynamics, and epidemiology \cite{lotka_contribution_1910,
  lotka_analytical_1920, lotka1925elements, volterra1926variazioni,
  kermack_contributions_1991}. One of the most convincing examples is the
transmission of viral infections from one individual to another
\cite{kermack_contributions_1991}, resulting in an exponential explosion of
numbers of infected individuals, e.g., as observed during the COVID-19
pandemic \cite{saxena_propagation_2021}.

From the simple idea of an autocatalytic reaction, a growing field of
research has developed over the past decades, resulting in different
concepts of autocatalysis \cite{andersen_defining_2021}.  A first line of
studies focused on entities driving their own reproduction in complex
networks, so-called \emph{replicators}, such as Eigen's quasi-species, an
ensemble of genotypes that arrange themselves around a common species of
which they differ by a series of mutations
\cite{eigen_selforganization_1971}.  Subsequent research applied this
concept to metabolic networks to identify important metabolic
\emph{autocatalytic replicators} such as ATP
\cite{kun_computational_2008}. In a more general way, sequences of
reactions whose combined net reaction is autocatalytic have been termed
autocatalytic cycles, and shown to be prevalent in the central carbon
metabolism of \emph{Escherichia coli} \cite{barenholz_design_2017,
  smith_universality_2004}. Similar ideas have been studied subsequently
\cite{joshi_autocatalytic_2021}, as they relate closely to the hypercycle
concept originally proposed by Eigen and Schuster
\cite{eigen_principle_1977}.

\PFS{A concise definition of \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} was
  proposed in \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}, as a generalization of the
  idea of an autocatalytic cycle in accordance with the IUPAC definition of
  autocatalytic reactions \cite{gold_iupac_2025}.}  This formalization has
recently received increasing attention \PFS{since its conception in
  2020. It has lead to} the development of algorithms for enumerating
minimal and irreducible autocatalytic subsystems in fully reversible
\cite{kosc_thermodynamic_2025} CRNs and CRNs with reversible and
irreversible reactions \cite{gagrani_polyhedral_2024, golnik_birne_2025,
  golnik_enumeration_2026, golnik_using_2026}.  \emph{Stoichiometric
autocatalysis} was introduced in \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} for fully
reversible CRNs lacking explicit catalysis. Recently, the theory has been
extended to CRNs containing both reversible and irreversible as well as
explicitly catalyzed reactions \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}.

An alternative approach \PFS{to formalize system-level autocatalysis}
focused on systems of chemical reactions in which every reaction is, in
general, catalyzed \cite{lohn_evolving_1998, steel_emergence_2000}. The
original idea dates back to Kauffman's considerations
\cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986} about how life on Earth may have emerged
as a metabolism-first event rather than a genetics-first one. Kauffman
explored the idea of whether sets of polypeptide complexes that were
generated via reactions that are exclusively catalyzed by members of the
set could have led to collectively self-reproducing systems.  Under
reasonable assumptions and a sufficient supply of amino acid monomers,
surprisingly, their existence becomes almost guaranteed as long as
polypeptide chains exceed a certain length. These ideas built the
foundation for later work on \textsc{Reflexively Autocatalytic
  Food-generated sets} (RAF), which were studied extensively in many
variations \cite{steel_emergence_2000, mossel05CAF, steel_minimal_2013,
  smith_autocatalytic_2014, hordijk_history_2019, steel20}.

Related to different approaches to thinking about the problem of how life
has emerged on a primordial Earth \cite{kauffman_autocatalytic_1986,
  unterberger_stoechiometric_2022}, \textsc{RAF sets} and
\emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} differ substantially in their
motivation, setting, and mathematical formalization. \PFS{Notably,
  \textsc{RAF} theory is phrased in terms of catalytic reaction systems
  (CRS) \textit{sensu} Lohn \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, while
  \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} is grounded in the tradition of
  chemical reaction network theory, tracing back to Rutherford Aris
  \cite{aris_prolegomena_1965}. A meaningful connection between these 
  two frameworks has been investigated} only very recently
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. Notably, the RAF framework, in its original
formulation, allows reactions to also occur uncatalyzed, at least
initially, which seems reasonable from a chemical perspective and
considering the time life required to develop. In a more restrictive
setting, i.e., in \textsc{Constructively Autocatalytic F-generated sets}
(CAFs), reactions can only be executed if all catalysts have been generated
beforehand. Interestingly, a RAF that is itself not entirely constrained in
such a manner is always \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}.

In this contribution, we extend this theory and set out to identify the
underlying structures carrying the autocatalytic capacity of a RAF that is
itself not a CAF. As an initial step, we overcome the menacing obstacle
that \textsc{RAFs} are embedded in catalytic reaction systems (CRS), while
\emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} was derived from chemical reaction
networks (CRNs). It turns out that the set of reactions of a CRS can be
obtained as the quotient set of the reactions in the underlying CRN. In
addition, we show that requiring minimality, with respect to the RAF
property, imposes remarkable constraints on the emerging graph
structures. Finally, we explore how these consequences impact on the
correspondence between the two notions of minimality: \textsc{irreducible
  RAFs} and \emph{autocatalytic cores}.

\section{Representation of Chemical Systems}

Research on systems of chemical interactions has attracted
considerable attention. Unfortunately, their formalization lacks
consistency among the various frameworks \cite{lohn_evolving_1998,
  steel_emergence_2000, feinberg_foundations_2019}. In particular,
different authors have introduced different notions of reactions
\cite{muller_what_2022}. We will therefore take advantage of different
style formats, i.e., $\CR$ and $\FR$, to highlight sets of reactions that
are derived from Chemical Reaction Networks \cite{muller_what_2022} and
from Catalytic Reaction Systems \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, respectively. We
will see that both sets do not contain the same mathematical objects.

\subsection{Chemical Reaction Networks (CRNs)}

\NV{One} setting is given by the framework of a chemical reaction
network (CRN).  A CRN $\Gamma \coloneqq (X,\CR)$ is a tuple of two finite
sets, a set of species $X$ and a set of reactions $\CR$. For any $r\in
\CR$, we can write a stoichiometric equation as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reaction}
  \sum_{x\in X}\ s^-_{xr} \cdot x \quad\underset{r}\rightarrow \quad \sum_{x\in X} \ s^+_{xr}\cdot x
\end{equation}
where $s^{\pm}_{xr}$ are the reactant and product stoichiometry of the
molecule $x$ in the reaction $r$. Reactants and products \NV{of a reaction $r$} are collected in
the sets:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \ceducts(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^-_{xr}>0\} \\
    \cproducts(r) \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ s^+_{xr}>0\} 
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
A \emph{sub-CRN} is a tuple $(X',R')$ with $X'\subseteq X$ and $R'\subseteq
R$, \NV{where $r\in R'$ omits the reference to species $x\not\in X'$}. A 
\emph{sub-CRN} is \emph{well-formed} if for each reaction $r\in R'$
there are species $x,y\in X': s^-_{xr}>0, s^+_{xr}>0$.  The $|X|\times|R|$
\emph{stoichiometric matrix} $\SM$ encodes all \emph{net-stoichiometries}:
\begin{equation}
  \SM_{xr}\coloneqq s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr}
\end{equation}
For a reaction $r$ we can define its \emph{net reaction} $\net{r}$ as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:NetReaction}
  \sum_{x\in X}\ \net{s}^-_{xr} \cdot x\quad\underset{\net{r}} \quad \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X}
  \ \net{s}^+_{xr}\cdot x 
\end{equation}
with 
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
    \net{s}^-_{xr} = -\SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr} < 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise;}\\
    \net{s}^+_{xr} = \SM_{xr}  \qquad &\text{ if } \SM_{xr}> 0, \text{and } 0 \text{ otherwise,}   
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
   \net{\ceducts}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \net{s}^-_{xr}>0\} \\
   \net{\cproducts}(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ \vert \ \net{s}^+_{xr}>0\} \\
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
as the sets of \emph{net-reactants} and \emph{net-products} of reaction $r$,
respectively. Note, we thereby have $\net{\ceducts}(r) = \ceducts(\net{r})$
and $\net{\cproducts}(r) = \cproducts(\net{r})$.

\PFS{There are multiple reasons for the case} where stoichiometry and 
net-stoichiometry are different, i.e. $s^{\pm}_{xr}\neq \net{s}^{\pm}_{xr}$; 
one is \NV{catalysis}. In the original CRN \NV{mathematical} setting, a
catalyst for a reaction $r$ is \NV{simply} a species $x\in X$ such that
$s^{-}_{xr}\cdot s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, thus it appears on both sides of the
stoichiometric equation \eqref{eq:reaction} of $r$. In case $s^-_{xr}>s^+_{xr}>0$, $x$
is both \holy{net-reactant} and catalyst of reaction $r$, while in case
$s^+_{xr}>s^-_{xr}>0$, $x$ is both \holy{net-product} and catalyst of reaction $r$. 

\holy{From a chemical perspective, in turn,} a catalyst is a species $x\in X$ that not only satisfies 
$s^{-}_{xr}\cdot s^{+}_{xr}\neq 0$, but also ``increases the rate of a reaction without 
modifying the overall standard Gibbs energy change in the reaction'' \cite{gold_iupac_2025}. 
However, a solvent, such as water, can also \holy{exert the same effect on a chemical reaction} 
without being considered a catalyst \cite{marx_nature_1999}. For example, in Br{\o}nstedt/Lowry's 
acid-base theory, water is rather considered a reactant, i.e., a proton acceptor, removing environmental
constraints to facilitate proton transfer between reactants \cite{eigen_proton_1964}. \NV{To be specific 
on the `chemically' catalytic role} that a species plays within a chemical reaction, we partition 
stoichiometries $s^{\pm}_{xr}$ as in
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026} by:
\begin{equation}
	 s^{\pm}_{xr}=\sigma^{\pm}_{xr} + k_{xr}.
\end{equation}
By this definition, it is readily verified that $\SM_{xr} =
s^+_{xr}-s^-_{xr} = \sigma^+_{xr} - \sigma^-_{xr}$, thus the net
stoichiometries are identically encoded in $\sigma^{\pm}_{xr}$ The stoichiometric Eq.~\ref{eq:reaction}
can then be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Mod.SmEq}
  \sum_{x\in X}\ \sigma^-_{xr} \cdot x + \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr}
  \cdot x\right)\quad\underset{r}\rightarrow \quad \sum_{x\in X} \ \sigma^+_{xr} \cdot x +
  \left(\sum_{x\in X}\ k_{xr} \cdot x\right).
\end{equation}
We refer to species appearing in Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq} with
$\sigma^{-}_{xr}>0$, $\sigma^{+}_{xr}>0$, and $k_{xr}>0$ as F-reactants,
F-products, and F-catalysts, respectively. We collect F-reactants and
F-products in the following sets:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \feducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^-_{xr} > 0\}; \\
    \fproducts(r) & \coloneqq \{x\in X \ | \ \sigma^+_{xr} > 0\}. \\
  \end{split}
\end{equation} 
\NV{In this setting, distinct reactions may share the same net reaction, and thus differ only in} their usage of
catalysts, \NV{e.g.} by requiring different 
catalysts. For example, the hydrogenation of ethene to ethane can be
mediated by platinum, palladium, or nickel separately
\cite{crampton_ethylene_2016}. An example for co-catalysis, i.e., where the
orchestrated cooperation of two catalysts is required, is the dual-catalyst
system of palladium and copper in the Hoechst-Wacker process, where ethene
is oxidized to acetaldehyde \cite{jira_acetaldehyde_2009}. \PFS{To account
  for such situations, we} therefore define the set of F-catalysts as a
multiset:
\begin{equation}
  \cat(r)\coloneqq \{\TG{k}_{xr} \cdot x\ | \ x\in X,\; \NV{k_{xr}}>0\}.
\end{equation}
The set of F-catalysts may be empty, i.e., for $\cat({\net{r}})=\emptyset$
by definition, or if the reaction is uncatalyzed. \NV{[I do not understand (10), but I discuss this directly with Richard.]}

In a CRN $(X,\CR)$, each reaction $r$, as specified by
Eq.~\ref{eq:Mod.SmEq}, is a distinct object. Thus, it is possible that two
reactions sharing the same net reactants are elements of $\CR$. In this
case, they appear as \NV{distinct} vertices in the bipartite K\"onigs
representation of a CRN $\king\coloneqq (X\cup \C{R}, E_1\cup E_2)$, where
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    E_1\coloneqq \{(x,r) \ | \ s^-_{xr}>0\} \quad \text{ and } \quad E_2\coloneqq \{(x,r) \ | \ s^+_{xr}>0 \}. 
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
We will refer to these kinds of reactions simply as reactions and denote
them in normal font. The reaction or R-graph of a CRN is the directed graph
$\mathcal{R}(\Gamma)$ with vertex set $R$ and edge set $E\coloneqq
\{(r_1,r_2) \ \vert \ \exists x\in X: s^+_{xr_1}>0,s^-_{xr_2}>0\}$, \Wim{i.e., 
there is a directed edge from $r_1$ to $r_2$ if a species $x$ is both a 
product of $r_1$ and a reactant of $r_2$.}

\subsection{Catalytic Reaction Systems (CRS)}

Catalytic Reaction Systems (CRS) \textit{sensu} Lohn
\cite{lohn_evolving_1998} take a different approach, \NV{which we overview here in our notation}. 
\holy{From the perspective of a CRS, for a given set of species $X$ and a set of stoichiometric equations:
	\begin{equation}
		R\coloneqq \left\{r: \sum_{x\in X}\ (\sigma^-_{xr} + \kappa_{xr}) \cdot x\underset{r}\rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ (\sigma^+_{xr} + \kappa_{xr} )\cdot x \  \right\}
	\end{equation}
two reactions $r_1, r_2\in R$ are considered indistinguishable if they differ
only in their F-catalysts but share the same F-reactants and F-products
with the same respective stoichiometric multiplicities. We thus assign to 
every such set the set of uncatalyzed reactions for all reactions: 
\begin{equation}
	\tilde{R}\coloneqq \{\tilde{r} \ | \ r\in R, \sigma^{\pm}_{xr}=\sigma^{\pm}_{x\tilde{r}}, 
	k_{x\tilde{r}}=0, \forall x\in X\}
\end{equation} 
and with $\bar{R}\coloneqq R\cup \tilde{R}$ the following map:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fEquiv}
  \chi: \bar{R} \rightarrow \bar{R}, r\mapsto \tilde{r}.
\end{equation}
In this way, we obtain the relation $\sim$:
\begin{equation}
	\sim: R' \times R', r_1\sim r_2\Leftrightarrow \chi(r)_1=\chi(r_2) \Leftrightarrow 
\end{equation}
or equivalently $r_1\sim r_2\Leftrightarrow \sigma^\pm_{xr_1}=\sigma^\pm_{xr_2}, \forall x\in X$.}
Indeed, $\sim$ is an equivalence relation, and we call a $\sim$-equivalence
class an \emph{F-reaction}.  For consistency, we denote them in bold, i.e.,
$\fr\coloneqq [r]_\sim$ for:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fEquivClass}
  \begin{split}
    \fr= \left\{r': \sum_{x\in X}\ (\sigma^-_{xr'} + \kappa_{xr'}) \cdot x \rightarrow \sum_{x\in X} \ (\sigma^+_{xr'} + \kappa_{xr'} )\cdot x \ \middle\vert \ \sigma_{xr'}^{\pm} = \sigma_{xr}^\pm, 	
    \forall x\in X \right\}
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
\NV{W}e write $\FR$ for sets of F-reactions. Moreover, we may also write
$\feducts(\fr)$ and $\fproducts(\fr)$ since $\feducts(r') =\feducts(r),$ $\fproducts(r') =\fproducts(r),$ $\forall r' \in[r]_\sim$. 
\holy{By definition, the natural representative of each equivalence class is the uncatalyzed reaction $\tilde{r}\in [r]_{\sim}:
\cat(\tilde{r}) = \emptyset$, which coincides with the net reaction $\net{r}$ if and only if $\feducts(r)= \net{\ceducts}(r)$ and
$\fproducts(\tilde{r}) = \net{\cproducts}(r)$. It is primarily added to aid understanding of the equivalence relation $\sim$, as
a CRN $(X,R)$ might contain $r\in R: \tilde{r}\notin R$.}

\PFS{For a given set of reactions $R$, we introduce a map $\varphi$ to conveniently obtain the corresponding F-reactions:}
\begin{equation}
  \varphi: R\mapsto \FR\quad\quad \varphi(r) \coloneqq \fr, 
\end{equation}
where $\FR\coloneqq \{[r]_{\sim} \ | \ r\in R\}$. This allows access to the
pre-image of a set of F-reactions $\FR$ by $\varphi^{-1}(\FR)$.

\holy{For a set of reactions $R$ together with a set of species $X$, 
each $\sim$-equivalence class is associated to pairs $(\cat(r), r): r\in \fr$,
which are called \emph{catalyzations}. \emph{Catalyzations} evoke sets 
of catalysts $\cat(\fr)\coloneqq \{\cat(r) \ \vert \ r\in \fr\}$ that can be stored 
in a collection $\cat(\FR)$ for $\FR=\varphi^{-1}(R)$. Thereby, if an F-reaction 
$\fr\in \FR$ has no catalyzations, then $\cat(\fr)=\emptyset$, which implies 
that $\fr$ contains only the natural representative, in particular, the uncatalyzed 
reaction $\tilde{r}\in \fr$. In general, the \emph{catalyzations} are predefined, also 
for sets of F-reactions \cite{hordijk_detecting_2004}.}

 A CRS is then the triple $\Phi \coloneqq (X,\FR, \cat)$, which has a unique corresponding 
 CRN $\Gamma(\Phi) = (X, \CR)$, where $\CR=\bigcup_{\fr \in \FR}\fr$. For clarity, we might 
 write the CRN as a triple as $(X, \CR, \cat)$.

Analogously to the bipartite graph representation of a CRN, the bipartite
graph of a CRS is given as $\queen(\Phi=(X,\FR, \cat))\coloneqq (X \cup
\FR, E_1\cup E_2)$ with:

\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    E_1\coloneqq \{(x,\fr) \ | \ x\in X, \fr\in \FR: s^-_{xr}>0 \text{ for some } r \in \fr \} \\
    E_2\coloneqq \{(\fr, x) \ | \ x\in X, \fr\in \FR: s^+_{xr}>0 \text{ for some } r \in \fr \}
  \end{split}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The Relationship between CRN and CRS}
\label{ssect:CRNCRS}

In fact, the $\sim$-equivalence relation induces a convenient connection
between the bipartite graph of $\queen(\Phi)$ and the corresponding
bipartite graph of the $K(\Gamma(\Phi))$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:QuotGraph}
  Let $\Phi\coloneqq (X,\FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with corresponding CRN
  $\Gamma(\Phi)$. The bipartite directed K\"onig graph 
  $\queen\coloneqq \queen(\Phi)$ is the quotient graph of $\king \coloneqq
  \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ w.r.t. the equivalence relation $\sim$ as defined in
  Eq.~\ref{eq:fEquiv}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Let $(x,\fr) \in E_1(\queen)$. By definition, there is an $r'\in \fr:
  s^-_{xr'}>0$, thus $(x,r')\in E_1(\king)$.  Let now, $(x,r)\in
  E_1(\king)$. By definition of $\queen$, there is an edge $(x,\fr)\in
  E_1(\queen)$.  Similar arguments hold for edges from $E_2(\queen)$ and
  $E_2(\king)$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

\holy{In the monocatalyst case, for example, where each F-reaction has exactly
one catalyst - in this case also only one catalyzation - we have 
$\vert \fr \vert = 1, \forall \fr \in \FR$.} Thus, each reaction vertex of $\queen(\Phi)$ 
is substituted in $\queen(\Gamma(\Phi))$ by its single representative, while all 
edges to adjacent vertices are conserved. This yields that $\queen(\Phi)$ 
and $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ are isomorphic. In turn, if one equivalence class 
contains more than one representative, then $|V(\king(\Gamma(\Phi))|>|V(\queen(\Phi)|$, 
which directly implies that $\queen(\Phi)\not \simeq \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:IsoQK}
  Let $\Phi\coloneqq (X, \FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with corresponding CRN
  $\Gamma(\Phi)$. Then $\queen(\Phi)\simeq \king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ if and only
  if $\vert \fr \vert = 1, \forall \fr \in \FR$.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}

We remark that Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK} not only holds in the monocatalytic
case, but for every CRS that exhibits only trivial equivalence classes, in
particular also for the uncatalyzed case.

An obvious result for quotient graphs is that $\queen(\Phi)$ is
strongly-connected whenever $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$ is strongly
connected. This follows immediately from the fact that strong connectivity
is preserved under surjective digraph homomorphisms
\cite{hell_graphs_2026}.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK}, the converse also holds
if each equivalence class is of size $1$.  In general, however, this is not
true, in particular, if one F-reaction has two alternative catalysts, and
one of them is not connected by a path originating in any of the products.

\section{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)}

Catalytic reaction systems (CRS) are the fundamental structure on which the
notion of reflexively autocatalytic sets is built
\cite{steel_emergence_2000, hordijk_detecting_2004}. In addition, they rely
on the closure of a set $X' \subseteq X$ w.r.t.\ to a set $\RAF \subseteq
\FR$, denoted as $\closure{\RAF}{X'}$, which is the unique minimal set $Y$
containing $X'$ such that for any $\fr\in \RAF: \feducts(r)\subseteq Y
\Rightarrow \fproducts(r)\subseteq Y$. Thus:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Closure}
  \closure{R'}{X'} \coloneqq \min \{\PFS{Y} \subseteq X \ | \ X'\subseteq \PFS{Y},
  \feducts(r) \subseteq \PFS{Y} \Rightarrow \fproducts(r) \subseteq \PFS{Y} \ \forall
  r\in \RAF\}
%%  %}_{\eqqcolon \mathfrak{C}_{\RAF}(X')}
\end{equation}
A reflexively autocatalytic set (RAF) is then defined as follows:

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}[RAF]\label{def:RAF}
  Let $(X,\FR,\cat)$ be a CRS with $\food\subseteq X$. A set $\RAF\subseteq
  \FR$ is called
  \vspace{6pt}
  \begin{itemize} 
  \item[i.]{\emph{reflexively autocatalytic} (RA) if $\forall r\in \RAF:
    \emptyset \neq \cat(r), \exists c\in \cat(r): c \subseteq
    \feducts(\RAF)\cup \fproducts(\RAF)$}\\[-1.0em]
  \item[ii.]{\emph{\food-generated} if $\feducts(\RAF)\subseteq
    \closure{\RAF}{\food}$}\\[-1.0em]
  \item[iii.]{\emph{reflexive autocatalytic} and \emph{\food-generated}
    (RAF) if both i. and ii. hold}
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

We remark that Property $i.$ requires each F-reaction to be catalyzed, and
for each F-reaction, all catalysts for at least one catalyzation are in the
support of $\RAF$, i.e. in $\feducts(\RAF)\cup \fproducts(\RAF)$. Property
$ii.$ of Def.~\ref{def:RAF}, together with Eq.~\ref{eq:Closure}, directly
implies that all F-products for any reaction $\fr\in \RAF$ are also
contained in the $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$, thus $\fproducts(r)\subseteq
\closure{\RAF}{\food}$.

The sequences specifying when an F-reaction can happen for the first time

$\preceq$ on the set of F-reactions $\FR$ and on the set of species
$X$. The generation in which a species $x\in X$ appears as a product for
the first time will be denoted by $\sgen(x)$ and set by definition
$\sgen(f)\coloneqq 0$ for all $f\in \food$. In addition, we denote the
generation in which all F-reactants are available for an F-reaction $\fr$
by $\rgen(\fr)$. A species $x_1$ precedes a species $x_2$, i.e.:
\begin{equation}
  x_1\preccurlyeq x_2 \Leftrightarrow \sgen(x_1) \leq \sgen(x_2)
\end{equation} 
and a reaction $\fr_1$ precedes a reaction $\fr_2$, i.e.: 
\begin{equation}
  \fr_1\preceq \fr_2 \Leftrightarrow \rgen(\fr_1) \leq \rgen(\fr_2)
\end{equation}
We refer to the values of $\sgen$ and $\rgen$ as the generation of a 
reaction or species. Since all representatives of an F-reaction share 
the same F-reactants, we may define the generation of a reaction as $\delta(r)\coloneqq \rgen(\fr)$.

\begin{definition}
  A \emph{constructively autocatalytic and food-generated} set (CAF) is a
  RAF such that for each F-reaction $\fr$ all $x\in \feducts(x)$ satisfy
  $\sgen(x)<\rgen(\fr)$ and there is at least one $r\in \fr$ such that all
  catalyst $x\in \cat(r)$ satisfy $\sgen(x)< \rgen(\fr)$.
\end{definition}
One important consequence of the partial order is that if a RAF contains
two $\sim$~equivalence classes that share the same net reaction, the
''later'' one w.r.t.\ $\prec$ can be removed.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:OneFReacPerRAF}
  Let $(X,\FR,\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food\subseteq X$,
  $\RAF\subseteq \FR$ a RAF, and $\fr_1,\fr_2 \in \RAF: \fr_1\neq \fr_2,
  \hat{\fr}_1 = \hat{\fr}_2'$. Then $\RAF\setminus \{\fr_2\}$ is a RAF if
  $\rgen(\fr_1)\leq \rgen(\fr_2)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Let $\RAF' \coloneqq \RAF \setminus \{r_2\}$. Since $\RAF$ was
  reflexively autocatalytic, $\RAF'$ is reflexively autocatalytic. In
  addition, since $\nfeducts(\fr_1)=\nfeducts(\fr_2)$ and
  $\nfproducts(\fr_1)=\nfproducts(\fr_2)$ and $\rgen(\fr_1)\leq
  \rgen(\fr_2)$, $\closure{\RAF}{F} = \closure{\RAF'}{F}$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt} 
\noindent We may remark that the converse is not true. To see this, consider the following CRN example:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    r_1&: f + (x_1) \hspace{22pt} \rightarrow x_1 + x_2 + (x_1)\\
    r_2&: f + x_1 + (x_1) \rightarrow 2x_1 + x_2 + (x_1)
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
Then clearly, $\fr_1\neq \fr_2$ and $\rgen(\fr_1) = 1 < 2 = \rgen(\fr_2)$
since $\fr_2$ requires an $x_1$ as an F-reactant.

The partial order $\preceq$ allows us to observe that each species vertex
of the $\closure{\RAF}{\food}$ is reachable from at least one food molecule
in $\food$ in the bipartite K\"onig graph, which is restricted to
$\closure{\RAF}{\food}$, the reactions in $\RAF$, and to those
catalyzations $\cat'$ of reactions in $\RAF\subseteq \FR$. Throughout, we
denote this graph as $\queen'\coloneqq \queen(\closure{\RAF}{\food}, \RAF,
\cat')$.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:RAFPath}
  Let $(X, \FR, \cat)$ be a CRS with $\food \subseteq X$ and a RAF
  $\RAF\subseteq \FR$.  For any $x\in \closure{}{\food}\setminus \food$
  there is an $f\in \food$ such that $P=(f, \ldots, x)$ is a path in
  $\queen'$.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Irreducible RAFs}\label{sec:IrrRAF}

Minimal \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} subnetworks, formally
introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:AutoCatIrrRAF}, are termed \emph{autocatalytic
cores} \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}. Their characterization and
computational enumeration have gained reasonable attention in chemical
reaction networks with and without explicit catalysis
\cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020, golnik_birne_2025, golnik_using_2026,
  golnik_autocatalytic_2026}.  In \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, we have
shown that each RAF that is not a CAF contains a stoichiometrical
autocatalytic subnetwork and thereby an autocatalytic core. It is therefore
of immediate interest to ask for the substructures of a RAF that carry
autocatalytic cores.

The most natural starting point for this endeavour are RAFs that contain no
proper subset that is also a RAF.
\PFS{
\begin{definition}\cite{steel_minimal_2013}.
  A RAF is irreducible if no subset of its set of F-reactions determine a RAF. 
\end{definition}
}
%% Minimal sets of F-reactions w.r.t.\ the
% RAF properties have been termed \emph{irreducible} RAFs in the literature
%% \cite{steel_minimal_2013}.
A direct consequence of
Lemma~\ref{lem:OneFReacPerRAF} is that irreducible RAFs contain, for each
net reaction, only one F-reaction.
\vspace{6pt} 
\begin{corollary}
  Let $(X,\mathbf{R}, \cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food\subseteq X$ and
  $\RAF$ an irreducible RAF.  For $\fr_r,\fr_2 \in \mathbf{R}: \fr_1\neq
  \fr_2, \hat{\fr}_1=\hat{\fr}_2$, $\fr_1\in \RAF$ implies $\fr_2\notin
  \RAF$.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
%
Still, they might contain F-reactions that have more than one
representative, as the following example shows.
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    r'_1&: f + (x_1) \rightarrow x_3 + (x_1)\\
    r''_1&: f + (x_2) \rightarrow x_3 + (x_2)\\
    r_2&:  x_3 + (x_3) \rightarrow x_1 + x_2 + (x_3)
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
The only irreducible RAF in this example is $\{\fr_1=\{r_1',r_1''\},
\fr_2=\{r_2\}\}$, which implies that Lemma~\ref{lem:IsoQK} does not
\PFS{apply to} irreducible RAFs.

According to Theorem 3.1 of \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, an autocatalytic
elementary circuit can be found in a RAF as long as it is not a
CAF. Irreducible RAFs exhibit the convenient property that they are not
CAFs as long as they are non-trivial, i.e., if they are composed of more
than a single reaction.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:trivRAFCAF}
  Let $(X,\mathbf{R},\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food$ and suppose
  $\RAF$ is an irreducible RAF. Then $\RAF$ is a CAF if and only if it is
  trivial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Let $\RAF$ be a CAF and assume $|\RAF|>1$. Let $\TG{\fr}\in \RAF:
  \TG{\rgen(\fr)}=1$. Then all F-reactants and all F-catalysts for one
  \emph{catalyzation} are in $\food$ since $\RAF$ is a CAF. Thus $\{\TG{\fr}\}$ is
  a RAF itself, contradicting the minimality of $\RAF$.  Conversely, assume
  that $|\RAF|=1$. Then all F-reactants and all F-catalysts for at least
  one \emph{catalyzation} are in $\food$ since otherwise $\RAF$ would not
  be a RAF. Thus, $\RAF$ is a CAF.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
%
This Lemma justifies \PFS{restricting attention to} non-trivial irreducible
RAFs. In fact, for each non-trivial irreducible RAF, each F-reaction has at
least one reactant that is not in the food set.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:OneReac}
  Let $(X,\RAF,\cat)$ be a CRS with food-set $\food$ and $\RAF$ a
  irreducible RAF.  Then $\fr\in \RAF$ has at least one reactant
  (F-reactant or F-catalyst) that is not in $\food$. For $\fr\in \RAF:
  \rgen(\fr)=1$, this is an F-catalyst.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Assume this is not the case for an $\fr\in \RAF$. Then $\{\fr\}$ is a RAF
  which contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}

The consequences of the result of Lemma~\ref{lem:OneReac} were already
investigated in \cite{steel_minimal_2013}. It was shown that the R-graph
for each such RAF contains an elementary circuit. In the next subsection,
we will therefore turn to investigate more thoroughly the structural
properties of two graph representations of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs,
the bipartite and the reaction graph, in both the CRS and the CRN
setting. In particular, we will strengthen a result by Steel et
al.\ \cite{steel_minimal_2013}.

\subsection{The Bipartite and Reaction Graphs of irreducible RAFs}

Throughout, we will consider a CRS $\Phi\coloneqq (X,\FR,\cat)$ with
food-set $\food\subseteq X$ and K\"onig graph $\queen\coloneqq
\queen(\Phi)$. In addition, we assume a non-trivial, irreducible RAF
$\RAF\subseteq \FR$ with sub-CRS
\begin{equation}
  \Phi'= (\closure{\RAF}{\food}, \RAF, \cat')\\
\end{equation}
and K\"onig graph $\queen'\coloneqq \queen(\Phi')$ as well as its CRN
representation $\king'\coloneqq \king(\Gamma(\Phi'))$. We denote both
R-graphs of $\queen'$ and $\king'$ as the graphs
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \RGraph(\queen')&\coloneqq (\RAF, E), \hspace{25pt} \quad E\coloneqq \{(\fr_1,\fr_2) \ \vert \ \exists x\in X: s^+_{x\fr_1}>0, s^-_{x\fr_2}>0\} \\
    \RGraph(\king') &\coloneqq (\varphi^{-1}(\RAF), E), \quad E\coloneqq \{(r_1,r_2) \ \vert \ (\fr_1,\fr_2)\in E(\RGraph(\queen')\},
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
respectively. In line with calling elements of $\food$ food-molecules, we kindly refer to molecules of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Waste}
  W\coloneqq \{x\in \closure{\RAF}{\food} \ | \ \nexists \fr \in \RAF \text{ s.t. } s^-_{xr}>0\}		
\end{equation}
as waste molecules. We note that $V(\king')=\closure{\RAF}{\food}$.

\subsubsection{Strong connectivity}

We start this section by showing that the R-graph of a non-trivial,
irreducible RAF in the CRS setting, $\RGraph(\queen)$, is strongly
connected.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:RGQSC}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then $\RGraph(\queen')$ is
  strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Assume this is not the case. Then there are at least two strongly
  connected components $\RAF_1$ and $\RAF_2$. Assume w.l.o.g. that there is
  no path from any $r_2\in \RAF_2$ to any $r_1\in \RAF_1$. Then all
  F-reactants and F-catalysts of all F-reactions in $\RAF_1$ are in the
  closure $\closure{\RAF_1}{\food}$, in symbols $\feducts\cup
  \cat(\RAF_1)\subseteq \closure{\RAF_1}{\food}$. With $\RAF_1\subseteq
  \RAF$ and $\RAF$ being a RAF, this implies that $\RAF_1$ is itself a
  RAF, which contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}
\vspace{6pt}
Lemmata~\ref{lem:trivRAFCAF} and \ref{lem:RGQSC} can be
summarized to the following statement.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:RAFStrCon}
  Let $\RAF$ be an irreducible RAF. Then $\RAF$ is non-trivial if and only
  if the graph $\RGraph(\queen')$ is not a single F-reaction and is
  strongly connected.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

It is not hard to see that $\RGraph(\queen')$ is also the quotient graph
for $\RGraph(\king')$. \holy{w.r.t.\ the equivalence relation $\simeq$ 
defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:fEquiv}.}
Fig.~\ref{fig:QScKNot} illustrates that the strong
connectivity of a quotient graph does not guarantee strong connectivity of
the original graph. However, for non-trivial, irreducible RAFs,
$\RGraph(\king')$ is indeed strongly connected.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:KSC}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then $\RGraph(\king')$ is
  strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}
%
It is readily verified that the subgraphs of $\queen'$ and $\king'$
containing only those vertices that have at least one incoming and one
outgoing edge are strongly connected as well. However, these subgraphs
might still contain food molecules. The next two statements show that their
removal does not destroy strong connectivity.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:PSC}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then 
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:PrinceGraph}
    \princess(\RAF) \coloneqq
    \queen'[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus (\food\cup W) \cup \RAF]
  \end{equation}
  is strongly connected.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Assume this is not the case. Then there are two strongly connected
  components $\princess_1,\princess_2$.  W.l.o.g., we assume that there is
  no path from any $u\in \princess_2$ to any $v\in \princess_1$. Then all
  paths from $\princess_2$ to $\princess_1$ pass through $\food$ in
  $\queen'$. This, however, implies that all F-catalysts and F-reactants
  for all F-reactions in $\princess_1$ are contained in $\food\cup
  \princess_1$. Then $\FR(\princess_1)$ is a non-empty RAF which
  contradicts the minimality of $\RAF$.
\end{proof}	
\vspace{6pt}

Following the same argument as in Lemma~\ref{lem:PSC}, we obtain an
analogous result for the subgraph of $\king'$ that contains no food and
waste molecules.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then 
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:PrinceGraph}
    \lord(\RAF) \coloneqq
    \king'[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus
      (\food\cup W) \cup \varphi^{-1}(\RAF)]
  \end{equation}
  is strongly connected.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

\subsubsection{Strong Blocks}

The existence of cut-vertices, which can only be reaction vertices, enables
the decomposition of $\princess$ and $\lord$ into strong blocks. For the
graph $\lord(\RAF)$, each of these blocks contains an autocatalytic core,
which is warranted by a unique structural property common to any strong
block that guarantees semipositivity. In particular, each block in
$\princess(\RAF)$ and $\lord(\RAF)$ contains reaction vertices for which
all reactants are F-catalysts but not F-reactants.  We thereby collect them
in the following sets:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \FR^*(\block)\coloneqq \{\fr\in \FR(\block) \ | \ s^-_{x\fr}>0
    \Rightarrow s^+_{x\fr}=s^-_{x\fr}, \forall x\in X(\block)\}
    \\ R^*(\block)\coloneqq \{r\in \varphi^{-1}{\FR}(\block) \ |
    \ s^-_{xr}>0 \Rightarrow s^+_{xr}=s^-_{xr}, \forall x\in X(\block)\}.
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
and refer to these vertices as \emph{pure F-reactions} and \emph{pure
reactions}, respectively.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:cutVertexPureCat}
  For a non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$, any strong block $\block$ of
  $\princess(\RAF)$ contains a pure reaction.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}
%
Following the same line of argument as in the proof of
Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat}, the same statement also holds for
$\lord(\RAF)$, with the small difference that for some of the cut-vertices
$\fr\in \RAF$ all representatives that are not cut-vertices in
$\lord(\RAF)$.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:cutVertexPureCat}
  For a non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$, any strong block $B$ of
  $\lord(\RAF)$ contains a pure reaction.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}
%
The proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} has three important
consequences. First, it identifies cut-vertices as pure F-reactions
(equivalently pure reactions), although not all cut-vertices necessarily
are. Second, pure F-reactions take an important role in the partial order
of strong blocks of irreducible RAFs, as there is always one with the
lowest generation number in a strong block, according to the partial
order. Third, Cor.~\ref{cor:cutVertexPureCat} implies that the
stoichiometric matrix of a strong block $B$ always contains a non-negative
column. This column represents a pure reaction, i.e., it has only reactants
that are F-catalysts but not F-reactants within $B$ and at least one
positive entry for a product $y^*\in X(\block): s^+_{yr^*}>0$:
\begin{equation}
  \SM[X(B), R(B)] = 
  \begin{array}{c c}
    & 	\begin{array}{c c c} 			
	  & 	r^* 		& 
	\end{array} \\
    \begin{array}{c} 
      \ \\ 
      x^* \\
      \ \\ [0.5em]
      y^* \\
      \ \\
      \ \\		
    \end{array}
    & \begin{pmatrix}						
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
	\ldots 	& 	0 		& \ldots \\
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots \\
	\ldots 	& 	s^+_{y^*r^*} & \ldots \\
	\ldots 	& 	0 & \ldots \\
	\ddots 	& 	\vdots 	& \ddots 
      \end{pmatrix}
  \end{array}
\end{equation}
\vspace{6pt}

The nonnegative column reflecting $r^*$ in $\SM(B)\coloneqq \SM[X(B),
  R(B)]$ renders $r^*$ as an import reaction for the strong block $B$. In
fact, this allows us to construct a strictly positive vector $v$ of
reaction rates which satisfies $\SM(B)v>0$.

\subsection{Autocatalytic Capacity of irreducible RAFs}
\label{sec:AutoCatIrrRAF}

In chemical reaction networks (CRNs) with explicit catalysis, the underlying network cannot be uniquely 
inferred from the stoichiometric matrix. The matrix-centered definition of autocatalysis as introduced by Blokhuis 
\cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020} and Vassena \cite{vassena_unstable_2024} therefore required 
a modification that is based on the network and the matrix \cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{definition}\label{def:autSubRN}
  A CRN $(X,R)$ is \emph{autocatalytic} if it contains a sub-CRN $(X',R')$
  such that
\begin{enumerate}
 \item[(i)]  $(X',R')$ is  well-formed;
 \item[(ii)]  $\SM'\coloneqq \SM[X',R']$ is semi-positive, i.e., there is $v\in \mathbb{R}_{>0}^{|R'|}: \SM'v>0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
\vspace{6pt}

A sub-CRN $(X',R')$ that satisfies \textit{(i.)} and \textit{(ii.)}, while
no proper subnetwork $(X'',R'')$ with $X''\subseteq X', R''\subseteq R'$
does, is called an \emph{autocatalytic core}. We are now in the position to
show that $\lord(\RAF)$ for each non-trivial, irreducible RAF $\RAF$ is
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}.

The results on the structure of irreducible RAFs imply that each strong
block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ satisfies $(i.)$ of Def.~\ref{def:autSubRN}. To
show semi-positivity, the key observation is that $B$ contains at least one
pure reaction, i.e., a reaction for which all reactants in $B$ are
F-catalysts (Lemma~\ref{lem:cutVertexPureCat} and
Cor.~\ref{cor:cutVertexPureCat}). In particular, all $r \in
\varphi^{-1}(\RAF)$ that satisfy $\delta(r)=1$ are pure reactions.

Pure reactions induce a nonnegative column with at least one positive entry
in the corresponding stoichiometric matrices, such as the matrices
$\SM[X(B), R(B)]$ for a strong block $B$ and
$\SM[\closure{\RAF}{\food}\setminus \food, \varphi^{-1}(\RAF)]$ of the
whole RAF without food molecules. They can therefore be viewed as import
reactions, which allow a sufficiently high import into the subnetwork such
that all succeeding reactions exhibit strictly positive flow values that
yield a net increase for all subnetwork species.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}[Proof: SI]\label{thm:NonFoodSemipositiv}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. The matrix $A\coloneqq
  \SM[V(\king')\setminus \food, \varphi^{-1} (\RAF)]$ is semi-positive.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

As a remark, the proof can be applied to the sub-stoichiometric matrix
representing the species and reaction vertices of any strong block $B$ of
$\lord(\RAF)$ to show their semi-positivity. Equivalently, every species of
$B$ is produced and consumed only by reactions belonging to that
block. Thereby, the vector $v_{R(B)}$ that denotes the sub-vector of $v$
restricted to the reactions of $B$ directly yields for the submatrix
restricted to the species and reactions of $B$:
\begin{equation}
  \SM[X(B), R(B)]v_{R(B)}>0.
\end{equation}
Thus, each block has a semipositive stoichiometric submatrix and is also
well-formed.
%
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}\label{Thm:StrBlockCore}
  Every strong block $B$ of $\lord(\RAF)$ for a non-trivial, irreducible
  RAF $\RAF$ contains an autocatalytic core.
\end{theorem}
\vspace{6pt}

Thm.~\ref{Thm:StrBlockCore} provides only information on the existence, but
not on the structure of the subnetworks responsible for the semipositivity
of the corresponding submatrix. In the proof of Thm.~3.1
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, it was shown that each RAF that is itself not
a CAF contains an autocatalytic elementary circuit. We will adapt this
construction to obtain such a circuit for each strong block.

Since strong blocks are strongly connected, each vertex, and thus, also
each pure reaction, is located on an elementary circuit. Together with the
partial order, this observation can be engaged to construct an elementary
circuit $C$ for every strong block that gives rise to a stoichiometric
submatrix $\tilde{\SM}(C)\coloneqq \SM[X(C), R(C)]$ in which each column
and row has at most one negative entry, and one column is nonnegative with
at least one positive entry. The columns of $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ can then be
reordered to obtain a matrix $\SM(C)$ with nonpositive diagonal and
nonnegative off-diagonal entries.  A matrix with nonnegative off-diagonal
entries is called a \emph{Metzler} matrix. It is not hard to see that, in
case $\tilde{\SM}(C)$ is semi-positive, $\SM(C)$ is semi-positive. In
addition, the subnetwork of an elementary circuit $(X(C),R(C))$ is
well-formed.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:MetzCircuit}
  Let $B$ be a strong block of $\lord(\RAF)$. Then $B$ contains an
  elementary circuit $C$ with one pure reaction vertex, and there is a
  reordering of the columns of $ \SM[X(C), R(C)]$ that yields a Metzler
  matrix.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt}

We call an elementary circuit with the properties defined in
Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit} a Metzler circuit. Unfortunately, such a
Metzler circuit can, as shown in the proof, still contain a species-to-reaction 
or, equivalently, metabolite-to-reaction (MR-) chord. The example in
Sec.~\ref{sec:MetzNotCore} depicts such a scenario. Nevertheless, they are
always \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{lemma}[Proof: SI]\label{lem:MetzCircAut}
  If $C$ is a Metzler circuit containing a \emph{pure reaction}, then
  $(X(C), R(C))$ is \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}.
\end{lemma}
\vspace{6pt} If the subnetwork induced by an elementary circuit $C$ is
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}, we say that $C$ is
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}. Elementary circuits free of
MR-chords play an important role in enumerating autocatalytic cores
\cite{golnik_using_2026}.  Since they are always Metzler circuits, we
obtain the following corollary as a direct consequence.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}
  Any MR-chordless elementary circuit $C$ of a strong block in $L$
  containing a \emph{pure reaction} is autocatalytic.
\end{corollary}
\vspace{6pt}

In case the strong block consists of a single reaction $r$ and a single
species $x$, then $x$ is an F-catalyst and an F-product. In addition, there
is only one elementary circuit, in particular, the digon $(x,r,x)$, which
is by definition a Metzler circuit.
\vspace{6pt}
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:AutoCatReac}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF and $B$ be a strong block of
  $L(\RAF)$.  If $r$ is composed of a single reaction $r$ and a single
  species $x$, then $r$ is an explicit autocatalytic reaction, and $x$ is
  an F-catalyst and an F-product of $r$.

Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}, \ref{lem:MetzCircAut}, and
Cor.~\ref{cor:AutoCatReac}, together yield the main result of this
contribution.

\vspace{6pt}
\begin{theorem}
  Let $\RAF$ be a non-trivial, irreducible RAF. Then every strong block $B$
  of $L(\RAF)$ contains an autocatalytic Metzler circuit. In case $B$ is
  composed of a single reaction $r$ and a single species $x$, then $r$ is
  an explicit autocatalytic reaction and $x$ is an F-catalyst and an
  F-product of $r$.
\end{theorem} 
\vspace{6pt}

As noted, these elementary circuits need not be autocatalytic cores, for
various reasons.  One is digons, i.e., elementary circuits of length $2$
where the species-to-reaction edge is not located along the original
Metzler circuit. An example of this situation is shown in
Sec.~\ref{sec:MetzNotCore}.  Another reason is non-unit stoichiometries,
which can give rise to strictly smaller circuits inside the constructed
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} Metzler circuit. The example in
Sec.~\ref{sec:NonUnStoich} captures such a scenario. As the K\"onig graph
is a simple digraph, stoichiometries are naturally not encoded besides
being specified as edge labels. We can, therefore, not hope to find a
general construction method that identifies autocatalytic cores
immediately.

\section{Discussion}

\PFS{\textsc{Reflexively autocatalytic sets (RAFs)} and
  \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} are grounded in different
  formalizations of chemical reaction networks that lead to inequivalent
  concepts of reactions, distinguished by the manner in which catalysis is
  handled. While in the CRNs underlying the theory of \emph{stoichiometric
  autocatalysis} catalysts are not formally distinguished from other
  reactants and products, the CRS framework focuses on net F-reactions
  considering catalysts as external to the F-reactions. As a consequence,
  F-reactions are equivalence classes of (CRN) reactions with respect to
  the equivalence relation $\sim$ describing reactions that share the same
  non-catalyst reactants (F-reactants), the same non-catalyst products
  (F-products) and the same net stoichiometry, see
  Sect.~\ref{ssect:CRNCRS}.  This observation also implies that graph
  representations of reaction systems in CRS are quotient graphs of
  corresponding constructions for CRN. Most importantly, the directed
  bipartite K\"onig representation of a CRS $\queen(\Phi)$ is the quotient
  graph of the K\"onig graph associated with its underlying CRN,
  $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$. The equation
  \begin{equation*}
    \queen(\Phi) = \king(\Gamma(\Phi))/\sim
  \end{equation*}
  establishing the formal mathematical correspondence between CRSs and CRNs
  that we then leveraged to analyze autocatalytic substructures.}

%% \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} \cite{blokhuis_Universal_2020}
%% represent two different interpretations of the concept of an explicit
%% autocatalytic reaction, which was first discussed in 1890 by Wilhelm
%% Ostwald \cite{Ostwald:90}. While RAFs are defined in catalytic reaction
%% systems (CRS), in which each reaction is explicitly catalyzed,
%% \emph{stoichiometric autocatalysis} developed, in accordance with the IUPAC
%% definition of an autocatalytic reaction \cite{gold_iupac_2025}, from
%% chemical reaction networks (CRN), where reactions can but don't have to be
%% catalyzed.


Although inherently distinct, it was recently demonstrated that
every \textsc{RAF} that is not a \textsc{CAF} is nevertheless
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. Here,
we continued examining this relationship in greater detail, focusing in
particular on the interplay between the minimal structures arising in both
frameworks.

%% The principal obstacle to a direct comparison between the two frameworks
%% lies in their divergent interpretations of chemical reactions. In CRNs,
%% reactions are defined via their associated stoichiometric equations
%% \cite{muller_what_2022}. Therefore, each equation reflects a different
%% chemical reaction, which we call a \emph{reaction}. In particular,
%% reactions that share the same net stoichiometries but engage a different
%% set of catalysts or involve species that appear on both sides of the
%% reaction with identical stoichiometry, albeit being considered catalysts,
%% are unique mathematical objects. In contrast, reactions in CRS
%% \cite{lohn_evolving_1998}, referred to here as \emph{F-reactions}, treat
%% reactions that differ solely in their catalysts as identical. Accordingly,
%% an F-reaction can be viewed as an equivalence class of reactions that share
%% the same non-catalyst reactants (F-reactants) and the same non-catalyst
%% products (F-products).

%% Building on this observation, we defined an equivalence relation, denoted
%% by $\sim$, on the set of reactions based exclusively on their F-reactants
%% and F-products. Under this relation, reactions are mapped to the set of
%% F-reactions and can be identified as representatives of their corresponding
%% $\sim$-equivalence classes, that is, their associated F-reaction images.
%% As a convenient consequence, we obtained the directed bipartite K\"onig
%% representation of a CRS $\queen(\Phi)$, as the quotient graph of the
%% K\"onig graph associated with its underlying CRN, $\king(\Gamma(\Phi))$,
%% thereby establishing a formal mathematical correspondence between CRSs and
%% CRNs. This result was not only essential for the results presented in this
%% contribution, but also provides a foundation for future research aimed at
%% further exploring the relationship between these two interpretations of
%% chemical reaction systems. Moreover, it facilitates the transfer of results
%% obtained in one framework to the other, and vice versa.

In the present contribution we examine this relationship in greater
detail, focusing in particular on the interplay between the minimal
structures arising in both frameworks.  \PFS{To this end we started from
  Thm.~3.1 in \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, which asserts that every
  \textsc{RAF} that is not a \textsc{CAF} is \emph{stoichiometrically
  autocatalytic}. As an immediate consequence, \emph{irreducible RAFs}
  \cite{steel_minimal_2013}, i.e., \textsc{RAF}s that do not contain proper
  sub-RAFs, are \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} as long as they are
  not CAFs.} Lemma~\ref{lem:trivRAFCAF} show that irreducible RAFs are CAFs
if and only if they are trivial, i.e., consisting of a single reaction.

The minimality of non-trivial, irreducible RAFs
%% is a particularly strong property, as it gives rise to several 
%%noteworthy structural consequences that are also characteristic of \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} CRNs. 
%%In particular, it imposes
implies strong connectivity on both the R-graph of the CRS and the
underlying CRN. 

Since strong connectivity is a also prerequisite for autocatalytic cores.
Their bipartite digraph representation in CRNs with explicit catalysis are
\emph{fluffles}, and thus in particular strong blocks
\cite{golnik_autocatalytic_2026}. The bipartite K\"onig graph of
non-trivial, irreducible RAFs, reflecting the underlying CRN without food
and waste molecules, $\lord(\RAF)$, \PFS{is not only strongly connected but
  composed of strong blocks that are separated by cut vertices that are
  always reaction vertices and never species vertices
  (Lemma~\ref{lem:CutVertexReac}).}
%See also Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex} for an example. 

Strong blocks are, by definition, well-formed. In addition, some of their
cut vertices, so-called \emph{pure reactions}, which have only F-catalysts
as reactants inside the corresponding block $B$, can act as import
reactions as they induce a non-negative column in the stoichiometric
submatrix $\SM(B)$. In theory, this would allow for an infinitely high
in-flow such that all species inside the block are net-produced. Indeed,
$\SM(B)$ is semi-positive for any strong block $B$, such that each block is
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} (Thm.~\ref{Thm:StrBlockCore}).

The structures responsible for each strong block being
\emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} are \emph{Metzler circuits};
elementary circuits that allow for a column-reordering of their associated
stoichiometric submatrix such that the resulting Matrix is a Metzler matrix
(Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}). Such an ordering can be obtained by
arranging the columns according to the direction of the circuit. In the
proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit}, we provided a construction of a
Metzler circuit that always contains a pure reaction.

The existence of a Metzler circuit with a pure reaction has already been
shown to exist for any RAF that is itself not a CAF
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}. However, in the proof of Thm.~3.1 of
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026}, the construction follows the F-catalysts as
reactants for each reaction, and thereby not necessarily the reactant with
the lowest generation number. Non-trivial minimal RAFs exist, where this
construction would not yield a \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
Metzler circuit for each strong block $B$. Such an example is depicted in
Fig.~\ref{ex:CutVertex2}. Indeed, the block $(\{x_1,x_2\}, \{r_1,r_2\})$
does not contain an elementary circuit, where each species vertex is the
F-catalyst of its appurtenant reaction. Nevertheless, $C\coloneqq
(x_1,r_1,x_2,r_2,x_1)$ is still a Metzler circuit with a pure reaction
($r_1$), and thereby \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic}
(Lemma.~\ref{lem:MetzCircAut}). In addition, $(x_3,r_2,x_4,r_3, x_3)$
satisfies the construction of Thm.~3.1 of \cite{golnik_bridging_2026}.

The example depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:AutCircNotCore} clearly illustrates,
however, that these \emph{stoichiometrically autocatalytic} Metzler
circuits are not necessarily autocatalytic cores. On the one hand,
non-trivial stoichiometries can give rise to strictly smaller cores that
can be contained inside the constructed circuits. An example of this case
is shown in Sec.~\ref{subsec:OneRAFManyCores}. In turn, \PFS{the same} core
can be contained in \PFS{more than one} irreducible RAFs (see
Sec.~\ref{subsec:ManyRAFsOneCore}) or the construction in
Lemma~\ref{lem:MetzCircuit} can yield two equivalent autocatalytic cores
(see Sec.~\ref{subsec:IrrEquivCores}). We might also find autocatalytic
reactions in trivial and non-trivial irreducible RAFs (see Eq.~4.18,
\cite{golnik_bridging_2026} and Fig.~\ref{Fig:ExplAutocatReac}).

%\section{Concluding Remarks}

\PFS{The results obtained here show that there are meaningful connections
  between the the CRS and CRN formalisms that also imply strong
  similarities between notions of autocatalysis, in particular between
  non-trivial irreducible RAFs and autocatalytic cores. While our results
  yield useful implications, they are still far from establishing 1-1
  correspondences between concepts in the two alternative formal
  frameworks. While the present results in essence characterize non-trivial
  minimal RAFs in terms of CRNs, the consverse, i.e., a characterization of
  autocatalytic cores in the language of CRS remains an open reseach
  project. Given the differences between the two formalisms, it is not
  clear 1-1 correspondences exist, in particular since \emph{stoichiometric
    autocatalysis} depends on the numerical values of the stoichiometric
  coefficient.}
